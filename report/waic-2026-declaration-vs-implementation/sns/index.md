## LinkedIn

WAIC 2026 개막 연설에서 시진핑 주석이 밝힌 숫자 하나가 눈에 걸렸다. 기상 조기경보 AI '마주(妈祖, Mazu)'를 30개국에 적용하겠다는 목표, 그런데 1차 사료로 확인되는 현재 실가동국은 7개국이었다.

목표와 달성치를 구분하는 순간 이 연설은 다르게 읽힌다. 세계AI협력기구(WAICO)는 29개국이 서명해 실체는 확정됐지만, 사무국·의결 규칙·예산 같은 작동 장치는 어느 1차 사료에도 공개되지 않았다. 설계도만 있는 건물에 가깝다. 개도국 AI 연수도 원문은 "5,000명"이 아니라 "5,000개 명액(名额)"이다. 한 사람이 여러 프로그램에 중복 참여할 수 있는 연인원 개념이고, 5년 누적 목표다.

이 간극을 이념으로 재단하지 않고 검증하려고, 이 보고서는 다섯 가지 중립 잣대를 세웠다. 집행 주체가 있는가, 대상이 명시됐는가, 정량 목표가 붙었는가, 서명으로 제도화됐는가, 진행을 외부에서 추적할 수 있는가. 이 잣대를 네 이니셔티브에 대 보면 공통적으로 비는 항목이 하나 있다. 추적성이다. 정량 목표까지는 붙어 있어도, 그 진행을 검증할 장치는 아직 없다.

이 글의 결론은 우열 판정이 아니다. 한국의 AI 국제기여도 같은 다섯 가지 체크를 이만큼 갖췄는가, 라는 질문이다. 가깝고 강력한 이웃의 AI 구상을 외면하지 않고 원문 그대로 냉정히 읽는 것이, 그 질문의 출발점이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

A target and an achievement are not the same number. Xi Jinping's WAIC 2026 keynote pledged the weather-warning AI "Mazu (妈祖)" to 30 countries; primary sources confirm it is currently operational in 7.

Separate the target from the current figure and the speech reads differently. The World AI Cooperation Organization, signed by 29 countries, is real and settled, but no secretariat, decision rules, or budget appear in any primary source. It resembles a blueprint without a building. The developing-country training pledge tells a similar story: the original text says "5,000 slots," not "5,000 people," a cumulative five-year target where one person can fill multiple slots.

To test this gap without ideology, the report applies five neutral yardsticks: a named executing actor, a named audience, a quantified goal, institutional signature, and traceability. Run all four initiatives through these checks and one gap repeats across the board — traceability. Numbers get attached; the machinery to verify their progress does not yet exist.

The report's conclusion is not a verdict on who is ahead. It is a question turned inward: does Korea's own international AI contribution clear these same five checks? Reading a close, powerful neighbor's plan coldly, rather than dismissing it, is where that question starts.

## Facebook (EN)

"Target: 30 countries. Operational: 7."

I sat with those two numbers for a few days before writing anything.

They came from Xi Jinping's opening keynote at WAIC 2026, a pledge to deploy the weather-warning AI "Mazu" across 30 countries, while primary sources confirm only 7 are actually running it today.

I wasn't sure how to read a speech like this, so I set the ideology question aside. Not "is China ahead," not "is this just political theater." Something plainer instead. Does this promise have a name attached? A number? Can anyone outside verify how it's going?

Running the speech through those three questions, one by one, turned up a pattern. The World AI Cooperation Organization, signed by 29 countries, is real, but no secretariat, no decision rules, no budget shows up in any primary source. The training pledge for developing countries turned out to read "5,000 slots," not "5,000 people." A blueprint, mostly, without the building yet.

But the question that actually stayed with me wasn't about them.

Does our own international AI contribution carry even that much: a name, a number, someone who could check? Before deciding whether a close, powerful neighbor's plan is something to like or dismiss, how closely have we actually read it?

I don't have an answer yet. Only the sense that reading the original, coldly, is where it has to start.

A number attached to a promise is not the same as a number delivered. That is the test this report runs against Xi Jinping's July 2026 keynote at Shanghai's WAIC — not a verdict on ideology, but a primary-source check of whether the speech's headline initiatives come with a named actor, a named audience, a quantified goal, institutional signature, and traceability.

The gap shows up fast. The weather-warning AI "Mazu" was pledged to 30 countries; primary sources confirm 7 are operational. The World AI Cooperation Organization has 29 signatures and a Shanghai address, but no disclosed secretariat or decision rules. Developing-country training turns out to mean 5,000 slots, not 5,000 distinct people, spread across five years.

None of the five yardsticks in this report are built to rank countries. They are built to be turned around and asked of any government — including Korea's. That self-check, more than any comparison, is the report's actual point.
