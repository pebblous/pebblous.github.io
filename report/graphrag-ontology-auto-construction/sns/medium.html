When the Graph Is Wrong, RAG Is Wrong

GraphRAG promises better AI reasoning through knowledge graphs. But auto-constructed ontologies carry five quality problems that most teams never check for.

The Promise

Since Microsoft published its GraphRAG paper in April 2024, knowledge graph-based retrieval-augmented generation has become one of the hottest paradigms in AI infrastructure. The appeal is clear: traditional vector RAG struggles with multi-hop reasoning, loses global context, and cannot infer relationships between entities. GraphRAG solves these by extracting entities and relationships into a graph, detecting communities via the Leiden algorithm, and generating hierarchical summaries.

The numbers are impressive. Multi-hop QA F1 improved by 27.23% over dense RAG. The Microsoft GraphRAG repository has accumulated 31,600 GitHub stars. The knowledge graph market is projected to grow from $1.07B to $6.94B by 2030 at a 36.6% CAGR.

The Problem Nobody Talks About

But there's a question that keeps getting skipped in the excitement: is the auto-constructed graph actually correct?

When LLMs extract entities and relationships from text to build knowledge graphs automatically, five distinct quality problems emerge:

  • Hallucinated Edges — LLMs generate relationships that don't exist in the source text. Even the best models show a 1.5-1.9% hallucination rate, and in knowledge graphs, a single false edge can propagate errors across multiple reasoning chains.
  • Entity Duplication — The same entity appears under different names ("Samsung Electronics" vs "Samsung" vs "SAMSUNG"). Without deduplication, the graph fragments into disconnected islands.
  • Incomplete Extraction — Entities and relationships present in the source text are simply missed. LightRAG's Diversity score of 59-77% versus GraphRAG's 23-41% suggests that community-based approaches may systematically miss fine-grained entities.
  • Schema Drift — As new documents are processed, the ontology's structure gradually loses consistency. Categories shift, relationship types multiply, and the graph becomes increasingly incoherent.
  • Domain Mismatch — General-purpose LLMs lack the domain knowledge to correctly identify specialized entities and relationships in fields like manufacturing, finance, or healthcare.

The Meta-Analysis Wake-Up Call

Perhaps the most sobering finding comes from a May 2025 meta-analysis that examined evaluation bias in GraphRAG benchmarks. After correcting for unrelated question generation, LLM evaluation bias, and unfair baseline comparisons, LightRAG's reported 72% win rate dropped to 48% — a 24-percentage-point correction that calls into question whether auto-constructed graphs genuinely outperform simple vector retrieval.

"The implicit promise is that a better graph means better answers. But an incorrect graph doesn't just fail to help — it actively misleads."

Six Frameworks, Zero Quality Guarantees

We compared six major frameworks: Microsoft GraphRAG, LightRAG, nano-graphrag, LlamaIndex PropertyGraphIndex, Neo4j + LLM, and Palantir AIP. Each excels in different dimensions — LightRAG achieves 94-98% cost reduction, Neo4j provides enterprise-grade ACID transactions, ArchRAG delivers 250x token savings.

But none of them includes a quality verification layer for the auto-constructed ontology. They compete on building graphs faster and cheaper, while the question "is this graph correct?" remains unanswered.

The Missing Layer

Graph database vendors focus on storage and querying. GraphRAG frameworks focus on construction and retrieval optimization. Between them lies an empty space: quality diagnostics for auto-constructed knowledge graphs.

This gap matters more as AI agents begin using knowledge graphs for autonomous reasoning. An agent that reasons over a flawed graph doesn't just return wrong answers — it makes wrong decisions with high confidence. The GraphEval framework shows that KG-based hallucination detection improves accuracy by +16% and F1 by +20% over self-checking methods, proving that graph quality verification is both feasible and impactful.

What Needs to Change

Every auto-constructed knowledge graph should be treated like any other data pipeline output: validated before use. The quality dimensions are well-established — Completeness (are all expected entities present?), Consistency (does the schema hold together?), Freshness (is the graph current?) — but the tooling to apply them to graph data at scale barely exists.

Incremental approaches like DIAL-KG (F1 +4.7% over static baselines) and streaming architectures like IncRML (315x storage reduction) show that quality-aware graph construction is technically possible. The question is whether the industry will prioritize it before auto-constructed graphs become load-bearing infrastructure.
