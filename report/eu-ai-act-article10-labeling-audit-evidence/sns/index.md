## LinkedIn (KO)

EU AI법이 데이터 준비 작업을 여섯 개의 동사로 처음 호명했다. 주석, 라벨링, 정제, 갱신, 보강, 집계.

10조는 이 여섯 작업을 이름으로 부르고, 학습 데이터가 "충분히 대표성 있으며 가능한 한 오류 없을" 것을 요구하며, 편향을 조사하고 데이터 공백을 식별한 기록을 남기라고 못 박는다. 규제가 어떤 작업을 이름으로 부르는 순간, 그 작업이 남긴 산출물은 감사관이 제출을 요구할 수 있는 증거가 된다.

문제는 이 증거의 성격이다. 라벨 오류율, 어노테이터 일치도, 편향 조사 기록 가운데 어느 것도 모델을 학습시킨 뒤에 소급해 만들 수 없다. Digital Omnibus로 적용은 2027년 12월로 미뤄졌지만, 유예된 것은 날짜지 요건이 아니다.

대표 벤치마크조차 평균 3.3%의 라벨 오류를 안고 있다는 측정값은, "오류 없음"이 완벽의 선언이 아니라 측정의 문제임을 먼저 일러 준다.

감사 증거는 소급 생성할 수 없기에, 유예 기간을 파이프라인 계측의 창으로 쓴 조직에게 규제는 비용이 아니라 진입 장벽이 된다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

For the first time, the EU AI Act names data preparation in the language of law: annotation, labelling, cleaning, updating, enrichment, aggregation.

Article 10 requires training data to be "sufficiently representative and, to the best extent possible, free of errors," and it obliges providers to examine for bias and document data gaps. Once a regulation names a task, the artifacts that task produces become evidence an auditor can demand.

The character of that evidence is what matters. Label error rates, inter-annotator agreement, and bias records cannot be reconstructed after a model is trained. The Digital Omnibus pushed application of Article 10 to 2 December 2027, but what moved was the date, not the requirement.

Even carefully curated benchmarks carry an average 3.3% label error, a reminder that "free of errors" is a question of measurement, not perfection.

Because audit evidence cannot be generated retroactively, the deferral is a window to instrument the pipeline, and for the organizations that use it, compliance becomes a barrier to entry rather than a cost.

## Facebook (EN)

A box drawn around a figure, "pedestrian" chosen from a dropdown, and on to the next frame.

An annotator repeats this click thousands of times a day, and for a long time no one thought to call it evidence. Whether the label was right, whether the guideline held together, was left to a team's diligence.

EU AI Act Article 10 brought that workbench into the text of the law. It named data preparation one verb at a time: annotation, labelling, cleaning, updating, enrichment, aggregation. Once a regulation names a task, whatever that task leaves behind becomes something an auditor can ask to see.

So what does a labeling team end up submitting? The article asks that data be, "to the best extent possible, free of errors." Yet even benchmarks reviewed in many passes carried an average 3.3% label error.

"Does 'free of errors' really mean zero?"

Measure it, and the answer shifts. What you can submit is not perfection but an error rate, and a record of how closely annotators agreed. Bias is the same. The same sentence can be labeled differently depending on who reads it, as several studies have shown. Mitigating bias turned out to be a matter of a paper trail, not of intent.

What Pebblous has long called "diagnosing data" meets the language of the regulation here. Measuring representativeness, gaps, and bias translates, one requirement at a time, into the clauses of Article 10.

The Digital Omnibus moved application to December 2027. But audit evidence cannot be built after a model is trained. So is this deferred time a rest, or a window to instrument the pipeline? By the time the deadline arrives, that difference may already be past changing.
