## LinkedIn (EN)

The first comprehensive AI law in the United States was rewritten before it ever took effect.

Colorado SB 24-205 passed in 2023, borrowing its architecture directly from the EU AI Act — mandatory impact assessments, algorithmic audits, risk management programs. The most ambitious state-level AI regulation ever attempted in the US.

Two years later, Colorado rewrote it. SB 26-189, signed into law on May 14, 2026, strips out the entire pre-deployment compliance layer and replaces it with post-deployment transparency obligations: 30-day public disclosure windows, consumer rights to explanation, correction, and human review.

The shift matters. EU AI Act builds a wall before the gate. Colorado's new model opens the gate, but makes every passage traceable.

The law covers Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT) across seven domains: employment, finance, healthcare, education, housing, insurance, and lending. Effective January 1, 2027. That's seven months to get compliant.

Here's why this matters for AI teams: when consumers gain the right to explanation, companies must trace back what data trained the model. Longpre et al. (2023) found that 70% of industry-standard training datasets were collected without documented licensing. Data provenance audits are no longer optional. They're the evidentiary layer that makes explanation rights actionable. That's exactly what DataClinic addresses.

## Facebook (EN)

"This decision was made by AI. You have the right to an explanation."

That sentence is now in a state statute. Colorado SB 26-189. Effective January 1, 2027.

What stayed with me after reading this law wasn't the new obligations themselves. It was the direction they came from.

In 2023, Colorado passed SB 24-205, the first attempt in the United States to transplant the EU AI Act's architecture into state law. Mandatory impact assessments, risk management programs, algorithmic audits. A wall built before the gate. Many saw it as the opening move of American AI regulation.

Three years later, Colorado tore it down and started over.

The pre-deployment compliance layer was removed entirely. In its place: post-deployment transparency. The question shifted from "prove this is safe before you release it" to "can you explain it after the fact, and can a human review the decision?" Seven domains — employment, finance, healthcare, education, housing, insurance, lending — fall under the new framework.

Whether the EU model or the Colorado model is better governance is an open question.

But what this shift actually reaches is clearer. When the right to explanation becomes a legal entitlement, companies must trace backward through their models — to the data those models were trained on. Longpre et al. (2023) found that 70% of industry-standard training datasets were collected without documented licensing information. Without data provenance, explanation rights don't have a foundation to stand on.

"Can you explain this decision?" requires first asking: "Can you account for what built it?"
