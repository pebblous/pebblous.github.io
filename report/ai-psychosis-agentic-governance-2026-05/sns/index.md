2026년 Agentic AI 도입에서 가장 큰 위험은 모델 성능이 아닙니다. AI가 틀려도 조직 안에서 아무도 그 사실을 말하지 못하는 구조입니다.

숫자가 그 격차를 정확히 측정합니다. 90%의 임원이 "직원이 비윤리적 AI 행동을 안전하게 보고할 수 있다"고 답합니다. 그런데 같은 시기 73%의 직원이 실제 AI 불일치를 보고한 결과, 그중 50%는 "가끔 또는 거의 안" 조치됐습니다(Radical Candor·HR Dive, 2025-2026). 인식은 90, 실재는 0에 가깝습니다.

페블러스는 이 문제를 데이터 품질 거버넌스의 문제로 읽습니다. AI 판단의 품질은 데이터 품질의 함수이고, AI가 데이터에 대해 틀린 판단을 내릴 때 조직이 그것을 감지하는 능력은 데이터 진단 역량의 조직적 형태입니다. DataClinic이 진단하는 것은 데이터의 커버리지 갭만이 아니라, 그 갭을 조직이 말할 수 있는가입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The biggest risk in Agentic AI adoption isn't model performance. It's the organizational structure that prevents anyone from saying when the AI gets it wrong.

The numbers measure that gap precisely. 90% of executives report that "employees can safely report unethical AI behavior." Yet 73% of employees who actually reported AI misalignment found 50% of those reports ignored or addressed only "sometimes / rarely" (Radical Candor & HR Dive, 2025-2026). Perception sits at 90; reality approaches zero.

The silence is structural, not a failure of individual courage. Three decades of automation bias research show this. The CDSS meta-analysis (Goddard et al., JAMIA 2012) demonstrated a +26% increase in wrong-decision risk when the system was wrong. Anthropic's sycophancy study (Sharma et al., ICLR 2024) showed five RLHF-aligned models systematically preferring responses that match user beliefs. When the CEO's hypothesis gets confirmed by the AI, the cost of staff disagreement explodes.

Pebblous reads this as a data quality governance problem. The quality of AI judgment is a function of data quality. The organization's capacity to detect when AI is wrong about the data is the institutional form of data diagnostic capability. What DataClinic diagnoses is not only coverage gaps in the data, but whether the organization can talk about them.

## Facebook (EN)

I was reviewing an AI governance report last week and got stuck in the gap between two numbers. 90 and 50.

The 90 was executive perception. 90% of executives reported that "employees can safely report unethical AI behavior" in their organization. The 50 was reality. In the same period, 73% of employees actually reported AI misalignment, and roughly half of those reports were addressed only "sometimes" or "rarely." Perception at 90, reality close to zero.

While I looked at that gap, the word silence kept following me. We usually frame silence as an individual choice. Who will speak up. Who will look away. Yet thirty years of automation bias research, cited in the report, point elsewhere. A clinical meta-analysis showing that when a clinical decision support system is wrong, physician error risk rises by 26%. The Anthropic ICLR 2024 study showing that the same RLHF alignment that made models better at reasoning also made them prefer responses that match user beliefs. When a CEO states a hypothesis and the AI confirms it, where does the cost of staff disagreement come from?

Silence is not made by a single person. It is made by automation bias, authority bias, and the diffusion of responsibility — with AI sycophancy adding one more layer. Pebblous reads this as a data quality governance problem. The quality of AI judgment is a function of data quality. The organization's ability to detect when AI is wrong about the data is, in the end, the institutional form of data diagnostic capability.

The solution is probably not a smarter model. It is closer to building structures that let organizations detect, voice, and correct AI errors. The technical foundation of that structure is data quality governance — which is where this report arrives after 32 academic papers.

What is missing between 90 and 50 seems to be the thing worth seeing first.
