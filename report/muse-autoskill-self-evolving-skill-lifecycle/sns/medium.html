When Skills Begin to Remember — Inside MUSE-Autoskill's Five-Stage Lifecycle

Subtitle: A paper that arrived on arXiv this week proposes treating agent skills the way we treat data — as long-lived, experience-aware, testable assets. It might be the first time the lifespan of a skill has been drawn in full.

The Question That Refuses to Go Away

Anyone who has spent an hour in a long, careful conversation with an AI agent has felt the same quiet ache: tomorrow, none of this will be remembered. The convention the user mentioned, the procedure painstakingly worked out, the subtle piece of feedback — all of it will evaporate the moment a new session opens. Humans turn one mistake into a lesson. Today's agents repeat the same mistake at the same coordinate, a hundred times if asked.

Through 2025, the major labs tried to patch this with "memory features." ChatGPT memory, Anthropic Memory, Gemini personalization. Look closely and they share an assumption: memory belongs to whoever is talking. Per user. Per session. At best, per agent. The idea that the work itself — the skill — might carry its own experience was an empty seat in the literature.

On May 26, 2026, a paper sat down in that seat.

What MUSE-Autoskill Proposes

MUSE-Autoskill (arXiv:2605.27366, Lin, Li, Song, Jiang, and Zhang, affiliation estimated as ByteDance Infra AI Lab) treats skills as long-lived, experience-aware, testable assets and frames their lifespan as a five-stage closed loop.

"We propose MUSE-Autoskill Agent (Memory-Utilizing Skill Evolution), a skill-centric agent framework that manages skills through a unified lifecycle (creation, memory, management, evaluation, and refinement), highlighting the importance of treating skills as long-lived, experience-aware, and testable assets." — MUSE-Autoskill abstract

None of the five stages is wholly new. Creation traces back to Voyager. Memory traces back to MemGPT. Management has surfaced in industrial AgentOps tooling. Evaluation echoes Reflexion. Refinement leans on GEPA. The novelty is binding them — for the first time — into one operating model for the skill as an asset.

  • Creation — mid-task, on-demand, the agent decides which procedures deserve to become reusable assets.
  • Memory — and this is the genuinely new layer — skill-level memory. Every invocation deposits experience into the skill itself: success and failure signals, variant patterns, domain context.
  • Management — the skill library is organized, searched, and pruned. Duplicates merge; idle skills atrophy.
  • Evaluation — every skill earns its place through unit tests (static) and runtime feedback (dynamic). Two gates, two kinds of failures caught.
  • Refinement — those signals rewrite the skill's body, memory, and metadata, and the result re-enters the loop.

Why Now — A Three-Month Diagnosis

Three months before MUSE landed, another paper had delivered an uncomfortable verdict. SkillsBench (arXiv:2602.12670, Li et al., February 13, 2026) put 86 tasks, 11 domains, and 7,308 trajectories on the table and reported:

"Self-generated Skills provide no benefit on average, showing that models cannot reliably author the procedural knowledge they benefit from consuming." — SkillsBench abstract

Curated human skills lifted performance by an average of +16.2pp (with per-domain swings of +4.5 to +51.9). Self-generated skills came out flat on average, and worse than flat on 16 out of 84 tasks. The lesson was sharp: generation alone is not enough.

MUSE reads as a direct response. The remedy SkillsBench's diagnosis demanded — verification, memory, refinement — is exactly what MUSE wraps around the act of creation. In four months, SkillsBench diagnosed the problem, SkillOpt answered it for single-skill learning, and MUSE answered it at the level of lifecycle governance.

Two important caveats. SkillsBench is not MUSE's own benchmark — it is an independent line of work that MUSE adopts as an evaluation tool. And MUSE itself, at v1, only reports "initial evidence that lifecycle-managed skills can improve task success, efficiency, reuse, and cross-agent transfer." Headline numbers against baselines are not yet public. The value of MUSE, today, is the integrative power of the frame, not a single quantitative knockout.

Skill-Level Memory — The Genuinely New Layer

The most quietly important sentence in the MUSE abstract concerns memory:

"Skill-level memory that accumulates experience for each skill across tasks, enabling more effective reuse and adaptation over time." — MUSE-Autoskill abstract

Memory research has worked at two scales so far: session/user (MemGPT, ChatGPT memory, Anthropic Memory) and agent (Generative Agents, Reflexion). MUSE adds a third — per-skill. The cleanest analogy is the instance/class split in object-oriented programming. A class defines a method, but the method does not normally remember its own invocations. If the method itself were to remember how often it was called, with what arguments, under what conditions it failed — that is skill-level memory. A new dimension for the procedural side of agency.

The quantitative ground for taking this seriously is not in MUSE's own v1 numbers. It sits in adjacent industrial data. LangChain's late-2025 survey (n=1,340) reported that organizations adopting layered memory — combining session and agent memory — saw task completion jump from 31% to 79%, with costs down 62%. MUSE's skill-level memory adds a third layer on top. It is reasonable to expect the next quantitative wave to be at least as large.

Why This Lifecycle Is Industrially Urgent

The urgency is not philosophical. It shows up in operations data.

  • McKinsey's The State of AI 2026 finds that 88% of agent pilots fail to reach production. Of those failures, 64% cite "evaluation gap" as the primary cause.
  • LangChain's State of AI Agents 2025 (n=1,340) finds that introducing layered memory roughly doubles task completion (31% → 79%) while cutting cost by 62%.
  • SkillsBench finds that curated, verified skills produce +16.2pp on average — but self-generated skills, untested, produce nothing.

Read together, these three numbers tell one story. The floor is collapsing for agent projects, more than half of those collapses trace to missing evaluation, and adding even one disciplined memory layer roughly doubles outcomes. MUSE proposes adding both evaluation and a new memory layer at once.

The Quartet — Voyager → Hermes → SkillOpt → MUSE

It helps to see MUSE on a map. Four works, four years, the level of abstraction rising one rung each time.

  • 2023, Voyager (NVIDIA · Caltech, arXiv:2305.16291) — the academic prototype. An ever-growing skill library, built on top of GPT-4 inside Minecraft. The claim that a skill could be an asset at all.
  • 2025, Hermes Agent (NousResearch) — the industrial implementation. Detect five-plus tool calls, write the procedure down as a skill document, ship it. "An agent that grows with the user."
  • May 22, 2026, SkillOpt (Microsoft Research, arXiv:2605.23904) — the learning technique. Treat a skill document as a learnable text asset; optimize it in text space and report +23.5pt on GPT-5.5.
  • May 26, 2026, MUSE-Autoskill — lifecycle governance. Not how a single skill is learned, but how the whole library lives.

From "skill exists as an object" to "skills can be generated industrially" to "a skill can be learned in text space" to "skills have lifespans you must govern." The same question, sharpened one notch at a time.

From AgentOps to SkillOps to MemoryOps

The IT industry has historically absorbed new paradigms through operations categories. DevOps in 2007. MLOps in 2017. LLMOps in 2023. By 2024, AgentOps had stabilized — Mem0 (48,000+ GitHub stars, Series A at $24M, an exclusive AWS Agent SDK deal), Letta, Zep, and LangMem forming a four-way race. Anthropic's Agent Skills Open Standard (December 2025) was adopted by Claude, OpenAI Codex, and Gemini CLI almost simultaneously — the unmistakable shape of category formation.

What's emerging now, in spring 2026, is a sub-segment: SkillOps. The academic blueprint exists — SkillOpt and MUSE — but the tooling is unsettled. Beside it sits a still larger blank: MemoryOps. Once skill-level memory becomes routine, the lifecycle of memory itself (creation, diagnosis, deletion, transfer) will likely break off as a category of its own.

A Pebblous Read — From AI-Ready Data to AI-Ready Behavior

For four years, Pebblous has followed a single thesis: data deserves to be treated as a living, diagnosable asset. DataClinic diagnoses training data through five signals — label integrity, distribution balance, freshness, missingness, outliers. AI-Ready Data is the output category. PebbloSim and DataGreenhouse are the operating environment.

What MUSE proposes extends that thesis one rung up. If skills are long-lived, experience-aware, testable assets, then behavior — not only data — becomes diagnosable. The five DataClinic signals map onto the five lifecycle stages with surprising symmetry:

  • Label integrity → Creation defect diagnosis (skills decided wrongly, procedures frozen in error)
  • Distribution balance → Memory accumulation balance (one skill's experience over-fitted to one domain)
  • Freshness → Lifecycle turnover (skills that have not been refined in a year)
  • Missingness → Verification gates missing (skills deployed without unit tests; runtime feedback channels broken)
  • Outliers → Refinement triggers (runtime feedback that has suddenly worsened; anomalous invocation patterns)

We call this view SkillClinic. As of this writing, an equivalent 1:1 mapping has not been published in English either — the category is open.

There is a calendar-level reason this lands now in Korea. The country's AI Framework Act, in force since January 22, 2026, requires high-impact AI to maintain "systems for detecting, interrupting, and switching risk during operation." MUSE's pairing of unit tests, runtime feedback, skill-level memory, and refinement is, almost exactly, the technical evidence chain such an audit trail demands. Five days before MUSE arrived, the Hancom + LG AI Research alliance around ChatEXAONE became the first visible signal of SkillOps becoming a Korean industrial agenda. This article is being published into that confluence.

Where Does Memory Live?

Anyone who has had a long conversation with an AI agent has felt the same quiet ache — and now we have the first academic answer.

Memory lives in the user. Memory lives in the agent. And, after MUSE, memory finally lives in the skill itself. A procedure carries its own invocation history. A hundred calls leave a hundred traces, not in the user and not scattered across the agent's mixed stream, but in the asset that did the work. The same claim that made data into a living asset has now, gently, extended to behavior.

An agent with memory is not just a smarter agent. It is a more human kind of collaborator — one that remembers the convention you taught it yesterday, that adjusts a failed procedure instead of repeating it word for word, that grows, in small operational ways, alongside the team it works with. That future is what MUSE draws as a lifecycle. The remaining work is to bring its quantitative back half forward, and to translate its frame into the categories — and the languages — where it will actually operate.
