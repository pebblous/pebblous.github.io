## EN — LinkedIn/Twitter

The AI industry just got a letter from 1.42 billion people.

Pope Leo XIV chose his name deliberately. Just as Leo XIII responded to industrial-age exploitation with Rerum Novarum in 1891, Leo XIV answered AI-era power concentration with Magnifica Humanitas in 2026 — 245 paragraphs, five chapters, one argument: "Technology is never neutral."

That single line dismantles the industry's default defense. "AI is just a tool; users are responsible" has been the moat around design choices, training data, and algorithmic bias for two decades. The encyclical names it a liability, not a shield.

Three moves reframe the governance debate. First, solidarity — cited by only 7% of the world's 84 AI ethics guidelines (Jobin et al., 2019) — is elevated to a core principle alongside transparency and fairness. Second, algorithms, data, and digital platforms are brought under the "universal destination of goods," recasting them as quasi-public assets. Third, Anthropic co-founder Christopher Olah stood on a Vatican stage and declared that "AI labs cannot steer AI alone" — a statement that works against his own firm's short-term interests.

The gap between principle and execution is wide. Only 6% of companies have hired AI ethics specialists; fewer than 25% have board-approved AI policies (McKinsey 2024/2025). Moral authority and legal obligation — EU AI Act full enforcement in August 2026, Korea's AI Basic Act already active — are pushing in the same direction. The bottleneck is not intent. It is infrastructure.

## EN — Facebook

There is a word the Catholic Church has used for 135 years to respond to each era's disruption.

Nova rerum. The new thing.

In 1891, the new thing was industrial capitalism — and Leo XIII wrote Rerum Novarum.

In 2026, the new thing is artificial intelligence — and Leo XIV wrote Magnifica Humanitas.

What struck me most was not the theology.

It was the precision of one sentence in paragraph six.

"Technology is never neutral — it reflects the characteristics of those who design, fund, regulate, and use it."

That sentence does not ask for better guidelines.

It dismantles the assumption that has insulated AI companies from accountability for two decades.

The document goes further.

Only 7% of the world's 84 AI ethics frameworks had ever explicitly named solidarity as a principle.

The rest stayed close to autonomy — individual rights, privacy, consent.

The encyclical names what was missing:

"That no one is left behind."

Not as aspiration. As obligation.

The moment that felt most unexpected was watching Anthropic co-founder Christopher Olah speak from a Vatican stage.

He did not come to bless the technology.

He said, clearly, that "AI labs cannot steer AI alone."

That is a strange thing for someone inside the industry to say publicly.

Constitutional AI and Imago Dei are different frameworks.

But they are asking the same question: what does it take for a system not to harm the people it touches?

Pebblous looks at this question from the data layer.

The encyclical's demand for "ethics embedded at the design stage" starts before the model.

It starts in the training data — in what is measured, what is missing, what is weighted.

DataClinic exists to make that layer visible.

"AI-Ready Data" is, in the end, data that has been prepared to be fair.

The gap between principle and practice remains open.

But the direction is clear.
