Six Robot Datasets, One Format, and a Shared Blind Spot

Put six of the most-cited public robot-learning datasets side by side and something strange shows up in the last column. Collection method, scale, and license all diverge. Distribution format does not.

DROID, Open X-Embodiment, GR00T, RoboCasa, MimicGen, and LIBERO were built by different teams, for different reasons, using different pipelines. And yet at the point of release, nearly all of them are repackaged into the same thing: HuggingFace's LeRobot format.

Two Ways to Make a Robot Dataset

Public robot data splits into two lineages. One is real-robot teleoperation, where a person physically operates a robot arm and the session gets logged. The other is synthetic generation, where a physics engine either inflates a handful of human demonstrations or manufactures trajectories at scale inside a simulator.

DROID sits in the first camp. Thirteen universities, including Stanford, UC Berkeley, and UT Austin, standardized on identical hardware, a Franka Panda 7-DoF arm with a Robotiq 2F-85 gripper and Oculus Quest 2 teleoperation, and spent twelve months collecting 76,000 trajectories across 564 real-world scenes. Standardizing the hardware is what let thirteen independent labs produce directly comparable data from day one.

Open X-Embodiment took the opposite route. Led by Google DeepMind, it pooled 60 existing datasets from 34 labs, spanning 22 different robot embodiments, into a single RLDS-formatted collection of over a million trajectories. The payoff showed up in model performance: RT-1-X, trained on the pooled data, scored 50% higher on in-domain tasks than models trained on any single source, and the larger RT-2-X showed 3x more emergent skills.

Simulation-based generation takes a third route entirely. MimicGen, built by NVIDIA's Seattle Robotics Lab, starts from roughly 200 human demonstrations and adapts them across scenes, objects, and arms to produce more than 50,000 trajectories, a roughly 250x amplification. RoboCasa layers that engine onto 120 kitchen scenes and about 2,509 unique 3D objects, expanding human demonstrations into 100,000 trajectories. NVIDIA's PhysicalAI-Robotics-GR00T-X-Embodiment-Sim dataset pushes the same idea to industrial scale: roughly 345,000 trajectories, 1.91TB, generated inside Isaac Sim to train the GR00T humanoid foundation model.

LIBERO is the odd one out. It doesn't generate data at all. It's a benchmark, 130 tasks split across Spatial, Object, Goal, and Long-horizon suites, designed to test whether a robot can learn a new task without forgetting an old one.

The License Minefield

Scale differences are easy to spot. License differences are where teams get burned. A few traps worth knowing before shipping a commercial product:

  • GR00T's two sibling datasets carry opposite licenses. X-Embodiment-Sim is CC-BY 4.0 and commercially usable. Teleop-Sim, despite the nearly identical name, is CC-BY-NC 4.0, non-commercial only.
  • RoboCasa and RoboCasa365 are different papers. The original RoboCasa (2024, arXiv:2406.02523) covers 120 kitchen scenes and 100 tasks. RoboCasa365 (2026, arXiv:2603.04356, ICLR 2026) is a separate, larger paper spanning 365 tasks across 60 kitchen activities. Citing the wrong one mixes up scale claims.
  • DROID's license depends on which mirror you pull. The official release is CC-BY 4.0; some community mirrors redistribute under Apache-2.0. Citing the official CC-BY 4.0 source is the safer bet.
  • MimicGen carries NVIDIA's Source Code License, which restricts commercial use, and both RoboCasa and LIBERO route through it for their amplification step.

What All Six Leave Out

Line up what these datasets actually record, and a pattern falls into two halves. RGB video, joint and gripper state, and action sequences: all six keep these in full. Contact force at the gripper, the seed and lighting a synthetic trajectory was generated under, a hash to verify physical consistency, and a log of demonstrations that failed and were discarded: none of the six keep any of it.

That second half is provenance, the evidence for how a data point was made and whether it can be trusted. It matters more, not less, for synthetic data. A physics engine that generates 50,000 trajectories from 200 demonstrations could, in principle, record exactly which seed and lighting condition produced each one. None of the datasets examined here do.

The consequence isn't abstract. Training data that discards physical-consistency evidence means a model can absorb physically invalid trajectories as if they were correct. Without a failure log, there's no way to diagnose after the fact what went wrong or why a given trajectory was thrown out during generation.

The Next Axis Isn't Scale

The scale race for robot data has already been fought and largely decided. NVIDIA, DeepMind, and university consortia hold that ground. GR00T's 345,000 trajectories and MimicGen's 250x amplification aren't going to be leapfrogged by a small team.

What's left open is the axis these six datasets never touched: can the data prove how it was made? That's the coordinate Pebblous is building toward, not by inventing a new format, but by attaching provenance, contact-force ledgers, generation lineage, physical-consistency hashes, to data that already speaks the same LeRobot format everyone else uses. Compatible with the standard pipeline. Different in what rides along with it.
