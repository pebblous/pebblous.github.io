## LinkedIn (EN)

China now reports the number of AI tokens it processes each day, 140 trillion, as if it were a national economic statistic. That is more than a thousandfold jump in two years.

The National Data Administration has formally named the token a "settlement unit linking technical supply and commercial demand," and capital markets and local governments have started moving to that number. Once a unit of measure is chosen, policy and investment follow it.

But roughly 86% of that 140 trillion is traffic from a single company, ByteDance, and a large share of it is bots talking to bots. Over the same period the price per token fell by nearly a thousandfold. The volume ballooned; the value did not grow in step.

This is Goodhart's law at national scale: when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. The same trap sits under any corporate dashboard that reads "tokens consumed equals adoption."

Using more is not the same as using well. Before you count, define what is worth counting.

## Facebook (EN)

Picture a country counting the number of AI tokens it burned through in a single day, and then announcing that figure to the public.

This March, China's National Data Administration did exactly that. 140 trillion tokens a day. More than a thousand times what it was two years ago.

Read as a number alone, it sounds like a story of an intelligence economy growing at that pace.

But once you learn that most of those 140 trillion tokens are traffic from a single company, and that a fair share of them are bots talking to bots, a quieter question surfaces.

"Is using more the same as using well?"

Over the same stretch, the price of a single token fell by nearly a thousandfold. The volume swelled, yet it is hard to say the value swelled with it. When a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure, an old warning returning this time at the scale of a national statistic.

And this is not only a story about a faraway country. The first number most companies reach for when reporting on their own AI is tokens consumed. A dashboard that doubled last month's usage: did it really double the value?

Deciding what is worth counting, before we start counting. That is close to why Pebblous has always looked at data as quality rather than volume. The question of whether we are measuring the right thing comes back, in the end, to everyone who works with data.
