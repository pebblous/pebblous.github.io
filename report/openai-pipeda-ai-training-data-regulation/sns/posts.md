## LinkedIn (KO)

G7에서 AI 훈련 데이터 합법성에 대해 공식 판정을 처음 내린 나라가 캐나다였다. 2026년 5월 6일, 4 위원회가 한 자리에서 같은 사실관계를 보고 네 가지 결론에 도달했다.

PIPEDA Findings #2026-002. 연방 OPC는 "well-founded and conditionally resolved"로 종결했고, 퀘벡 CAI는 권고로 처리했고, BC와 Alberta는 시정 약속을 거부하며 "well-founded but unresolved"로 남겼다. BC 위원장 Michael Harvey의 한 줄이 헤드라인이 됐다 — "ChatGPT, by design, cannot be compliant with the province's privacy law."

가장 결정적인 일곱 글자는 BC·Alberta 공동 입장에 있다. "사후 동의는 불가능하다(consent cannot be obtained after the fact)." 크롤 → 학습 → 사후 옵트아웃이라는 AI 산업의 표준 흐름이 법적으로 닫혔다는 선언이다. 다섯 위반은 한 사슬로 묶인다 — 과다 수집 → 동의 부재 → 정확성 결여(환각의 법적 재정의) → 권리 행사 불가능 → 책임 회피. 이탈리아 €15M 과징금(2024) → EU AI Act 고위험 전면 시행(2026-08-02, 최대 매출 7%) → 한국 AI 기본법(2026-01-22)과 강화된 PIPA(매출 10%)가 같은 도미노에 서 있다. 가상의 한국 LLM 기업(글로벌 매출 US$1B 기준)의 4중 노출 보수 추정치는 약 US$140M.

페블러스는 이 결정을 AI-Ready Data → AI-Ready Compliance의 등장으로 읽는다. DataClinic이 진단해 온 5신호(레이블·분포·신선도·결측·이상치) 위에 6번째 신호 "합법성"이 얹힌다. 도덕이 묻고(Magnifica), 학술이 답하고(SkillOpt, MUSE), 법이 강제하는(PIPEDA) 4부작의 마지막 좌표.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Canada became the first G7 jurisdiction to issue a formal ruling on the lawfulness of AI training data. On May 6, 2026, four privacy commissioners looked at the same set of facts and arrived at four different conclusions.

PIPEDA Findings #2026-002. The federal OPC closed the file as "well-founded and conditionally resolved." Quebec's CAI chose recommendations. BC and Alberta refused the resolution and marked it "well-founded but unresolved." BC Commissioner Michael Harvey's line ran as the headline — "ChatGPT, by design, cannot be compliant with the province's privacy law as currently written."

The most decisive sentence belongs to the joint BC and Alberta position: "Consent for scraped data simply cannot be obtained after the fact." That one clause closes the AI industry's standard sequence — crawl, train, then offer opt-out — as a legally viable path. Five violations link into one chain: overcollection → absence of consent → factual inaccuracies (a legal redefinition of hallucination) → broken access and correction rights → accountability gap. The same domino runs through Italy's €15M Garante fine in 2024, the EU AI Act's high-risk regime going live on August 2, 2026 (up to 7% of global turnover), Korea's AI Framework Act effective January 22, 2026, and Korea's PIPA cap raised from 3% to 10% of revenue. A hypothetical Korean LLM provider at US$1B in global revenue carries an estimated four-layer exposure of about US$140M.

At Pebblous, we read this ruling as the arrival of AI-Ready Data → AI-Ready Compliance. The five signals DataClinic has long diagnosed — labels, distribution, freshness, missingness, outliers — are joined by a sixth: lawfulness. Final coordinate of the AI Governance Quartet, where morality asks, scholarship answers, and law enforces.

## Facebook (EN)

"Consent cannot be obtained after the fact."

It took me a few days to sit with the fact that those seven words came out of a joint position by two Canadian privacy offices — British Columbia and Alberta. The decision is PIPEDA Findings #2026-002. Four commissioners. A three-year joint investigation. One sentence read together in the same room.

What stayed with me was not the verdict itself but the way four offices, looking at the same record, walked away with four different conclusions.

The federal OPC accepted OpenAI's remediation plan. Quebec's CAI issued recommendations instead of penalties. BC and Alberta refused the resolution. BC Commissioner Michael Harvey's line became the headline across the Globe and Mail and CBC — "ChatGPT, by design, cannot be compliant with the province's privacy law as currently written."

A diagnosis of design, not operation.

That single sentence closes the standard sequence the AI industry has run on for years — crawl, then train, then offer opt-out. I keep returning to how that sequence has carried the entire LLM industry for half a decade, and how a seven-word clause from two Canadian commissioners can press the legal door shut on it.

Italy's Garante fined OpenAI €15M in late 2024. The EU AI Act's high-risk regime goes live on August 2, 2026, with penalties up to 7% of global turnover. Korea's AI Framework Act took effect on January 22, 2026, and Korea's PIPA cap was raised from 3% to 10% of revenue. Canada's ruling is not the first piece in this domino — it is closer to the inflection. A hypothetical Korean LLM provider at US$1B in global revenue carries a conservative four-layer exposure of about US$140M. Heavier than the number is what sits next to it: the risk of being closed out of a market.

This is the line on which Pebblous has been writing the AI Governance Quartet — morality (Magnifica), scholarship (SkillOpt, MUSE), and law (PIPEDA) as one shape. AI governance, I think, is a three-dimensional question, and any single dimension alone leaves it unfinished. Canada's ruling is the first coordinate where the three are tied into a single knot.

The legal quality of data is something we now diagnose. That sentence keeps surfacing.
