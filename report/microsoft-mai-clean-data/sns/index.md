## LinkedIn (KO)

마이크로소프트가 새 모델 MAI를 "깨끗하고 라이선스된 데이터로 학습했다"고 발표했다. 그런데 같은 회사가 며칠 뒤 낸 기술 보고서에는 Common Crawl에서 가져온 242억 페이지가 학습 코퍼스로 적혀 있었다. 마케팅 문장과 기술 문서가 한 회사 안에서 부딪힌 것이다.

그리고 이 선언은 점점 비싸지고 있다. EU AI Act는 8월부터 학습 데이터 공개를 의무화하고, 위반 과징금 상한은 매출의 3%다.

당신 회사가 "우리 데이터는 깨끗하다"고 말할 때, 그 문장을 무엇으로 증명할 수 있는가. 페블러스가 DataClinic으로 학습 데이터의 품질과 계보를 정량 진단하는 이유가 여기에 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Microsoft unveiled its new MAI models as trained on "clean, licensed" data. Days later, the company's own technical report listed 24.2 billion pages pulled from Common Crawl in the training corpus. A marketing line and a technical document, contradicting each other inside the same company.

The point isn't that Microsoft lied. With 64% of major LLMs relying on Common Crawl, this is closer to the industry norm than an exception. The real problem is that "clean" was a claim no one could prove. Without infrastructure to record and verify data lineage, even the world's largest software company gets tripped up by its own paperwork.

And the claim is getting expensive. The EU AI Act mandates training-data disclosure from August, with fines reaching 3% of global revenue.

## Facebook (EN)

I watched a single company put out two different sentences within days of each other.

One was an announcement: trained on "clean, licensed" data.
The other was a technical report listing 24.2 billion pages pulled from Common Crawl.

Both came from Microsoft.

The first instinct is to ask who lied. But the documents point somewhere else. 64% of major language models draw from that same Common Crawl. This wasn't Microsoft's private misstep; it was the ground nearly the whole industry stands on.

So the question moved.
"What, exactly, makes data 'clean'?"

We say "clean data" often, yet we rarely ask what holds the word up. Where the data came from, what path it traveled, the lineage that someone recorded and verified. Without that record, even the largest software company on earth gets caught by a single line in its own report.

And the question now carries a price. From August, the EU AI Act asks for training-data disclosure, and the weight of getting it wrong is measured as a share of revenue. Provable data is sliding from a matter of ethics into a matter of accounting.
