## LinkedIn (KO)

AI에 돈을 쓰는 기업은 많다. 그런데 그 돈의 결실은 쏠린다. PwC가 1,217개 글로벌 대형 상장사를 조사했더니, AI가 만든 경제적 가치의 74%를 상위 20% 기업이 가져갔다.

같은 기술, 비슷한 투자, 다른 결과. 왜일까. PwC의 분석은 직관을 뒤집는 답을 내놓는다. 리더와 낙오를 가른 것은 AI를 얼마나 많이 도입했느냐가 아니라, 무엇에 썼느냐였다. AI 재무 성과의 가장 강한 단일 요인은 효율 절감이 아니라 성장 기회의 포착이었다. 리더 기업은 비즈니스 모델 재창조를 보고할 확률이 동종 대비 2.6배 높았고, 도구를 추가하는 대신 워크플로를 재설계했다. 그 결과 동종 기업 대비 7.2배 많은 AI 기반 재무 성과를 거뒀고, 영업이익률은 4%포인트 더 높았다.

낙오 기업이 멈춘 자리는 "효율"이었다. 인건비를 줄이고 속도를 높인다. 측정하기 쉬운 목표다. 문제는 경쟁사도 같은 효율을 추구하기 때문에, 성과가 산업 중앙값에 묻혀 격차로 드러나지 않는다. 효율은 따라잡히는 우위고, 성장은 누적되는 우위다.

리더의 우위는 모델에서 나오지 않았다. 깨끗한 데이터와 재사용 가능한 컴포넌트, 그리고 자율성과 거버넌스를 함께 키운 토대에서 나왔다. AI fitness index의 절반이 토대다. AI 효과가 보이지 않을 때 던져야 할 첫 질문은 "더 좋은 모델은 무엇인가"가 아니라 "우리는 무엇 위에서 이 일을 하고 있는가"다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

PwC surveyed 1,217 executives at large-cap companies across 25 sectors. The finding was blunt: 74% of AI's economic value is captured by just 20% of firms.

Same technology. Similar investment levels. Vastly different outcomes. The gap between AI leaders and laggards, PwC found, was not about how much AI they adopted. It was about what they pointed it at. The strongest single driver of AI-attributed financial performance was not efficiency. It was growth. Leaders were 2.6 times more likely to report improved ability to reinvent their business model, and 7.2 times more likely to outperform peers on AI-linked financial results, with operating margins running 4 percentage points higher.

The trap for most companies is an efficiency bias. Cost reduction is measurable, defensible, and easy to pitch. The problem is that competitors adopt the same automation tools within months, and the gains level out to an industry baseline. Efficiency is an advantage that gets copied. Growth — capturing new revenue streams, entering cross-industry opportunities — is an advantage that compounds.

Leaders built on a different foundation: clean data, reusable components catalogued centrally, and governance scaled alongside autonomy. Half of PwC's AI fitness index measures these foundations. When AI returns look flat, the right question is not which model to upgrade — it is what the model is standing on.

## Facebook (EN)

"We've deployed AI. Why aren't we seeing results?"

This question comes up often in conversations about enterprise AI adoption.
And I keep wondering whether the problem is not the absence of results — but that the results are being absorbed into an industry average.

PwC published a study this April surveying 1,217 executives at large-cap companies across 25 sectors.
The headline: the top 20% of firms are capturing 74% of AI's economic value.

Same technology. Similar investment levels.
Yet the returns are concentrating.

What made the difference was not the amount of AI deployed.
It was where the AI was pointed.

Companies that focused on efficiency — automating tasks, reducing headcount costs — saw their gains level out.
Competitors adopt the same tools within months, and the savings become a new industry baseline.

Leaders aimed at growth.
They were 2.6 times more likely to report improved capacity to reinvent their business model.
They were 2.8 times more likely to expand autonomous AI decision-making — and simultaneously strengthened governance to match.
Not less oversight because they trusted the AI more.
More oversight because they were asking the AI to do more consequential things.

The research points to something quiet but important: the leaders' advantage did not come from better models.
It came from the foundation — clean data that could be pulled and reused, components catalogued centrally so new projects didn't start from zero, and governance built to keep pace with autonomy.

"Good results come from good foundations." That is how PwC summarizes it.

It is the kind of sentence that seems obvious after you read it, and quietly damning before you do.
