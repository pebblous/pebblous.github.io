The 20% Taking 74% of AI's Value — What They Did Differently

PwC's 2026 AI Performance Study surveyed 1,217 large-cap executives across 25 sectors. The results suggest the question most companies are asking — "Are we using enough AI?" — may be the wrong question entirely.

A split that keeps widening

Almost every large company is spending on AI. That much is settled. What is not settled is where the returns go.

PwC's 2026 AI Performance Study, published in April, put numbers on a gap that practitioners have sensed but rarely been able to quantify: 74% of AI's economic value is captured by just 20% of firms. The study covered 1,217 executives at large-cap companies globally, all of them firms already investing meaningfully in AI. This is not a comparison between early adopters and skeptics. It is a comparison between companies that are all in — and still arriving at very different places.

AI leaders outperformed peers by 7.2 times on AI-attributed revenue and efficiency gains, with operating margins running 4 percentage points higher. PwC's researchers note that the gap is not stable — it is widening. The faster learners are compounding their advantage each year.

The wrong variable

The instinct when AI returns look disappointing is to reach for a better model. The PwC data suggests this instinct is misplaced.

What separated leaders from laggards was not the volume of AI deployed. It was the direction. The single strongest driver of AI-linked financial performance was not efficiency optimization — it was the pursuit of growth opportunities, particularly in spaces where industry boundaries are breaking down.

What leaders did differently (vs. industry peers):

2.6x — more likely to report improved ability to reinvent their business model

2–3x — more likely to identify and pursue cross-industry growth opportunities

2.8x — more likely to expand autonomous AI decision-making

2.4x — more likely to build reusable, centrally catalogued AI components

2.0x — more likely to redesign workflows rather than layer tools on top

The efficiency trap is real. Cost reduction is a visible, measurable goal — easy to report to a board, easy to defend in a budget cycle. The problem is that competitors adopt the same automation tools within months. Once everyone is using the same toolset for the same tasks, the savings level out to a new industry baseline. Efficiency is an advantage that gets copied. Growth — entering new markets first, building products at the intersection of industries — is an advantage that compounds.

Autonomy and governance, together

Leaders expanded autonomous AI decision-making. They were also 1.7 times more likely to have a Responsible AI framework in place, and 1.5 times more likely to operate a cross-functional AI governance committee. Employees at leader firms were twice as likely to trust AI-generated outputs.

This pairing matters. It is tempting to read autonomy and governance as a trade-off — more AI control means less human oversight. The data shows the opposite. Leaders scaled governance alongside autonomy, precisely because expanding AI's decision-making footprint raises the stakes for getting it wrong. The trust that results is not abstract: employees who trust AI outputs are more likely to actually use them, which is what makes expanded autonomy productive rather than cosmetic.

The foundation question

PwC measures AI performance through an "AI fitness index" built on two axes: AI use (how deeply and broadly the organization applies AI) and AI foundations (the infrastructure enabling that use). The index's foundations component covers data and technology, talent, governance and risk, strategy, investment, and innovation.

The two axes multiply rather than add. Strong foundations amplify the return on every unit of increased AI use. Weak foundations mean that adding more AI use produces only marginal improvement. This is why leaders invested in reusable AI components (2.4x more likely) and in data quality that could be trusted for autonomous decisions.

The pattern behind "stuck in pilot" — the common situation where impressive demonstrations never scale to operational use — traces back to the same structural gap. When data is scattered across department silos in inconsistent formats, every new project starts at zero. The cost of starting at zero keeps teams inside the efficiency perimeter, where the scope is small enough to manage without clean, reusable assets.

Three questions before the next model upgrade

PwC's practical implication is a reordering of priorities. When AI returns look flat, the diagnostic questions should run in this sequence:

First: what are we aiming at? If the portfolio of AI use cases is weighted toward cost reduction, the returns will be real but invisible at the portfolio level — averaged into an industry baseline that everyone else is also hitting. The question to ask first is not "what can we automate?" but "what new revenue could we open?"

Second: how is it embedded? Tools added on top of unchanged workflows produce incremental gains. Redesigned workflows — where AI handles defined decision ranges while humans focus on judgment-intensive tasks — produce structural advantages.

Third: what is it standing on? The reusability of verified data, models, and validation pipelines is what allows successful pilots to scale and new projects to start at 70 rather than zero. That reusability is a function of data quality and governance — the foundations that half of PwC's index measures.

The conclusion PwC draws is direct: "Good results come from good foundations." The 74:20 split is not about who deployed AI first. It is about who built something durable enough to compound on.
