## LinkedIn (EN)

In a single session, Korea's stock market shed roughly 400 billion dollars. The trigger was not weak earnings. It was Meta conceding that it has servers to spare.

On July 1, Bloomberg reported that Meta is preparing to rent out spare AI compute from its data centers. Meta's stock rose nearly 9 percent. The next day SK hynix fell 14.6 percent, Samsung 9 percent, and Micron more than 10.

When one of the world's largest chip buyers admits it has idle capacity, the shortage narrative that propped up the entire AI hardware trade begins to crack.

Yet the data to test that narrative never existed. Measured GPU utilization sits near 43 percent against an assumed 80, and idle capacity and per-service AI revenue have never been disclosed.

The selloff was not a sudden accident. It was the bill for a demand signal no one had verified. Verifying the quality of a signal is close to the work Pebblous has spent years on.

## Facebook (EN)

"We have servers to spare."

That line from Meta drifted across the feed a few days ago.

The same news lifted Meta's stock by almost nine percent. The next day, Korea's market erased roughly four hundred billion dollars, and SK hynix alone fell more than fourteen percent in a single session.

What stayed with me was not the price.

For three years, one belief carried the entire chip market: AI hardware sells out faster than anyone can build it. And yet I kept wondering who had actually seen the data behind that belief.

How much of each GPU is really running? The measured figure sits near forty-three percent, well below the eighty everyone assumed. How much capacity sits idle, and how much do these AI services truly earn? None of it has ever been disclosed.

So "it sells out" was never a fact. It was a story no one had checked. Hundreds of billions were built on top of it, and Meta's spare servers became the first visible proof of the excess.

That is why I read the crash as a settlement rather than an accident. "What are we using to verify this demand?" The cost of postponing that question came due in a single day.

Verifying the quality of a signal is close to the work Pebblous has spent years on. The way training-data quality decides what a model becomes, the quality of a demand signal decides where hundreds of billions of dollars go.

The absence of a signal. Perhaps that was the clearest signal of all.
