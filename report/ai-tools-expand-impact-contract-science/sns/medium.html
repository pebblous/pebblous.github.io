Individuals Get Stronger, Science Gets Narrower — What 41.3 Million Papers Are Saying

A January 2026 paper in Nature finally weighs an old argument: does AI help science or hurt it? The answer is "both, in opposite directions." And the six fields it did not analyze tell a story of their own.

The Argument That Outlived Its Anecdotes

"Does AI help science or hurt it?" has been a coffee-table argument for at least four years. One camp has pointed to AlphaFold 2 and proclaimed a new golden age. The other has pointed to hallucinations and warned of erosion. Both sides fought with anecdotes.

In January 2026, Nature Vol. 649, pp. 1237–1243 placed the first answer at scale on the table. James A. Evans (University of Chicago Knowledge Lab) and Yong Li (Tsinghua University), with co-authors Qianyue Hao and Fengli Xu, published "Artificial intelligence tools expand scientists' impact but contract science's focus" (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09922-y). Using a BERT-based classifier (F1 = 0.875, five-expert Fleiss' Kappa 0.964) across 23 years of papers in six natural-science fields — biology, medicine, chemistry, physics, materials science, geology — they nailed down what had only lived in stories. 41.3 million papers.

Six Statistics, One Paradox

The result fits in one paragraph. Two individual-level gains, two collective-level losses, all in the same 23 years:

  • +3.02x papers. A researcher who routinely uses AI publishes roughly three times as many papers as a peer in the same field at the same career stage.
  • +4.84x citations. The same AI-using researchers receive nearly five times more citations — a clear acceleration of the Matthew effect.
  • −1.37 years to PI. AI users become project leaders roughly fourteen months earlier than non-users.
  • −4.63% collective topic diversity. The total volume of distinct topics studied by the field shrinks.
  • −22% follow-on engagement. Scientists engage with one another's subsequent work much less often. Evans calls these "lonely crowds."

And the citation-concentration Gini sits at 0.754. The top 22.20% of papers absorb 80% of citations. The Matthew effect, in its AI-era form.

"AI adoption in science presents a seeming paradox — an expansion of individual scientists' impact but a contraction in collective science's reach — as AI-augmented work moves collectively toward areas richest in data."
— Hao, Xu, Li, Evans (2026)

The one-line summary is the headline of those two sentences: individuals get stronger, science gets narrower.

The Streetlight Effect, Measured

Why would aggregating individual rationality narrow collective knowledge? The geometry is the old streetlight effect, scaled.

AI tools are trained on data. They are sharper where data is abundant and weaker where it is scarce. A rational individual researcher, choosing where to point an AI assistant, will choose the place where the assistant is sharpest — the data-rich domain. Multiply that choice across an entire field, across 23 years, and the lit circle under the streetlight grows brighter while the alleys around it darken. Evans, in his own words:

"AI concentrates attention on data-rich domains while leaving a growing number of potentially fruitful areas unexplored."

This is not a moral failure. It is what happens when many locally optimal choices stack into a globally narrowing portfolio. The literature has a name for the resulting state — algorithmic monoculture (Kleinberg · Raghavan, PNAS 2021): when many actors lean on a small number of shared AI systems, their outputs converge, and the diversity of solutions in the system collapses. The 41.3-million-paper study is the first time that convergence has been measured at the scale of an entire scientific literature.

The Meta-Evidence Hiding in the Method

Here is the part that arrests me. The six fields the paper analyzed are biology, medicine, chemistry, physics, materials science, geology. Mathematics, theoretical physics, and philosophy were never in the set.

Why not? Because publication data for those fields is sparse and structurally different — fewer co-author networks, fewer machine-readable abstracts of the kind a BERT classifier can chew through. Evans's team picked the six fields where the method works.

Which is, of course, the very thesis the paper proves. Data-poor fields drop out of analysis before they drop out of attention. The selection of fields is itself the strongest evidence of the streetlight effect. The fields most at risk of being narrowed away cannot even appear in the figure that shows narrowing.

The Policy Window Is Open — and Pointing the Wrong Way

On March 25, 2026, Nature ran an editorial titled "AI scientists are changing research — institutions, funders and publishers must respond." Funders moved quickly. Korea, in particular, launched a rapid sequence:

  • NRF AI+S&T six priority fields — bio, materials chemistry, earth science, semiconductors, energy, secondary batteries.
  • 2026 AI Co-Scientist Challenge Korea — KRW 1.9 billion prize pool (~USD 1.3M).
  • InnoCORE postdoc program — 400 researchers across four science-and-technology institutes at KRW 90M per year.
  • LG's EXAONE Discovery — screening 40 million materials per day, an industrial-scale incarnation of the same coordinate.

Funding moves fast, but every one of the priority fields is data-rich. None of the programs include a diversity-preservation mechanism. The policy window is open and the resources are pointing into the brightest part of the lit circle.

What to Take Away

Three things, lifted out of the 41.3 million.

First, the paradox is real and now measured. The same 23 years that produced a three-fold jump in individual productivity also produced a four-and-a-half-percent narrowing of the collective topic portfolio. Both are facts. The argument is over.

Second, the streetlight effect is structural, not moral. Every local choice was rational. The aggregate is still a contraction. Policies that reward only individual output will accelerate, not solve, the narrowing.

Third, the response is not to slow AI down. It is to widen the data foundation under it — to restore signal in domains where natural data is sparse, and to measure diversity as a first-class property of the data communities train on. That is the work Pebblous is in.
