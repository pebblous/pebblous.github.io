Nature 4,130만 편이 처음으로 답을 내놓았다 — AI는 과학을 돕는가, 망치는가. 답은 "둘 다, 그러나 정반대 방향으로".

James Evans(시카고대 Knowledge Lab)와 Tsinghua 공동연구팀이 23년 시계열 자연과학 6 분야를 BERT 분류기(F1 0.875, 5인 Fleiss κ 0.964)로 매칭했다. AI 활용 연구자는 동료 대비 논문 3.02배, 인용 4.84배, 프로젝트 리더 등극 1.37년 빨라진다. 같은 23년에 집단 주제 다양성은 4.63% 줄었고, 연구자 후속 참여는 22% 감소했다. 인용 집중 지니 0.754 — 상위 22.20%가 인용의 80%를 흡수하는 매튜 효과의 AI 시대 변형이다.

분석된 6개 분야는 생물·의학·화학·물리·재료·지질이다. 수학·이론물리·철학은 처음부터 빠져 있다. 데이터 빈약 영역은 가시화되기 전부터 분석에서조차 누락된다. Evans 본인의 표현으로 "AI가 데이터 풍부 영역에 주의를 집중시키는 동안, 비옥할 수 있는 미탐색 영역이 점점 더 늘어난다." 가로등 효과(streetlight effect)와 알고리즘 단일문화의 정량 증거다.

Nature는 3월 25일 사설로 "AI scientists are changing research"를 띄웠다. 한국은 NRF AI+S&T 6대 분야, AI Co-Scientist Challenge Korea 19억, InnoCORE 400명 박사후 사업을 잇따라 출범했다. 자금은 빠르지만 6대 분야 모두 데이터 풍부 영역이고, 다양성 보존 메커니즘은 부재하다. 페블러스는 이 공백을 정조준한다 — DataClinic 7번째 신호 "지식 다양성", 데이터 빈약 영역을 복원하는 DataGreenhouse(합성데이터)와 PebbloSim(시뮬레이션). 4부작(Magnifica · SkillOpt · MUSE · PIPEDA)에 이은 메타-과학사회학 좌표.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Nature has finally put a number on a decade-old argument — does AI help science or hurt it? The answer is "both, in opposite directions."

James Evans (University of Chicago Knowledge Lab) and a Tsinghua team analyzed 41.3 million papers across 23 years and six natural-science fields using a BERT classifier (F1 = 0.875, Fleiss κ = 0.964 across five experts). Scientists who use AI publish 3.02x more papers, receive 4.84x more citations, and become PIs 1.37 years earlier. In the same 23 years, the collective volume of topics shrinks by 4.63%, and follow-on engagement among scientists drops by 22%. The citation Gini sits at 0.754 — the AI-era variant of the Matthew effect, with the top 22.20% of papers absorbing 80% of citations.

The six fields analyzed are biology, medicine, chemistry, physics, materials science, geology. Mathematics, theoretical physics, and philosophy were never in the set. Data-poor fields drop out of analysis before they drop out of attention. In Evans's words: "AI concentrates attention on data-rich domains while leaving a growing number of potentially fruitful areas unexplored." That is the streetlight effect and algorithmic monoculture, finally measured at scale.

Nature followed on March 25, 2026 with an editorial — "AI scientists are changing research — institutions, funders and publishers must respond." Korea is moving fast: NRF AI+S&T six priority fields, AI Co-Scientist Challenge Korea (KRW 1.9B), InnoCORE 400 postdocs. Funding moves quickly, but all six are data-rich, and no diversity-preservation mechanism is in place. Pebblous targets that gap head-on — a seventh DataClinic signal, "Knowledge Diversity," and the diversity-restoration infrastructure of DataGreenhouse (synthetic data) and PebbloSim (simulation). Following Magnifica · SkillOpt · MUSE · PIPEDA, this is the meta-and-sociology-of-science coordinate of our five-part AI-governance arc.

"Does AI help science or hurt it?"

It is an old argument. Both sides have been fighting with anecdotes.

This January, Nature placed 41.3 million natural-science papers on the table and offered the first answer at scale.

James Evans at the University of Chicago and a Tsinghua team matched 23 years of work through a BERT classifier. The result fits in a single sentence.

"Individuals get stronger. Science gets narrower."

Scientists who use AI publish 3.02 times more, receive 4.84 times more citations, and become project leaders 1.37 years earlier than their peers. Across the same 23 years, the collective volume of topics shrinks by 4.63%, and follow-on engagement among scientists drops by 22%.

I sit with those two lines for a long time. People are doing better. The shared body of work is getting narrower. Both at once.

And then I sit with another fact.

The fields analyzed are biology, medicine, chemistry, physics, materials science, geology. Mathematics, theoretical physics, and philosophy were never in the set.

"Data-poor fields drop out of analysis before they drop out of attention."

Evans writes that AI "concentrates attention on data-rich domains while leaving a growing number of potentially fruitful areas unexplored." Only the ground beneath the streetlight is bright; the alleys without one grow darker. Science walks together into the lit circle.

That is why Pebblous keeps looking at this coordinate alongside the work it is already doing. Diagnosing what is data-rich, with DataClinic. And — beyond the moral (Magnifica), academic (SkillOpt), legal (PIPEDA), and evolutionary (MUSE) coordinates of the previous four pieces — restoring what is data-poor, with the synthetic foundation of DataGreenhouse and the simulation foundation of PebbloSim. The other half of the same picture. The 41.3 million papers, in their own way, testify to it from the same place.

I would be glad if you would look at this scene with me — the era we live in, where acceleration and narrowing are happening at the same time.
