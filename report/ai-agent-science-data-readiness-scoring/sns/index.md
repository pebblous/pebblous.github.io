과학 데이터가 AI 학습에 쓸 만한지를, 이제 사람 체크리스트가 아니라 멀티에이전트 시스템이 루브릭으로 채점한다. 2026년 4월 공개된 SciHorizon-DataEVA는 천문부터 사회경제까지 6개 분야 약 80개 데이터셋을 읽고, 데이터셋마다 필요한 분석 도구를 스스로 만들어 점수를 냈다. 전체 평가 성공률은 89%였다. 채점의 축은 거버넌스 신뢰성, 데이터 품질, AI 호환성, 과학 적응성 네 차원으로 짜인 Sci-TQA² 루브릭이다. 준비도를 매기는 주체가 사람에서 에이전트로, 대상이 학습을 끝낸 모델에서 학습 전 데이터셋으로 옮겨간 것이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Scientific data is no longer judged AI-ready by a human with a checklist. A multi-agent system now grades it against a rubric. Released in April 2026, SciHorizon-DataEVA read roughly 80 datasets across six scientific fields, wrote its own analysis tools for each, and reached an overall evaluation success rate of 89%. The rubric, Sci-TQA², scores readiness on four axes: governance trustworthiness, data quality, AI compatibility, and scientific adaptability. The grader has shifted from human to agent, and the object from a finished model to the dataset before it ever reaches training.

But the moment an agent does the grading, the trust question moves up a level. Agreement with human experts landed at ICC 0.742, just under the threshold most call "good." Strip out the loop where the system rechecks its own scores, and success collapses from 89% to 33%. The question is no longer "is this data good?" but "who vouches for the rubric?" Governance that once verified data now has to verify the standard doing the verifying.

## Facebook (EN)

"Is our data actually ready for AI?"

For years, the person answering that question in a data team was usually the most experienced one in the room.

They would open a checklist, scan for missing values, and settle on a verdict. The judgment leaned on a trained eye.

A study released this April moves that judgment somewhere else. The one doing the grading is no longer a person but a multi-agent system, and what it grades is not a finished model but the dataset before it ever reaches training. It read data across six scientific fields, wrote its own analysis tools, and scored each set. The overall success rate was 89%, and human experts rated its grading above four out of five.

The numbers look reassuring. What held me was somewhere else.

Agreement between human and system came to 0.742, just beneath the line we call "good." And when the researchers removed the single layer where the system rechecks its own work, success fell from 89% to 33%. More than half of a confident-looking number was resting on one thin layer.

Which raises a second question. If an agent can tell us whether the data is good, then who vouches for the rubric it grades with?

Governance used to mean verifying the data. It now seems to reach one level higher, to the standard doing the verifying. That Pebblous arrived at a similar four-layer picture through DataClinic's fieldwork is, I suspect, just two paths crossing the same map.

We can grade readiness now. Learning to question the grade may be the part that just opened.
