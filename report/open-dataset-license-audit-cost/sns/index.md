Data Provenance Initiative가 1,858개 데이터셋을 감사했더니, 인기 사이트의 텍스트 데이터셋은 열 개 중 일곱 개가 라이선스 정보를 아예 빠뜨리고 있었다. "라이선스 없음"은 "그냥 써도 됨"이 아니다. "직접 확인하라"는 뜻이고, 그 확인이 곧 감사 노동이다.

취득 비용은 0에 수렴했지만 검증 비용은 그대로 남아 사람이 시급으로 지불한다. 미국 기준 가장 흔한 시나리오에서 프로그램 하나 감사 비용은 3,600~6,700달러, 변호사가 대거 붙는 고위험 시나리오에서는 3만 달러대로 뛴다.

결국 오픈 데이터 시대의 진입장벽은 데이터를 확보하는 능력이 아니라, 쓸 수 있음을 싸고 빠르게 증명하는 능력이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Public training datasets are free to download. Auditing whether you're actually allowed to use them costs 40 to 160 hours per training program.

The Data Provenance Initiative audited 1,858 datasets and found that roughly seven in ten text datasets carry no license information at all. "No license" does not mean "free to use." It means "verify it yourself," and that verification is the work.

Download costs have collapsed toward zero; verification costs have not, and people still pay them by the hour. In the most common scenario, auditing one program runs $3,600 to $6,700 in the US, climbing into the $30,000 range once lawyers are heavily involved.

Auditing is still always cheaper than the alternative, because the bill for skipping it is an order of magnitude larger. Anthropic settled at roughly $3,113 per work, and the EU AI Act, in full force from August 2026, penalizes data-governance violations at up to 3% of global revenue.

In the open-data era, the barrier to entry is no longer the ability to acquire data. It is the ability to prove, cheaply and quickly, that you're allowed to use it.

## Facebook (EN)

Downloading a dataset now takes a few clicks.

Terabytes land on your disk in minutes, from Hugging Face, for free.

But who checks whether you're actually allowed to put that data into a product?

The cost of downloading has fallen close to zero. The cost of confirming you may use it has not moved at all. And the more data you gather, the more there is to check.

In one audit, seven of every ten text datasets on popular hubs had no license information at all. "No license" turned out to mean not "go ahead" but "verify it yourself."

Measured in human hours, that verification runs a few to fifteen hours per dataset, and roughly forty to a hundred and sixty hours to clear a whole program. The hardest parts — reading ambiguous clauses, deciding whether commercial use is safe — call for a lawyer's time, not an engineer's.

What struck me is that the audit was always cheaper than the alternative. The bill for skipping it, and infringing, sits an order of magnitude higher. Seen that way, the audit reads less like a cost and more like an insurance premium.

Pebblous has said the same thing for years: ask a dataset's provenance before you ask about its performance. This piece simply put a price tag on that question for the first time.

Maybe the real edge in the open-data era isn't how much data you hold. It's how quickly and cheaply you can prove you're allowed to use it. That question has stayed with me.
