## LinkedIn (EN)

On April 10, 2026, the Artemis II crew of four returned from the first crewed lunar flight in 52 years. They traveled 252,756 miles -- breaking Apollo 13's 56-year-old record of 248,655 miles. But the real significance lies elsewhere.

That same month, NASA cancelled the Gateway lunar space station entirely and reallocated $20B to lunar surface base construction. The strategy shift: permanent surface presence, not orbital transit. Communications infrastructure (LunaNet), positioning and navigation systems, energy supply chains, and resource extraction -- these four layers define the new battleground on the Moon. The US has rallied 60 nations through the Artemis Accords; China is building its own bloc through the ILRS.

South Korea is an Artemis Accords signatory and has announced a 2029 lunar communications orbiter, but unlike Japan (lunar rover) or Canada (robotic arm), it has not secured a concrete role in the lunar operations framework. This report analyzes the technology, geopolitics, and business structure of the lunar operations race.
