## LinkedIn (KO)

AI 회사가 콘텐츠를 사들이는 방식이 3년 만에 뒤집혔다. 출처를 표시하고 실시간으로 접근하는 형태의 라이선싱 거래는 2023년 2건에서 2026년 34건 규모로 늘었다.

워싱턴포스트는 OpenAI와의 계약에서 아예 '학습'을 지웠다. ChatGPT가 기사를 요약·인용하고 원문으로 링크하되, 콘텐츠를 모델 학습에는 쓰지 않는 조건이다. 레딧은 과거 게시물을 파는 대신 실시간 스트림을 API로 구독시켜 구글과 OpenAI에서 연 1억 3천만 달러를 번다. 사는 대상이 정적 아카이브에서 갱신되는 접근권으로 옮겨간 것이다.

이유는 데이터의 유통기한에 있다. 학습 컷오프 이후의 사실을 다룰 때, 검색 증강을 붙인 모델과 그렇지 않은 모델의 정확도 격차가 44%까지 벌어진다. 한 번 산 데이터는 출시 순간부터 노화하니, 계속 빌리는 쪽이 합리적이다.

그런데 흐르는 데이터를 다룰 표준은 아직 없다. 정정이 모델 안으로 어떻게 전파되는지, 출력에 출처를 역링크할 수 있는지 모두 미해결로 남아 있다. 검증되지 않은 흐름은 자산이 아니라 부채가 된다.

데이터의 가치가 보유하는 자산에서 흐르는 무엇으로 옮겨가면, 데이터 품질도 한 번 검수하는 일에서 흐름을 상시 보증하는 운영으로 옮겨간다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The way AI companies acquire content flipped in three years. Licensing deals built around attribution and live access grew from two in 2023 to a projected 34 in 2026.

The Washington Post struck the word "training" from its OpenAI contract. ChatGPT may summarize, cite, and link back to its articles, but cannot use the content to train the model. Reddit, instead of selling its archive, subscribes its live post stream through an API and earns about $130M a year from Google and OpenAI. What is being bought has moved from a static archive to a refreshed right of access.

The reason is data's shelf life. On facts that postdate the training cutoff, the accuracy gap between a model with retrieval augmentation and one without it widens to 44%. Data bought once starts aging the day a model ships, which makes renting the rational choice.

Yet the standards for handling flowing data do not exist yet. How a correction propagates into a model, whether an output can back-link to its source — both remain unresolved. An unverified flow is not an asset but a liability.

Once data's value moves from a held asset to something that flows, data quality has to move with it: from a one-time inspection to always-on assurance of the flow.

## Facebook (EN)

I read fewer articles all the way through these days.

The answer is already sitting in the AI summary box. In the meantime, the clicks that used to reach publishers have fallen by roughly 80%.

That quiet shift is now reshaping how data itself is traded.

The old licensing looked like a property sale. A backlog of past text, handed over once, the way AP's archive since 1985 was. But the Washington Post struck the word "training" from its OpenAI contract. Summaries, citations, links back to the original were all allowed; using the content to train the model was not. Reddit, rather than selling its old posts, now subscribes out the live stream of new ones.

I started thinking of these as "rented data."

The reason buying gives way to renting is that data has a shelf life. Data bought once begins to age the moment a model ships. Ask it about anything that happened after training, and its accuracy visibly wavers.

So maybe the line we've repeated for years, "data is an asset," was only half true.

Data is less something you stockpile than something that pays only while it flows. Who keeps assuring the freshness and provenance of that flow? An unverified flow is not an asset but a liability. I find myself reading that sentence again, slowly.
