## LinkedIn (KO)

정부가 피지컬 AI 경쟁의 병목으로 칩도 모델도 아닌 '행동 데이터'를 지목했다.

6월 19일 발표된 'K-피지컬 AI 풀스택 전략'은 한국형 파운데이션 모델이나 칩 공급망보다, 전국 5개 권역에 로봇 행동 데이터 트레이닝센터를 먼저 짓겠다고 밝혔다. 칩과 모델은 수입할 수 있지만, 한국 공장과 도로에서 로봇이 실제로 움직인 궤적은 그 환경에서만 나온다는 판단이다.

이유는 기술적이다. 어떤 로봇에서 모은 행동은 관절 구조가 다른 로봇으로, 미국·중국 공장에서 모은 데이터는 한국 라인으로 잘 옮겨가지 않는다(embodiment gap, domain gap). 그래서 데이터는 빌릴 수 없다.

다만 함정이 있다. 한국은 1조 4천억 원을 들인 '데이터 댐'이 형식적 검증으로 "값비싼 무용지물" 비판을 받은 경험이 있다. 행동 데이터는 결과가 더 가혹하다. 잘못 학습된 행동은 물리 세계에서 사고가 되기 때문이다.

텔레오퍼레이션 단가가 2년 만에 3분의 1로 떨어진 지금, 진짜 전선은 '얼마나 모으느냐'가 아니라 '학습 가능한 품질로 보증하느냐'에 그어진다. 데이터를 학습 가능한 품질로 만드는 일을 해 온 페블러스가 같은 질문 앞에 서 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

South Korea has named the bottleneck in the physical AI race, and it isn't chips or models. It's behavior data.

On June 19, the government's "K-Physical AI Full-Stack Strategy" placed a network of regional robot behavior-data training centers ahead of any homegrown foundation model or chip supply line. The logic is simple: GPUs and model weights can be imported, but the trajectories a robot produces on a Korean factory floor exist only there.

The reason is technical. Behavior collected on one robot transfers poorly to another with different joints (the embodiment gap), and data gathered in U.S. or Chinese plants degrades on a Korean line (the domain gap). Data, in other words, cannot be borrowed.

There is a cautionary precedent. Korea's earlier 1.4 trillion-won "Data Dam" was dismissed as an expensive white elephant after its quality checks proved cosmetic. With behavior data the stakes are higher: a badly learned action becomes a collision, not a wrong answer.

As teleoperation costs have fallen by two-thirds in two years, the contest is shifting from how much data you gather to whether you can certify it as trainable, the question Pebblous has worked on all along.

## Facebook (EN)

Ask which piece of the physical AI race a country should secure first, and most answers land on chips, or models.

Korea's answer, announced on June 19, went somewhere else.

Before any homegrown model or chip line, the plan starts with training centers in five regions whose job is to collect one thing: how robots actually behave.

Chips and models can be bought on the open market. The path a robot traces on a Korean factory floor cannot. It exists only where it happens.

It read, to us, like a national-scale version of something we keep saying: you can borrow a model, but you can't borrow data.

What stays with me is what comes next. Korea once spent 1.4 trillion won on a "Data Dam" that filled up on volume and fell short on quality. With behavior data the cost of that mistake is heavier. A wrongly learned motion isn't a wrong answer on a screen; it's an accident in the physical world.

Not how much did we gather, but did we gather it in a form a machine can actually learn from.

That may be the question the next year has to answer.
