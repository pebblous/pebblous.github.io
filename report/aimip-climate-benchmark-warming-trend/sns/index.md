## LinkedIn (EN)

For the first time, eight AI climate models from six research groups stood side by side on a single physics baseline. It is called AIMIP Phase 1, led by the Allen Institute for AI.

On reproducing the average climate of the training period, the best AI models cut the time-mean error to roughly half that of NOAA's physics model — and they did so almost regardless of architecture, from diffusion models to graph neural networks.

Then came the held-out test: 2015–2024, which happens to be the hottest decade on record. Half of the eight models significantly underestimated the warming trend. It is a crack the average metric alone would never have exposed.

Fast and cheap does not automatically mean trustworthy. Without a standard there is no comparison, and without comparison there is no trust. That proposition reaches well beyond climate, into every domain that wants to judge AI by its performance.

## Facebook (EN)

"AI beats the weather agency's supercomputer, again."

You have probably seen that headline a few times over the past years. Each time, I found myself wondering something small: winning is clear enough, but if everyone sits a different exam and calls it a win, is it really a win?

This year, the Allen Institute for AI sat eight AI climate models down in the same exam room for the first time. Same data, same grading sheet, one physics baseline.

The results split in a curious way. On the average climate of the past, the AI models edged out the physics model. But faced with the warming of a recent decade they had never studied, half of them read the slope as gentler than it was. And that decade happened to be the hottest ever recorded.

An average describes how things usually are. A trend describes where they are heading. What we actually want to know is often the latter, yet the scorecard keeps showing us the former. This is also why, when we at Pebblous talk about data quality, we look not only at the input but at the quality of the evaluation data and the protocol behind it.

Between "fast and cheap" and "trustworthy" there seems to be a river not yet crossed. And to measure that river, perhaps we first need to hold the same ruler.
