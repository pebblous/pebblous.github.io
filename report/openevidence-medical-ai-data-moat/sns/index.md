## LinkedIn (EN)

Roughly 40% of licensed US physicians now query a single medical AI about 18 million times a month. OpenEvidence saw its valuation climb twelvefold in eleven months, to $12 billion.

The striking part: the underlying model is not meaningfully different from ChatGPT or Gemini. What sets it apart is what it answers from. The company licenses more than 300 peer-reviewed journals, including NEJM, JAMA and Cochrane, and forces a source citation onto every answer.

General-purpose LLMs fail in medicine not for want of reasoning but for want of verifiable sourcing. Ungrounded medical models hallucinate at rates of 15 to 67%; grounding answers in peer-reviewed literature cuts that by more than 40%.

Adoption and revenue figures are mostly company-reported or third-party estimates, so the structure matters more than the numbers. What can't be copied isn't the model. It's the licensing deals and the curated, source-traceable data behind every answer.

Anyone can rent the model. The data is another matter.

## Facebook (EN)

There's a moment, mid-consult, when a doctor runs out of certainty.

For generations the next move was a colleague, a textbook, a search bar. In the US, more and more, it's an AI. Close to 40% of licensed physicians now turn to one, about 18 million times a month.

The company behind it, OpenEvidence, grew its valuation twelvefold in under a year. And yet its model, by most accounts, is not so different from the chatbots the rest of us use.

So what was different?

Not the model. The sources. The company licenses peer-reviewed medical literature, including NEJM, JAMA and Cochrane, and attaches to every answer the paper it came from. In front of a decision a physician is liable for, an answer you can verify turns out to be a different thing entirely from an answer that merely sounds right.

Anyone can borrow the model. What you can't borrow is data you can trust and trace back to its source. That is not far from why we at Pebblous keep returning to data quality and source-traceable, AI-ready data.

And it leaves a question that may be everyone's: is the data we hold in a shape someone could stake their name on, and cite?
