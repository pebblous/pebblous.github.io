## LinkedIn (KO)

유럽 최고법원이 'LLM은 무엇을 학습해도 되는가'를 처음으로 법정에서 물었다. 2026년 3월 10일, 유럽사법재판소 대재판부 재판관 15인이 헝가리 언론사 Like Company와 Google Gemini의 사건(C-250/25)을 여섯 시간에 걸쳐 심리했다. 쟁점의 핵심은 EU 저작권 지침의 '데이터 마이닝 예외'가 상업용 LLM 학습까지 덮느냐다.

미국이 학습을 일단 하고 나서 공정이용으로 사후에 저울질한다면, 유럽은 데이터를 긁어오는 그 순간 각 저작물의 opt-out 상태로 합법성을 미리 가른다. 뉴스 사이트의 AI 학습봇 차단율이 2년 만에 58%에서 79%로 뛴 것은, 제도가 이제야 관행을 따라잡고 있다는 신호다.

판결은 아직 없고 법무관 의견이 9월 3일로 예정돼 있다. 그러나 방향과 무관하게 결론은 하나로 모인다. 출처와 라이선스, opt-out 상태를 증명하지 못하는 데이터셋은 유럽 배포에서 리스크가 된다. 데이터 출처 추적이 소송 방어 카드에서 배포 요건으로 옮겨가는 지점이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Europe's top court has, for the first time, asked what an LLM is legally allowed to learn from. On 10 March 2026, a 15-judge Grand Chamber of the Court of Justice of the EU heard Like Company v Google (C-250/25), a Hungarian publisher's case against Gemini. The question at its core: does the EU copyright directive's text-and-data-mining exception stretch to cover commercial LLM training?

Where the US weighs fair use after the fact, Europe decides legality up front, at the moment data is scraped, based on each work's machine-readable opt-out. The share of news sites blocking AI crawlers jumped from 58% to 79% in two years, a sign that regulation is only now catching up to practice.

No ruling yet; the Advocate General's opinion is due 3 September. But whichever way it lands, the conclusion converges. A dataset whose provenance, licensing and opt-out status can't be proven becomes a liability for European deployment. Data provenance is moving from a litigation defense card to a condition of shipping.

## Facebook (EN)

Imagine you're building a dataset.

On the first day, as you scrape a few million articles, you tend to count one thing: how much. Europe has just started asking something else first. Did the people who wrote this actually agree to let a machine learn from it?

On 10 March 2026, Europe's highest court heard its first case on generative AI and copyright. A Hungarian publisher had brought it against Google's Gemini. Fifteen judges spent six hours circling a single question: what is an LLM allowed to learn from?

In the US, you would train first and argue later, letting a court weigh fair use after the fact.

Europe reverses the clock. Legality is settled the moment the data is collected, by whether each work carries a machine-readable note saying leave me out.

The share of news sites blocking AI crawlers climbed from 58% to 79% in two years, a quiet sign that the rules are only now catching up to what already happened.

There's no ruling yet. Only an Advocate General's opinion, due in September. And yet, whoever wins, the same question remains: can you prove when, and in what state, you took this data?

Tracing where data comes from is quietly moving from a card you play once you're sued to something you need in place before you ship. Pebblous looks closely at the origin and quality of data for the same reason, because everyone now stands before the same question.
