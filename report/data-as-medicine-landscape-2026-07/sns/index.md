## LinkedIn (EN)

We surveyed nine data observability vendors end to end. Seven of them stop at diagnosis and monitoring.

They borrow medical language freely — "health," "diagnose," "resolve" — but what they actually fix is an incident or a pipeline, not the data value itself. The industry has industrialized the checkup and the alert, while the part that treats data and reads it again stays mostly empty.

The reason sits in the lineage of the metaphor. "Data downtime" and "uptime" are really the language of servers, and only "health" got borrowed as quasi-medical. Data-Centric AI built the discipline of iterating on data, but never put on the clinical vocabulary. Academic diagnostic methods find the problem and, for the most part, stop there.

What is striking is that the decisive precedent was a twenty-year-old medical paper. Van den Broeck et al., PLoS Medicine 2005, already structured data cleaning as a clinical procedure: screening, then diagnosis, then treatment.

What Pebblous adds is not invention but integration. DataClinic images data by density, distance, and distribution to diagnose it, prescribes a data diet or a bulk-up, then images it again to re-diagnose — closing the clinical loop. Running that loop autonomously through Agentic AI rather than by hand is the direction of Data Greenhouse, with the whole process measured against ISO/IEC 5259.

The models are largely built. The remaining bottleneck is data, and the way we handle it is moving from cleaning toward the clinic.

## Facebook (EN)

When did we start saying, so casually, that data is sick?

"Data health." "Diagnosing data." Records that get "quarantined" and "triaged." Read enough pipeline docs and the language of the hospital has quietly seeped in.

So I began to wonder. Is this medical metaphor only a name tag, or has it actually reached the examination room?

I went through nine observability vendors, one by one. Seven of them stop at diagnosis and monitoring. They say they "resolve," but usually what they mend is the incident or the pipeline beside the data, not the data itself. The checkup and the emergency triage have arrived; the operating room, where you treat the data and image it again, sits empty.

The clearest precedent, it turned out, was a twenty-year-old medical paper. A 2005 piece in PLoS Medicine had already framed the work of cleaning data as a clinical procedure: diagnose first, and only then touch it. What felt like a new story was, in fact, a seat that had been waiting a long time.

Pebblous did not invent data medicine. It only threads the scattered pieces — the "health" of observability, quarantine and triage, the diet that removes and the bulk-up that fills, and that diagnose-then-treat procedure from twenty years ago — into one closed loop, and tries to run it autonomously rather than by hand. Less "how much data did we gather," and more: after diagnosing and prescribing, image it again and measure what actually changed.

The models are largely built. If what remains is data, then perhaps it is time our posture toward it moved, too — from cleaning toward the clinic.
