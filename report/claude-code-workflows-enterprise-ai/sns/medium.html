Every Company Is a Graph of Algorithms — and an Engine Just Arrived

What Claude Code Dynamic Workflows, Bun's 960K-line port, and McKinsey's 88%/5.5% gap have in common.

Miessler's Thesis, Two Years Later

In 2024, security researcher Daniel Miessler wrote: "Every company is a graph of algorithms, and most employees are simply nodes executing those algorithms." The line sat with many of us as a metaphor. Two years later, it reads less like a metaphor and more like a description of what's in front of us.

On May 28, 2026, Anthropic released Claude Code Dynamic Workflows. The system orchestrates up to 1,000 subagents in a single run, cross-validates results through adversarial verification, and resumes long-running tasks from checkpoints. The combined capability is a workflow execution engine that operates at a scale unreachable by single-agent loops.

The Bun Proof Point

The empirical proof arrived almost immediately. Bun's team used Dynamic Workflows to port 960,000 lines of Zig code to Rust in 6 days, with 99.8% of tests passing. Imagine what that task looks like by hand: a multi-week or multi-month project across many engineers, with constant context-switching and a long tail of test failures.

What disappeared in 6 days is not a "feature" of a single agent. It is a unit of work that used to live inside a graph of human handoffs.

Skills Matter More Than Models

If the Bun result tells one half of the story, SkillOpt (Microsoft Research, 2026) tells the other. Across 52 (model, benchmark, harness) configurations, optimizing only the natural-language skill documents — without touching model weights — delivered improvements of +19 to +25 percentage points.

This is direct empirical support for Miessler's thesis. The enterprise value sits in the quality of the algorithm — the SOP, the workflow, the playbook — not in the model running underneath it.

The 88% / 5.5% Gap

The tools work. The proofs are in. But adoption is not the same as outcome. McKinsey reports that of companies that have adopted AI (88%), only 5.5% report meaningful financial impact. That is the gap between "we use it" and "it pays."

What sits in that gap?

  • SOP mapping — How explicit and stable are the business processes that the agents are supposed to execute?
  • Data pipeline quality — At each node of the workflow graph, how clean, complete, fresh, and provenance-traced is the data flowing through?
  • Human supervision design — Where do humans verify, intervene, override?
  • Cost structure — Subagent orchestration consumes significantly more tokens. What's the unit ROI?

The second item is where this entire piece points. Gartner estimates the cost of neglected data quality at about $12.9M per enterprise per year. That number existed before Dynamic Workflows. Now it has a much sharper meaning: it's the friction inside every node of the algorithmic graph.

The Engine Needs Diagnosed Fuel

Dynamic Workflows is the engine that runs business operations as a graph of algorithms. But an engine operates as accurately as the quality of its fuel.

"AI adoption pushes individual utility and collective breadth in opposite directions" — the line from a recent Nature study reappears here in a different form. Adoption is 88%, but impact is 5.5%. The variable in between is the quality of the data that flows through.

Where to Look Next

This shift will not be tidy. The next year will surface harder questions:

  • Which dimensions of data quality matter most for raising SOP-Bench's current 27–48% success rates in real workflows?
  • Can adversarial verification and automated data-quality auditing be combined into a single error-suppression layer?
  • How does the 96% of "non-creative" work get redistributed when agents handle it? What becomes the actual job description of the remaining humans?

One thing is already clear. The graph view of the enterprise has stopped being a metaphor. The engine has arrived. The fuel is the next conversation.
