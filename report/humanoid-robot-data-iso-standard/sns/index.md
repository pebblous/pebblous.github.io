## LinkedIn (KO)

휴머노이드 로봇은 2025년 한 해 약 1만 3,000대가 출하됐고, 2030년이면 25만 대를 넘어선다는 전망이 나온다. 그런데 로봇의 대수는 폭증하는데, 로봇의 경험은 그 속도로 쌓이지 않는다.

2026년 6월, 휴머노이드 로봇 데이터셋의 첫 국제표준 초안(ISO/WD 26264-1)이 공개됐다. 텍스트와 이미지 데이터의 품질을 측정 가능하게 만든 ISO 5259의 골격을, 로봇 데이터로 확장한 표준이다.

로봇 데이터는 센서 로그가 아니라 몸체·동작·장면·실행 흔적·결과가 맺는 관계다. 좌표계와 캘리브레이션, 동기화가 기록되지 않으면 같은 동작도 다른 로봇에게는 전혀 다른 신호가 된다. 카메라와 IMU의 타이밍이 40밀리초만 어긋나도 위치 추정이 10미터까지 벌어진다.

그래서 트라젝토리를 100만 개 모아도, 포맷이 제각각이면 100만 개의 고립된 기록일 뿐이다. 실세계 데이터는 시뮬레이션보다 82배 비싸서, 재사용하지 못한 데이터의 비용은 고스란히 되풀이된다.

다음 Physical AI의 병목은 더 큰 모델도, 더 좋은 액추에이터도 아니다. 표준이 없어 누적되지 않는 데이터다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

About 13,000 humanoid robots shipped in 2025. By 2030 the figure is projected to pass 250,000. Yet the robots are multiplying far faster than their experience accumulates.

In June 2026, the first international draft standard for humanoid-robot datasets — ISO/WD 26264-1 — went public. It extends ISO 5259, the standard that made text and image data quality measurable, into the world of robot data.

Robot data is not a sensor log. It is a relationship between a robot's body, its action, the scene, the execution trace, and the outcome. When coordinate frames, calibration, and synchronization go unrecorded, the same motion becomes a different signal on a different robot. A 40-millisecond camera–IMU timing offset alone can push position estimates off by up to 10 meters.

So a million trajectories collected in mismatched formats are still a million isolated records. And because real-world data costs roughly 82 times more than simulation, the price of data you cannot reuse simply repeats.

The next bottleneck in Physical AI is not a bigger model or a better actuator. It is data that never accumulates because nothing standard holds it together.

## Facebook (EN)

The robots keep arriving. I keep wondering where everything they go through actually ends up.

More than 13,000 humanoid robots shipped last year. By 2030 the number is supposed to be twenty times larger.

And yet the motion data one team gathers over months rarely reaches the robot next door, or even reaches the same robot six months later.

Ken Goldberg, at UC Berkeley, puts the gap in orders of magnitude. The text a language model reads adds up to something like 100,000 years of human experience. The largest robot dataset ever assembled holds barely a year.

The reason is that robot data isn't a simple recording. Inside the half-second of an arm closing on a cup, the robot's body, the scene it saw, the force in that instant, and whether it succeeded are all bound together. Without a note of where the coordinate origin sat or when the sensors were last calibrated, the same motion becomes a different story on a different robot.

In June 2026, a draft tried for the first time to write this condition into an international standard (ISO/WD 26264-1). It is the moment ISO 5259, which has measured the quality of text data, crosses over into the body of a robot. The work Pebblous has done diagnosing text and image data stands on the same question.

Maybe "data accumulates" and "experience accumulates" were never the same sentence.

What do we have to leave behind so that, six months from now, today's data is still worth reusing?
