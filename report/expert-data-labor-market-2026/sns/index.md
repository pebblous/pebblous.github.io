## LinkedIn (EN)

AI swallowed the easy labels. What remains is a layer of PhD-level experts billing $50 to $200 an hour.

Vendors now automate roughly 80% of repetitive labeling, from bounding boxes to simple classification. Humans are left with the hard 20%, and the labor market has split in two: a commodity tier at a few dollars an hour and an expert tier at two hundred. The segment that still requires human judgment is growing about ten points faster than the market overall.

The sharper shift is that finding and vetting those scarce experts is now being handed to AI as well. Mercor screens candidates through a 20-minute AI interview; Handshake AI sources from a verified identity graph built over twelve years. The axis of competition has moved from "how many labels you can produce" to "how fast you can find the right person."

The problem comes next. If an AI filter chooses the people, that filter's bias is imprinted on the labeler pool, then the labels, then the training data itself. Who verifies the experts who verify the labels?

As the race to secure talent accelerates, the question of who ultimately guarantees data quality only grows blurrier.

## Facebook (EN)

For a while, data labeling was work anyone could do. Drawing a box around an object in a photo, tagging a sentence. Quiet, repetitive.

Machines do most of that now. Vendors automate roughly four-fifths of it, and people are left only with the hard cases the machine can't settle.

So did the people simply disappear?

No. They moved up. What remains belongs to PhD-level experts billing $50 to $200 an hour, no longer attaching labels but building the environments a model learns inside.

The strange part arrived quietly, one layer later. These scarce experts are now chosen by AI too. A twenty-minute video interview, or twelve years of verified identity data, decides who gets in.

And no one checks what the machine misses. "Who verifies the experts who verify the labels?" The market keeps accelerating, yet the person who ultimately vouches for the data grows harder to name.

That gap is why we keep watching this market. The moment quality rests on one person's reputation, the way back to check it disappears.
