## LinkedIn (EN)

Starting September 11, the first person to answer for a data breach in Korea is no longer the security lead. It is the CEO.

Korea's amended privacy law moves final responsibility for data safeguards up to the executive level and raises the penalty ceiling to 10% of total revenue, surpassing the GDPR's 4% and the EU AI Act's 7%. Once a fine is measured against the whole company's top line rather than one team's budget, it lands on the boardroom agenda by default.

But the same law does more than punish. It opens a lawful path for training AI on pseudonymized data, sorts generative AI adoption into risk tiers, and funds public infrastructure for safe analysis. The price is a new burden: proving where your data came from and how it was processed.

That redefines what AI-Ready data means. Well-cleaned data that cannot prove its right to be used is no longer an asset but a latent liability. Lawfulness has become the sixth axis of data quality.

## Facebook (EN)

When a report says data has leaked, the person called into the room first has, for years, been the head of security.

The executives read the apology. The weight of it settled somewhere down the line, among the people who handle the systems.

From September 11, that arrangement shifts. Korea's amended privacy law begins to treat a data incident not as one person's mistake but as a failure of governance. Once a fine is measured against a company's entire revenue, the number stops belonging to a single department's budget and starts belonging to the whole business.

What I find more interesting is that the law does not stop at punishment. It opens a lawful route for training AI on pseudonymized data, it funds public infrastructure for safe analysis, and it divides responsibility by how a company adopts generative AI. The message is less "do not use it" and more "use it this way, and it is lawful."

In return, it asks one quiet question. Do you have the right to use this data? And can you prove it?

Data can be clean and well-ordered and still fail to say where it came from or whose consent it rests on. I am not sure we can keep calling that an asset. After September, data quality seems to be widening into something that includes the relationship between the data and the world it was drawn from.
