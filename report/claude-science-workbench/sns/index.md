Anthropic이 어제 내놓은 건 새 모델이 아니었다. 앱 하나였다.

Claude Science. 기존 Claude 모델 위에 문헌 검색, 데이터베이스, 코드 실행, HPC 접속, 재현성 기록을 얹은 연구 워크벤치다. 더 똑똑한 챗봇을 겨눈 게 아니라, 방향을 "검증 가능한 과학" 쪽으로 튼 제품이다.

핵심은 provenance다. 그림과 표마다 그것을 만든 코드와 실행 환경, 대화 기록이 함께 남는다. 별도의 리뷰어 에이전트가 잘못된 인용과 코드-그림 불일치를 출판 전에 잡아낸다. 이유는 분명하다. 연구자 70% 이상이 남의 실험을 재현하지 못한 경험이 있고, LLM이 만든 가짜 인용이 생의학 문헌을 오염시키는 중이다.

다만 provenance가 재현성을 보장하지는 않는다. 결과를 추적 가능하게 만들 뿐, 최종 검증은 여전히 연구자 몫이다.

과학 AI의 승부처가 답의 속도에서 답의 근거로 옮겨가고 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Anthropic's newest release isn't a model. It's an app.

Claude Science, out this week, wraps literature search, databases, code execution, HPC access and reproducibility logging around the same Claude models scientists already use. The bet is not benchmark supremacy but verifiable science.

At its core is provenance: every figure and table carries the exact code, runtime and conversation that produced it, while a separate reviewer agent flags miscited references and figures that no longer match their code before anything is published. The problem is real. More than 70% of researchers have failed to reproduce an experiment, and LLM-fabricated citations are now seeping into the biomedical literature.

The caveat matters. Provenance makes results traceable, not guaranteed. Final verification still rests with the researcher.

The contest in scientific AI is shifting from the speed of an answer to the evidence behind it.

## Facebook (EN)

"Could I rebuild the figure I made a few months ago, right now, from scratch?"

More researchers stall on that question than you'd expect. In a Nature survey, seven out of ten said they had failed to reproduce an experiment — sometimes their own.

That's the exact spot where Anthropic's Claude Science, released yesterday, caught my attention.

It isn't a smarter chatbot. It's a research workbench layered over the Claude models scientists already use, tying together literature, databases, code, HPC, and a record of how each result came to be.

What stayed with me was provenance. Every figure and table keeps the code, the runtime, and the conversation that produced it, so months later you can still ask, "what was this actually made from?" A reviewer agent sits alongside it, flagging miscited references and figures that no longer match their code before anything goes out.

It doesn't guarantee reproducibility. It only makes results traceable — the final judgment still belongs to a person. I found that modest line more reassuring than any benchmark claim.

It also brought back a sentence we've held onto for a long time at Pebblous: data over models. Attaching lineage to a result and filtering out numbers that look right but aren't is, in the end, the same problem we've worked on with DataClinic.

As the race to generate discoveries faster heats up, the real bottleneck seems to move elsewhere — not to speed, but to whether we can trust the discovery again. Will the results you made today still explain themselves months from now?
