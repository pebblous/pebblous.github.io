2025년 9월 나오고 12월 1.0이 확정된 RSL(Really Simple Licensing)은 그 한 마디를 "이 조건이면 들어와"로 바꾼다. RSS 계보의 공동창시자가 주도한 오픈 XML 표준으로, 라이선스를 robots.txt와 HTTP 헤더, HTML, RSS, 파일 메타데이터에 직접 새기고 크롤·학습·추론을 구분해 과금 모델까지 선언한다. 미디어 1,500곳 이상이 지지를 표했다.

그런데 이 표준이 푸는 건 쉬운 절반이다. 크롤에 과금하는 일은 네트워크에서 관측 가능한 단일 HTTP 이벤트라 CDN이 문 앞에서 청구할 수 있다. 어려운 절반은 그 데이터가 어떤 답을 만드는 데 쓰였는지에 과금하는 일이고, 이건 모델 내부에서 "어떤 학습 문서가 이 출력에 기여했나"를 귀속할 수 있어야 성립한다.

문제는 출발선에 있다. 주요 학습 데이터셋의 70% 이상이 라이선스 정보를 잃은 채 모델로 흘러들어, 추적은 시작점부터 끊겨 있다. 공식 준수를 약속한 메이저 AI 기업은 아직 한 곳도 없고, 그사이 15억 달러짜리 합의가 이 격차의 값을 못 박았다.

라이선스를 메타데이터로 새기는 건 쉽다. 그 권리가 추론까지 따라가게 만드는 건 다른 층위의 일이고, 그 빈 계층이 데이터 인프라의 진짜 질문이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

For years, a content owner could say only one thing to an AI crawler: "Stay out."

RSL (Really Simple Licensing), announced in September 2025 and finalized at version 1.0 in December, turns that one word into "Come in, on these terms." Led by a co-creator of RSS, the open XML standard carves a license directly into robots.txt, HTTP headers, HTML, RSS, and file metadata, separating crawling, training, and inference so each can carry its own price. More than 1,500 publishers have voiced support.

But the standard solves the easy half. Charging for a crawl is a single observable HTTP event a CDN can meter at the door. The hard half is charging for how that data shaped an answer, and that requires attributing inside the model which training documents fed a given output.

The break is at the starting line. Over 70% of major training datasets reach models with their license information stripped, so the trail is severed before it begins. Not one major AI company has formally committed to honoring the terms, and a $1.5B settlement has already put a price on the gap.

Declaring a license is easy. Making that right follow the data into inference is a different layer of work, and that empty layer is the real infrastructure question.

## Facebook (EN)

"Stay out."

For a long time, that was the only thing a website could say to an AI crawler.

The Disallow line in robots.txt can refuse, but it cannot set a condition.

This winter, a standard meant to turn that one line into "come in, on these terms" reached version 1.0. Its name is RSL. More than 1,500 publishers have stood behind it.

At first it read like copyright news. The longer I looked, the more it became a different question. Declaring a right and making that right follow the data are not the same act.

Blocking a crawl is easy. You count one request passing the door.

But to trace where a piece of writing ended up, you have to look inside the model and ask which data fed a given output.

"Did my words help make this sentence?"

Today's AI has no settled way to answer that.

And the trail is cut at the start. Over 70% of major training datasets reach models with their license stripped away. The right that was meant to follow the data has already lost the road it would travel.

This is why Pebblous kept returning to the report. Beneath the layer that inscribes a license, the layer that makes data traceable from origin to use is still empty. At the center of that empty layer sits one variable: data quality.

A declaration ends in a single line of metadata. Making that promise walk all the way into inference is only beginning.
