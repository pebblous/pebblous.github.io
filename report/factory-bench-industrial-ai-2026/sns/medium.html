Factory AI vs. Real Machines: What FactoryBench Reveals About LLM Limits

A new benchmark quantifies exactly how poorly today's best AI systems understand industrial sensor data — and why the fix isn't a bigger model.

The Benchmark Nobody Was Running

Ask a frontier LLM to summarize a maintenance manual, and it does so fluently. Ask it to diagnose an anomaly from 125Hz torque readings on a KUKA industrial arm, and something breaks down. Practitioners in manufacturing AI have known this anecdotally for years. What they lacked was a number.

FactoryBench (arXiv:2605.07675), published in May 2026, provides that number — and the number is striking. Researchers built the first benchmark specifically designed to evaluate LLMs on industrial robot telemetry, using Judea Pearl's four-level Causation Ladder as the evaluation framework:

  • L1 — State Recognition: Can the model identify what is happening right now?
  • L2 — Intervention: Can it predict what would happen if an action were taken?
  • L3 — Counterfactual: Can it reason about what would have happened under different conditions?
  • L4 — Decision-Making: Can it generate an actionable maintenance or optimization plan?

The dataset draws from three sources: FactoryWave (8,983 episodes from a UR3 cobot and KUKA KR10), AURSAD (4,094 episodes), and voraus-AD (2,122 episodes). Total: approximately 71,000 Q&A pairs, 100+ sensor channels, 27 systematically injected fault types.

The Scoreboard

Six LLMs were evaluated in zero-shot setting: Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-4o, GPT-5.1, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Llama 4 Maverick, and DeepSeek-V3. The results:

  • Claude Sonnet 4.6 led L1-L3: L1 46.8%, L2 47.1%, L3 45.9%
  • Every single score across all six models fell below 50% in L1-L3
  • GPT-5.1 ranked 4th in L1-L3 but 1st in L4 at 17.7% — the best L4 score overall
  • The non-LLM baseline (random/heuristic) sat at 28.4%

The L4 ceiling of 17.7% is what it looks like: on a task with four choices, the best model in the world performs barely above random guessing when asked to make an actual maintenance decision from sensor data.

The Protocol-Signal Dissociation

The most structurally important finding is GPT-5.1's split ranking. It underperformed in structured reasoning (L1-L3) yet outperformed in decision-making (L4). This is not noise — it reflects a genuine decoupling of two distinct capabilities:

  • Protocol retrieval: Finding the right procedure from a knowledge base
  • Signal understanding: Interpreting what high-dimensional sensor time-series data actually means

Current LLMs were trained to retrieve and summarize text. They were not trained on 125Hz industrial telemetry streams. The benchmark quantifies the gap between these two competencies for the first time.

The Data-Centric Evidence

If model architecture isn't the bottleneck, what is? FactoryBench's findings gain additional weight when placed alongside recent VLM experiments:

  • Florence-2 (0.23B) vs GPT-4o: A 0.23-billion parameter open-source model, fine-tuned on just 400 domain-specific microchip blueprint samples, outperformed GPT-4o by +52.4% F1 and reduced hallucinations by 43.15%
  • NVIDIA Cosmos VLM: The same model scored 14.37% zero-shot on wafer defect classification. After fine-tuning on domain data: 96.8%. An 82.4 percentage-point gap, closed entirely by data quality
  • Zha et al. (2024), ACM Computing Surveys: Data optimization alone — without model changes — yielded 2.3–6.8% accuracy gains on standard benchmarks. In industrial domains, the effect is likely larger

The pattern is consistent across modalities (time-series, image), model sizes (0.23B to GPT-4o), and domains (semiconductor, robotics, safety). The bottleneck is domain data, not model architecture.

Pilot Purgatory: A Market Problem With a Technical Cause

The manufacturing AI market is projected to grow from $34.18B (2025) to $155.04B (2030), CAGR 35.3%. But Rockwell reports that 95% of manufacturers are investing in AI while only 10% reach production integration. BCG found 60% of companies failing to create measurable value from AI. Deloitte's 2025 Smart Manufacturing Survey: 47% of industrial leaders cite data quality as the core cause of project failures.

FactoryBench provides the technical underpinning for this ROI gap. The reason pilots stall isn't integration complexity or model selection — it's that the sensor data feeding these systems hasn't been made AI-ready. Models that score 85%+ on general benchmarks collapse to the 30–50% range when confronted with real industrial data.

What This Means in Practice

For teams deploying AI in manufacturing environments, FactoryBench suggests a reordering of priorities:

  • Audit sensor data quality before model selection. Florence-2 beating GPT-4o with 400 samples means your data strategy matters more than your model procurement budget
  • Distinguish protocol retrieval from signal understanding. An LLM that writes excellent maintenance reports may still fail at anomaly detection from raw telemetry — these are not the same task
  • Use the Causation Ladder as a requirements framework. L1 and L4 require different data, different fine-tuning strategies, and different success metrics

The 82.4pp gap that separates zero-shot Cosmos from fine-tuned Cosmos is not a gap that a better foundation model closes. It is a gap that domain-specific, high-quality labeled data closes.
