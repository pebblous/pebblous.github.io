AlphaFold Folded Proteins. The Next Leap Isn't a Bigger Model.

In 2021, AlphaFold2 essentially solved how a protein folds. Feed it an amino-acid sequence and it predicts the three-dimensional shape with near-experimental accuracy. It felt like an ending.

It was a beginning. The center of gravity in protein science has been shifting from prediction to generation. Not "how does this sequence fold," but "build me a protein that does this job." That second question runs in the opposite direction, and it changes everything about how you measure success.

From reading structures to writing them

Prediction is reverse-engineering: the answer already exists in nature, encoded over billions of years of evolution, and the model recovers it. De novo design has no answer key. You specify a function first ("bind this target") and the model generates a sequence and structure that never existed before. The only way to know whether it worked is to synthesize the molecule and run the experiment.

That single fact is why a data loop stops being optional here. If there's no label until you measure it, then performance depends as much on how fast and how accurately you can measure as on how clever the model is.

Success rates became something you can plan

The clearest evidence that protein design turned into engineering is the hit rate, the share of synthesized designs that actually do their job. Traditional Rosetta-based design typically landed under 1%, and for hard targets like antibodies, below 0.1%. Then the curve bent:

  • RFdiffusion (2023) reached roughly 19% in vitro, a two-orders-of-magnitude jump.
  • AlphaProteo (2024) reported anywhere from single digits to 88%, depending on the target.
  • BindCraft (2025) reported averages in the 30 to 50% range.

The practical consequence: instead of screening hundreds of thousands of candidates, teams now put fewer than 100 designs per target on the bench and still find working molecules. Making fewer while hitting more often, that's what engineering looks like.

A caveat belongs right here. These numbers swing hard by target, and in vitro results are not the same thing as in silico estimates. The honest read is a range with conditions, not a single triumphant figure.

The lever was data, not scale

So what produced the leap? Mainstream coverage tends to answer "bigger generative models." The data tells a different story.

BindCraft is the cleanest counterexample. It trained no new model. It reused AlphaFold2's existing weights and added a step that refolds each design to filter it, and that pipeline change alone delivered more than a 10x improvement in hit rate. The model size stayed the same. What changed was how the data was handled.

The evidence runs the other way too. In protein fitness prediction, performance degrades once you push past roughly 3 billion parameters. Work on protein language models has found that careful attention to data quality reaches top performance with models far smaller and cheaper than the assumption "scale equals performance" would predict.

The answers are scarce, and skewed

If data is the lever, is there enough of it? No. There are about 240,000 experimentally verified structures in the Protein Data Bank, against more than 200 million predicted by AlphaFold. The raw material exploded by nearly 870x, while the verified ground truth stayed at roughly one part in 870.

Quantity isn't even the worst of it. The PDB is biased toward crystallizable, globular proteins, so membrane and disordered proteins are underrepresented. More damaging still: there's almost no negative data. The database holds successes, not the binders that failed, so models never learn what doesn't work. And reusing predicted structures as training labels propagates a model's artifacts into the next model. These are exactly the failure modes (label error, class imbalance, measurement bias) that we have spent years naming in classification data. Garbage in, garbage out holds for proteins too.

What's left for the human

The lever that pushes hit rates higher from here isn't a bigger model either. Researchers point to retraining and alignment on lab-generated experimental data. Models of every size improve when they are aligned on measured results, and the largest models gain the most from that alignment. Scaling parameters only pays off when a measured-data loop sits on top of it.

That reshapes the scientist's job rather than removing it. The more AI floods the bench with hypotheses and designs, the more value moves to deciding what to measure first, which data to trust, and how to record failure. Prioritizing targets, standardizing measurement protocols, labeling the negatives instead of discarding them, all of it is data curation.

So "AI does science" is only half true. AI generates hypotheses quickly, but turning them into discoveries still needs someone who decides what to measure and what to believe. As design gets automated, the human job converges on guarding the data quality that sets the speed of discovery itself.
