## LinkedIn (EN)

Anthropic told regulators that a rival had run "the largest known distillation attack." The same Anthropic recently agreed to pay $1.5 billion for the roughly 482,000 books it scraped without permission — about $3,000 a title.

Columnist Will Lockett called the whole episode "profoundly stupid." But the hypocrisy is only the surface. Peel back one layer and a sharper question appears: why is distillation so cheap in the first place?

The answer is simple. Distillation is cheap not because the algorithm is clever, but because it free-rides on the diversity and curation of the source data the teacher model absorbed. The student never sees a single line of the original corpus. It copies the statistical shadow that data left behind.

Copy only the shadow, though, and the model eventually breaks. A 2024 Nature study found that training on distilled and synthetic data across generations, with no fresh source data, collapses the model in about nine generations. The only fix that held was keeping 10% real original data in every generation.

Seen through that lens, the sovereign-AI race looks different. Roughly 59% of global sovereign-AI budgets go to compute infrastructure and just 7% to national data initiatives. You can stack GPUs indefinitely, but without a pipeline of licensed source data you end up distilling someone else's frontier model to catch up. Data sovereignty is AI sovereignty.

### You Can't Buy Sovereignty by Distilling It

Anthropic recently told U.S. regulators that a rival had carried out "the largest known distillation attack" — some 28.8 million conversations across roughly 25,000 accounts. It was a striking accusation. It was also made by a company that, weeks earlier, had agreed to pay $1.5 billion to settle claims that it trained on hundreds of thousands of books scraped without permission — about $3,000 a title.

The tech columnist Will Lockett called the episode "profoundly stupid," and he had a point. When you brand a rival's use of a standard, widely used technique an "attack" while your own unlicensed data collection is framed as innovation, the yardstick stops making sense.

But the hypocrisy is the surface of this story, not its center. Peel back one layer and a more useful question appears: why is distillation so cheap in the first place — and what does that tell us about the global race for sovereign AI?

**What distillation actually copies**

The mechanics are modest. You query a large "teacher" model and train a smaller "student" to imitate not just its answers but its probability distributions — the subtle weighting behind each next word. That richer signal is why a little teacher output can produce a surprisingly capable student. DeepSeek's R1-Distill-Qwen-32B, for instance, scored 94.3% on MATH-500, closing much of the gap to far larger frontier models.

Here is the part the benchmarks hide. A teacher's output is a compressed image of the data distribution it learned from. The student never touches the original corpus. It cheaply inherits the statistical shadow that data left behind — and, crucially, it does not inherit the hundreds of millions of dollars the teacher's owner spent collecting and curating that data. That is the free ride.

**What distillation cannot copy**

Copy the shadow, and something goes missing: the tail of the distribution — the rare but decisive edge cases, the robustness of alignment, the thin slices of domain knowledge that rarely surface in sampled outputs.

A 2024 study in Nature measured what happens when this loss compounds. Retrain a model on purely synthetic data across generations and its perplexity drifts from 34 into the 50s; in about nine generations the output degenerates into meaningless repetition. The rare cases vanish first, because they were scarce to begin with, and the model collapses toward the average. The one intervention that held the line was keeping roughly 10% real, original data in every generation. That 10% is, in effect, the price of the source.

**The cost of legality**

Original data now carries a visible invoice. In Bartz v. Anthropic, the company agreed to pay more than $1.5 billion over some 482,000 works obtained from pirate libraries. Judge Alsup's logic mattered as much as the number: training a model on copyrighted works may be fair use, but acquiring and storing pirated copies is infringement. The problem was never what you trained on — it was where you got it.

The market has already noticed. Stanford's AI Index reports that the share of restricted tokens in the common C4 crawl jumped from 5–7% in 2023 to 20–33% in 2024. Meanwhile real licensing deals have set prices — News Corp–OpenAI at $250 million over five years, Reddit–Google near $60 million a year. The open web is quietly closing, and legitimate source data is being priced in real time.

**The sovereign-AI dilemma**

Zoom out to the level of nations and the bottleneck sharpens. Most countries pursuing sovereign AI pour money into the same place: GPUs and foundation models. By one CNAS measure, roughly 59% of sovereign-AI budgets go to compute infrastructure and just 7% to national data initiatives.

This produces a paradox. To be sovereign you need high-quality domestic data; lacking it, you end up distilling a global frontier model to keep pace. A goal like "reach 95% of frontier performance" is, in practice, a commitment to follow — and to keep distilling. Epoch AI has argued that high-quality language data is the higher-order bottleneck: once it concentrates in a few hands, adding compute yields less and less.

You can distill your way to the frontier quickly. But the place you arrive is always inside the shadow of a distribution someone else built. GPU subsidies cannot buy sovereignty. A sovereign-AI program that leans only on distillation, with no source data of its own, declares independence while remaining dependent on another country's data.

**Where this leaves us**

One chain runs from start to finish. The hypocrisy debate reduces to a question of data provenance; provenance leads to the value distillation cannot carry; that value hardens into a price in the licensing market; and whether a nation can afford that price decides its AI future. Real sovereignty comes not from the number of GPUs but from the lineage of the data.

This is the axis Pebblous works on — diagnosing the quality and lineage of AI training data, and building the AI-Ready data infrastructure that keeps a model's source pipeline healthy over time. The Nature finding — that a durable source-data pipeline is a necessary condition for model health — is not an abstraction for us. It is the market.
