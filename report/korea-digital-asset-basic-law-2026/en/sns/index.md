## LinkedIn

Korea just introduced the most granular digital asset regulation in the world — 12 business domains, each with its own licensing tier.

Most coverage has focused on the investor protection angle. That matters. But the more consequential shift is buried in Chapter 5: disclosure requirements for digital asset issuers. When data is tokenized and becomes a tradable asset, those disclosure obligations turn data quality into a legal requirement, not just a best practice.

Why does that change things? Because today, there is no standardized way to certify a dataset's provenance, distribution, or fitness for purpose before it hits a trading venue. The bill creates legal demand for exactly that infrastructure. The academic parallel is striking: just as real estate transactions require licensed appraisers, tokenized data markets will need a class of "Data Asset Appraisers." That role does not exist yet in any formal regulatory framework — Korea or otherwise.

The AI agent angle is equally consequential. Of the 12 business domains, agents can technically participate in eight or more — from advisory and order routing (notification-tier entry) through to discretionary management. None of the four major jurisdictions (Korea, EU, US, Japan) has written explicit rules for autonomous agent participation. That gap is both a legal risk and a first-mover opportunity for whoever closes it first.

Korea's Digital Asset Basic Act introduces 12 regulated business domains — the most detailed classification of any major jurisdiction. The EU MiCA covers 8 CASP types. The US FIT21 uses a dual SEC/CFTC structure. Korea draws finer lines.

That granularity matters for a specific reason. Advisory, order routing, and quasi-advisory services sit at the notification tier — the easiest entry point. That is precisely where AI agents are most likely to participate first. By early 2026, more than 550 AI agent crypto projects had a combined market cap of $4.3 billion. When data gets tokenized and traded, those requirements create legal demand for third-party quality certification. The analogy is real estate: you cannot transact without an appraisal. For data assets, quality metrics become the equivalent of property records — the sole objective evidence of value. Tokenization alone does not create liquidity; certification does.
