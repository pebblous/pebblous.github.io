## LinkedIn (EN)

AI predicted 2.2 million stable new materials. Independent labs have so far synthesized and confirmed 736 of them.

The real shift AI brought to science over the past five years isn't speed. AI has moved into the first step of discovery: deciding what question to ask. In 2021 DeepMind surfaced a connection across 243,000 knots that mathematicians had missed, and in math, AI moved from a 2024 silver-medal score at the IMO to an officially certified gold in 2025.

Still, a gap of three or four orders of magnitude always sits between prediction and verification. The most common failure in AI materials discovery is mistaking a disordered phase for an ordered one, and its root is not the model but bias baked into the training data.

The trust of a discovery ultimately comes from the quality, coverage, and traceability of the data behind it. The boundary of the data is the boundary of the questions AI can ask.

## Facebook (EN)

'AI discovers 2.2 million new materials.'

When a headline like that scrolls past, my eye lingers less on the 2.2 million than on the word 'discovers.'

The predicted count was real enough: 2.2 million stable structures. But the number that outside labs actually synthesized and confirmed, in the same period, was 736. Prediction and discovery differ by a single word, and by four orders of magnitude in between.

What AI truly changed in science over the past five years was not the speed of its answers. It moved into the first step: deciding what to ask. When DeepMind pointed, in 2021, to a connection across 243,000 knots that mathematicians had overlooked, the model didn't hand over an answer. It said where to look.

So one question stays with me. As AI asks more and more of the questions, how do we check which data its questions were born inside of?

The most common mistake in AI materials discovery, it turns out, is reading a disordered phase as an ordered one. Its root lies not in the model but in a bias inherited from the data it learned. That the trust of a discovery comes, in the end, from the quality of its data. That is what the two numbers, 2.2 million and 736, seem to say, quietly.
