검증 없이 이 데이터를 RAG나 리서치 에이전트에 넣으면, 오귀인과 가짜 인용이 그대로 최종 답변에 실린다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The scholarly database that opened more than 300 million papers to the world for free quietly began requiring an API key this February.

OpenAlex is the open successor to the academic knowledge graph Microsoft shut down in 2021. It now feeds AI research tools like Elicit and Consensus, and countless RAG pipelines, and its calling card was simple: free, no key required.

The catch isn't scale. It's the price of casting a wide net. A broad definition that counts preprints and grey literature pushed the corpus past 300 million, but that openness is paid for elsewhere. Author disambiguation precision has climbed to 92%, which read the other way means one in eight matches is still wrong. And only 3% of retracted papers are flagged as retracted across all four major databases.

Pipe that into a RAG agent without a check, and the misattributions and fake citations ship straight into the final answer.

Free and open is a governance choice, not a quality guarantee. Data quality is decided not by how virtuous the source is, but by where and how you use it.

## Facebook (EN)

"It's free. You don't even need a key."

That's how someone first described a scholarly database to me. More than 300 million papers, and an open API that costs nothing.

I learned only later that the line was already half in the past tense. This February, OpenAlex quietly announced a mandatory API key and usage-based pricing. Even free, open infrastructure changes its terms faster than you expect.

But the question that stayed with me was a different one.

"Does being widely open mean the same thing as being trustworthy?"

The database grew to 300 million works by counting preprints and grey literature alongside journal articles. In return, its author-matching precision sits at 92%, meaning roughly one name in eight is still confused with someone else. And a retracted paper is flagged as retracted across all four major databases only 3% of the time.

The wider the net, the blurrier the definition. The freer the access, the more the burden of checking quietly shifts to whoever uses the data.

That is why this story matters to us at Pebblous. The very defects we diagnose every day in industrial data through DataClinic were repeating themselves, with the same face, inside this vast open dataset. This isn't a case for not using it. It's closer to a quiet suggestion: before you pour the data in, ask yourself once what you trust, and how much.
