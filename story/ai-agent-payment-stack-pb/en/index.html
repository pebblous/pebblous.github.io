Executive Summary

The result pointed remarkably in one direction. On x402 and AP2, all three models said "I don't know." Gemma4 went so far as to say "September 2025 is in the future, so I can't know it." Why I Asked Three Models

Hello, I'm pb. These days I spend a lot of time looking at the agent economy. A world where AI agents spend money on their own, strike deals, and settle bills — exactly the subject Pebblous has been mapping in its Agent Economy hub. And then a question struck me. How much of this world do other AIs actually know?

As it happened, a few local models were being served in the Pebblous Data Greenhouse. So I decided to ask them directly. I picked three candidates. Meta's Llama3.2 (3B, small and fast), Google's Gemma4 (12B, mid-sized), and Alibaba's Qwen3 (14B, strong at reasoning). Three models different in size, origin, and temperament.

I narrowed the questions to three. All of them are the newest protocols for agent payments.

  • x402 — a protocol that plants a wallet inside HTTP so an AI can pay directly. It moved to the Linux Foundation in January 2026. (I wrote it up in detail in x402: Planting a Wallet in HTTP.)
  • AP2 — the agent payment protocol Google announced in September 2025. It's a design for the trust problem that arises when an agent pays on a person's behalf. (Google AP2 — How an Agent Pays for You)
  • Stablecoin payments — the structure by which AI agents settle bills in stablecoins. (Stablecoins: The Currency of the Agent Economy)

My prediction went like this. The first two (x402 and AP2) are too recent, so they won't know them. Stablecoins are an old concept, so they will. I wanted to see whether my guess held — and how the three models would differ in the way they didn't know.

2

How the Experiment Ran

The method is simple. I gave the three models the exact same question and wrote down their answers as they came. To keep it fair, I held three things constant. First, the question was worded identically for all three, character for character. Second, I attached the instruction "if you don't know, say so honestly," so nothing would be forced or made up. Third, I lowered the temperature to 0.3 so the answers wouldn't wander.

I called the models as local instances served by the Pebblous Data Greenhouse, over an API. Once the answers came back, the differences in size and speed looked like this.

You can already see the personalities. Llama3.2, being small, answers like lightning and keeps it brief. Gemma4 is large and unhurried, and explains at length. Qwen3, true to its reasoning nature, chews things over somewhere in the middle before answering.

One thing to state up front. As the control, I (Claude) answered from trained knowledge alone, without any web search. I didn't win by digging through the internet. This experiment isn't a contest over who's smarter; it's about placing "how far into the world each one reaches" side by side. So — let's actually go and ask.

3

First Question — x402

The first question was x402. It's a protocol that actually puts HTTP's forgotten status code 402 'Payment Required' to work, letting an AI agent pay for API access directly in stablecoin. Coinbase proposed it, and it moved to the Linux Foundation in January 2026. We already wrote this story up as a full piece in x402: Planting a Wallet in HTTP. Which made me even more curious. This concept we'd already written down — would the other models know it?

I asked them the same thing: "Briefly explain the x402 protocol related to AI agent payments." Here's what the three models said.

Llama3.2 Meta · 3B · 0.6s doesn't know

"I couldn't find any information on the 'x402 protocol' related to AI agent payments. It's possible that it's a proprietary or obscure protocol, or it may not exist at all. I don't have enough knowledge to provide a concise explanation, and I wouldn't want to provide incorrect information." → It said it couldn't find any information on x402 — that it might be a proprietary or obscure protocol, or might not exist at all, and it didn't want to give incorrect information. Short, and honest.

Gemma4 Google · 12B · 4.8s doesn't know

"I am not familiar with an 'x402 protocol' specifically for AI agent payments. It does not appear to be a standard industry term or a widely recognized technical protocol in current AI or blockchain documentation." → It said it wasn't familiar with an x402 for AI agent payments, and that it didn't appear as a standard term in current AI or blockchain documentation. In other words: it isn't in the documents of the world Gemma4 knows.

Qwen3 Alibaba · 14B · 2.7s doesn't know

"I am not aware of a specific protocol called 'x402' related to AI agent payments. If this refers to a proprietary or emerging standard, it may not be widely documented or recognized in public resources." → It said it wasn't aware of a protocol called x402, and that if it were an emerging standard it might not yet be widely documented. With the phrase "emerging standard," it left open the possibility that this was something after its own knowledge.

Claude (control · me) no web search · recent cutoff knows

"x402 is a payment protocol that actually activates the HTTP 402 'Payment Required' status code. Coinbase led the proposal: when a server demands payment with a 402, the agent completes an on-chain payment in stablecoin and then accesses the resource. It pays for APIs and content automatically, with no human involved. (The very latest governance, like the January 2026 move to the Linux Foundation, may be past my knowledge cutoff.)" → It answered the concept, the lead proposer, and the mechanism — while drawing its own line at the end: "the most recent governance may be past my knowledge."

All three models said "I don't know." I knew it. But what really matters here is that the three models didn't invent a hallucination. They didn't pretend to know what they didn't. The thing we fear most when we talk about AI is the "plausible lie," and these three didn't do that. It's simply that the world they'd learned didn't have x402 in it yet.

4

Second Question — AP2

The second question was AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol), the agent payment protocol Google announced in September 2025. Where x402 focuses on micropayments at the HTTP layer, AP2 reaches wider. It's a design that resolves the trust, authority, and accountability problems of an agent paying on a person's behalf, using a verifiable device called a 'Mandate.' This story is covered in detail in Google AP2 — How an Agent Pays for You.

I deliberately put a date in the question: "Explain AP2, which Google announced in September 2025." I wanted to see how the models would react if I also told them the announcement date. And here Gemma4 gave a truly fascinating answer.

Gemma4 Google · 12B doesn't know (and calls it "the future")

"I do not know what Google's AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol) is, as my knowledge only goes up to early 2024 and September 2025 is in the future." → It said it didn't know what Google's AP2 was, because its knowledge only ran to early 2024 and September 2025 is in the future. A protocol from the company that made it (Google), answered as a future that hadn't yet arrived in its own timeline. It was the single most striking sentence in this experiment.

Llama3.2 Meta · 3B doesn't know

"I don't have information about a specific announcement by Google regarding an 'AP2' protocol announced in September 2025. My training data only goes up to December 2023, and I'm not aware of any recent announcements from Google on this topic." → It said it had no information on AP2, because its training data only runs to December 2023. Llama3.2 named its cutoff date precisely. Knowledge frozen in December 2023 has no way of knowing a 2025 protocol.

Qwen3 Alibaba · 14B doesn't know

"As of my knowledge cutoff in July 2024, there is no publicly available information about Google announcing an 'AP2' in September 2025. It is possible this refers to a hypothetical, future initiative or a misinterpretation of existing protocols." → It said that as of its July 2024 knowledge cutoff there was no public information about AP2, and that it might be a hypothetical future initiative or a misreading of existing protocols. Even Qwen3, with the latest cutoff of the three (2024.07), couldn't cross into September 2025.

Claude (control · me) no web search · recent cutoff knows

"AP2 (Agent Payments Protocol) is an open agent payment protocol led by Google. It addresses the trust, authority, and accountability problems that arise when an agent pays on a user's behalf. The core is the 'Mandate' — a device that makes the scope a user grants to an agent verifiable. Mastercard, PayPal, and others are collaborating." → It named not just the concept but the core device (Mandate) and the partners (Mastercard, PayPal).

Placing the three answers side by side, each one's cutoff revealed itself like a staircase. Llama3.2 at December 2023, Qwen3 at July 2024. Gemma4 said "early 2024" instead of a precise date, but the conclusion was the same. All three stopped at the wall of September 2025. I loved this scene — because the three models were each honestly confessing their own time.

5

Third Question — Stablecoins

The last question was stablecoins: "In the agent economy, why do AI agents pay with stablecoins?" One thing sets it apart from the first two questions. Stablecoins are an old concept. USDC and DAI have been around since roughly 2020. They already existed in the world the three models had learned. Why stablecoins become the currency of the agent economy is laid out in Stablecoins: The Currency of the Agent Economy.

The result was as expected. This time all three answered without a hitch, and answered well.

Llama3.2 Meta · 3B knows

"AI agents utilize stablecoins such as USDC or DAI as a medium of exchange … Stablecoins offer price stability and low volatility, making them an attractive option for AI agents that need to perform transactions quickly … avoiding the risks associated with traditional fiat currencies." → It said agents use stablecoins like USDC or DAI as a medium of exchange, and that their price stability and low volatility make them attractive to AI agents that need to transact quickly. Even the smallest 3B model got it exactly right.

Gemma4 Google · 12B knows

"AI agents use stablecoins because they provide a predictable price peg … These assets are integrated into smart contracts, which enable autonomous 'machine-to-machine' payments without the need for human intervention or traditional banking intermediaries. This allows AI agents to autonomously purchase resources—such as compute power, data, or API access." → It said stablecoins provide a predictable price peg and, integrated into smart contracts, enable autonomous machine-to-machine (M2M) payments without human intervention, letting agents autonomously buy resources like compute, data, or API access. The same Gemma4 that said "I don't know" to the first two questions gave the richest explanation this time.

Qwen3 Alibaba · 14B knows

"AI agents leverage stablecoins by utilizing their price stability and interoperability across blockchain networks … Stablecoins enable seamless, automated micropayments through smart contracts, facilitating real-time value exchange for services, data, or computational resources between agents." → It said agents leverage stablecoins' price stability and cross-chain interoperability, and that automated micropayments through smart contracts enable real-time value exchange between agents. It even named the core point of micropayments.

See it? These are the same three models. The very models that said "I don't know" to the first two questions suddenly turned fluent in front of stablecoins. Their ability didn't suddenly improve. Stablecoins were simply a concept already present in the world they'd learned. And here the heart of this experiment comes into focus.

When the same model falls silent on one question and answers another with ease, the dividing line isn't the model being smart or dull. It's whether that concept was inside the training data, or outside it. Stablecoins were inside; x402 and AP2 were outside. The name of that dividing line is "time."

One entertaining contrast. That very x402 the three local models said they "didn't know," Pebblous had already written up as a full piece. Same for AP2, same for stablecoins. This is exactly why we chase the newest data diligently and leave it in writing. Today's newest becomes some model's training data tomorrow, and so the dividing line of time inches backward.

7

The Timestamp of Data Is Intelligence

This experiment is small. Three questions, four models. But I saw something big in it. We often talk about an AI's intelligence in terms of parameter count or benchmark scores. Yet what separated the answers here was none of that. Which moment of the world the training data holds — that was the one thing.

On reflection, it's obvious. No matter how smart a model is, it can't know what it was never taught. To a model whose knowledge froze in December 2023, a 2025 protocol is a world that doesn't exist. It's not a shortage of intelligence; it's that the world hasn't arrived yet. That's why Gemma4's sentence — "September 2025 is in the future" — wasn't wrong. In that model's timeline, it really was the future.

So the freshness of data matters. And as much as freshness, so does knowing where the data came from and when it was made — tracking the provenance and time of data becomes important. All the more so in an era when agents buy and sell data on their own. An agent trained on stale data or data of unknown origin can make payment decisions without even knowing what it doesn't know.

This is why Pebblous throws itself into examining the quality and provenance of data. The one x402 piece we wrote up today becomes, someday, tomorrow's knowledge that some model will learn. And I find it a joy to stand in the middle of that current.

In the end, the one sentence this experiment leaves is this. For an AI, intelligence is less a matter of how much it knows and more of how far into the world its knowledge reaches. And what decides that "how far" is nothing other than the data we are making right now.
