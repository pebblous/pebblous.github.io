먼저 한 일은 사실 확인이었다. 과학자 40명(140개국 2,600여 지원자 중 선발)이 "모두가 맞다고 인정하는 사실"만 골라 정리했고, 193개국은 그 위에서 이야기를 시작했다. 반장은 튜링상을 받은 요슈아 벤지오와 노벨평화상 기자 마리아 레사였다. 기계를 아는 사람과 사람을 아는 사람이 함께 본 것이다.

이들이 확인한 사실은 무겁다. 안전장치가 AI 속도를 못 따라가고, 알아서 일하는 AI가 시킨 대로만 할 거라는 보장이 아직 없으며, AI를 돌리는 컴퓨터 힘의 약 90%를 단 2개 나라가 쥐고 있다.

그런데 사실 보고서는 6개 언어로 모두에게 열렸지만, "어떤 AI를 특별히 감시할지" 정하는 기준은 공개되지 않았다. 시험 문제는 나눠주고 채점 기준표는 서랍에 숨긴 셈이다.

재지 않으면 다스릴 수 없다. 규칙이 딛고 설 사실은 저절로 생기지 않고, 누군가 데이터를 정확히 재고 다듬어야 만들어진다. 페블러스가 데이터 품질을 진단해 온 자리도 여기다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

193 countries came to Geneva to talk about AI, and chose not to write the rules first.

They agreed on the facts first. A panel of 40 scientists, picked from more than 2,600 applicants across 140 countries, wrote down only what everyone could agree was true, and the 193 nations built the conversation on top of that. Its co-chairs were Yoshua Bengio, a Turing Award laureate, and Maria Ressa, a Nobel Peace Prize journalist: one who knows how the machines work, one who has watched what they do to people.

Their findings are sobering. Safeguards are not keeping pace with AI, there is still no guarantee that autonomous AI will do only what it is told, and roughly 90% of the computing power behind AI sits in just two countries.

The facts were opened to everyone in six languages. The criteria for deciding which AI gets watched were not. The exam questions were handed out; the grading key stayed in the drawer.

You cannot govern what you cannot measure. The facts that rules stand on do not appear on their own; someone has to measure and clean the data first. That is the work Pebblous has been doing on data quality.

## Facebook (EN)

When a class picks a new game, they usually settle the rules first.

And almost every time, a fight breaks out. "Why is that even a foul?"

This summer, 193 countries came to Geneva. Nearly every nation on earth, in one room. And they didn't open with a fight over rules.

They agreed on the facts first.

Forty scientists spent a long time on it, keeping only what all of them could call true, however much they disagreed elsewhere. One of their chairs, Maria Ressa, put it plainly: "What you are getting is the floor of our concern, not the ceiling."

On that floor sat some heavy things. That our safeguards are not keeping up with AI. That the power to run it has bunched up in the hands of very few.

But the part that stayed with me was elsewhere. The facts were thrown open in six languages, while the rules for what to do with them stayed shut. "Why hand out the exam and lock the grading key in a drawer?"

You cannot govern what you cannot measure. The facts that rules rest on do not appear on their own. Someone has to measure the data, and clean it, before it becomes a fact at all. That is where Pebblous has spent its years.

Maybe the hand that writes the rules and the hand that makes the facts were one hand all along.
