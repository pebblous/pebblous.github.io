"Stablecoins Are Not Cryptocurrency" — Circle CEO Jeremy Allaire's Seoul Declaration

On April 13, 2026, Circle Internet Financial CEO Jeremy Allaire and Hashed CEO Simon Kim held a public conversation in Seoul. The occasion was not routine. Earlier that day, Circle had announced USDC partnerships with South Korea's two largest cryptocurrency exchanges. Allaire had also met with major Korean banks, payment companies, and regulators.

The conversation covered three territories: Circle's strategy for entering the Korean market before stablecoin legislation is finalized, the convergence of AI agents and USDC payment infrastructure, and the coexistence of a future Korean won stablecoin with the global digital dollar network Circle is building.

What follows is an analysis of the key arguments Allaire and Kim made, and why they matter beyond Korea.

The Pre-Regulation Playbook

Korea's Digital Asset Basic Act is still being drafted. Stablecoin issuance rules, reserve requirements, and licensing frameworks remain unfinished. Most companies would wait. Circle moved first.

Allaire described this as deliberate strategy, not opportunism. Circle has used the same approach in Abu Dhabi, the EU (under MiCA), Hong Kong, and Japan: engage regulators during the drafting process, share operational experience from other jurisdictions, and position the company to comply from day one once rules take effect.

The partnership with Korea's top two exchanges makes USDC immediately available as a trading and investment asset. But Allaire's conversations with banks and payment firms signal a longer play. When Korean stablecoin legislation does arrive, Circle intends to be the company that already understands the local financial ecosystem — and has relationships with the institutions that will implement the new rules.

The GENIUS Act and What It Means for Korea

Allaire made a pointed argument about timing. The U.S. GENIUS Act — a stablecoin regulation bill defining reserve requirements, licensing, and consumer protections — is moving through Congress. Once passed, USDC becomes an effectively regulated digital dollar.

The implications are global. Stripe already lets its millions of merchants accept USDC alongside credit cards with a single settings change. Shopify pays merchants an extra 0.5% on USDC transactions as an adoption incentive. Gusto offers USDC payroll for global remote workers. These are not crypto-native companies. They are mainstream payment infrastructure providers integrating stablecoins as a default option.

Allaire's warning to Korea was direct: "The longer Korea waits, the more it falls behind." If the U.S. digital dollar network becomes the global standard before Korea launches its own won stablecoin, Korean institutions will be competing against entrenched infrastructure rather than building alongside it.

The AI Agent Economy

The most forward-looking section of the conversation was about AI. Allaire did not treat the AI agent economy as a theoretical future. He described it as an operational reality.

Circle recently held a USDC hackathon where only AI agents could participate and vote. Autonomous AIs interacted with each other using USDC as the payment layer, creating content and evaluating each other's work. Allaire called the results "surprising."

Three technical components define Circle's AI strategy:

Circle Gateway enables AI agents to set up integrated USDC balances and execute cross-chain payments in under one second. Transaction costs are measured in millionths of a cent.

X402 Protocol uses the HTTP 402 status code ("Payment Required") to let AI agents pay for APIs, data, and services directly through web requests. Thousands of services have adopted it. Ninety-nine percent of X402 payments are in USDC.

Arc Network is Circle's public blockchain for stablecoin payments, designed from the ground up with AI agents — not human users — as the primary customer.

Simon Kim added two concepts to the conversation that capture where this is heading. B2A, or Business to Agent, describes a market category where the buyer is an AI agent, not a person or company. KYA, Know Your Agent, extends financial identity verification from customers (KYC) and businesses (KYB) to the agents that are increasingly executing transactions on their behalf.

Won Stablecoin Coexistence

Allaire was careful to frame Circle's Korean strategy as complementary, not competitive, to a future won stablecoin. Circle does not plan to issue a won-denominated stablecoin. Instead, it wants Korean issuers to build on Circle's infrastructure — the same payment, foreign exchange, and settlement rails that USDC runs on.

This is a calculated position. If Korea's stablecoin ecosystem uses Circle's underlying infrastructure for cross-border settlement between won and dollar stablecoins, Circle becomes an indispensable layer regardless of which local stablecoin wins market share.

CPN as a SWIFT Competitor

The Circle Payments Network (CPN) connects banks, fintechs, and exchanges for dollar-based cross-border settlement. The explicit comparison point is SWIFT — but faster, cheaper, and operating 24/7. For Korean financial institutions evaluating their international payments infrastructure, CPN represents an alternative rail that does not require waiting for domestic legislation to be finalized.

The Data Quality Question Underneath

If AI agents are buying data and executing financial transactions autonomously — paying for API calls, purchasing datasets, settling invoices — a question emerges that Allaire's infrastructure does not yet answer: who verifies the quality of the data these agents consume?

An AI agent with a verified identity (KYA) and a funded USDC wallet can still make catastrophic decisions if it operates on corrupted, biased, or stale data. The X402 protocol guarantees payment. It does not guarantee that what was paid for is accurate.

As stablecoin infrastructure becomes the circulatory system of the AI agent economy, data quality infrastructure becomes the diagnostic layer that ensures what flows through the system is trustworthy. The payment rails and the data rails are two halves of the same architecture.
