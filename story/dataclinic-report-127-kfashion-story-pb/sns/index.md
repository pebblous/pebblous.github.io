AI Hub의 K-Fashion 이미지 97만 장을 DataClinic으로 진단했습니다. 24개 스타일로 라벨링된 데이터셋이고, 공식 분류 정확도는 91%로 우수합니다. 그런데 클래스 구별 렌즈로 '가장 전형적인 스트리트'를 뽑자, 1·2위를 같은 촬영 세션의 꽃무늬 블라우스 사진이 차지했습니다.

이유는 배치 촬영에 있습니다. 룩북은 한 모델이 한 옷을 입고 같은 세트에서 수십 장을 연속으로 찍고, 거의 같은 사진이 한 클래스에 쌓입니다. 스트리트 한 클래스가 전체의 46.5%를 차지하는 물량과 만나면, 모델이 배우는 건 스타일이 아니라 '스튜디오에 전신으로 서서 밝은 상의에 데님을 입은' 촬영 포맷입니다.

정작 도시 창가에서 찍은 오버사이즈 그래픽 맨투맨, 진짜 거리 감성의 사진은 '비전형'으로 분포 변두리에 밀려나 있었습니다. 게다가 같은 세션의 near-duplicate가 학습셋과 평가셋에 동시에 들어가면, 정확도가 실제보다 높게 측정되는 누수도 생깁니다.

라벨이 맞다고 데이터가 옳은 건 아닙니다. 모든 사진에 '스트리트'가 정확히 붙어 있어도, 밀도가 가리키는 전형은 전혀 다른 곳을 향할 수 있습니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The single most "typical" street-fashion image an AI learned from was a floral chiffon blouse, shot in a white studio — the kind of look most people would call romantic.

We ran 967,806 K-Fashion images through DataClinic. The dataset is labeled across 24 styles and posts a strong 91% official classification score. Yet when we asked which images were most prototypical of "street," the top two were near-identical frames from a single studio shoot of that floral blouse.

The cause is batch shooting. A lookbook captures one model in one outfit dozens of times, piling near-duplicates into a single class. Combined with one class holding 46.5% of the data, the model doesn't learn a style. It learns a shooting format: studio, full-body, bright top, denim.

The genuinely street look, an oversized graphic sweatshirt by a city window, was pushed to the margins as atypical. And when those near-duplicates land in both train and test sets, accuracy reads higher than it really is.

A correct label doesn't make the data right. Every photo here is tagged "street," yet what density treats as typical points somewhere else entirely.

## Facebook (EN)

"The most typical street outfit."

When we asked the AI to point to it, it chose a photograph of a floral chiffon blouse and skinny jeans, taken against a clean white studio wall.

Anyone would call that look romantic. And yet the label read, clearly, "street."

Of the 967,000 K-Fashion images we went through, this single frame stayed with me the longest.

The dataset is tidy. Twenty-four styles, an official accuracy of 91%, nothing obviously wrong. But once you spread the images out and look at how they cluster, you start to see what the number quietly hides.

One class, street, holds nearly half of everything. And a lookbook shoot captures one model, in one outfit, dozens of times in the same spot. Those near-identical frames pile up until the model stops learning the clothing and starts learning the way the photo was taken.

The truly street image, an oversized graphic sweatshirt shot by a city window, was the one pushed to the edges, filed away as atypical.

Every label was correct. And still, what the data called typical was looking somewhere we never meant it to.

So does a correct label make the data right?

It's a question worth carrying back to every other dataset we trust. That is part of why we look at how data actually clusters, rather than only at how it is labeled.
