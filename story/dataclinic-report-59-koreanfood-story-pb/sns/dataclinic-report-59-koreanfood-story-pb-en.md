## Facebook

🌱 What does AI see when it looks at Korean food? Not what you'd expect.

Imagine a foreigner visiting Korea for the first time.
They look at a table full of dishes and classify them the simplest way possible:
"This has soup. This doesn't."

That's exactly what a general-purpose AI does with 150 Korean food categories.

We ran DataClinic on the Korean Food Image dataset — 150 classes, 150,507 images — and scored it 71 out of 100 (Fair).

The dataset itself is remarkably well-balanced. Nearly perfect uniformity across all 150 categories.
But when we asked a general-purpose AI to organize these images, it split them into two clusters:
soups on one side, dry dishes on the other.

Kimchi-jjigae and doenjang-jjigae became neighbors — not because of taste, but because of broth.
Japchae and bulgogi sat together — not by recipe, but by visual dryness.

Then we switched to a food-specialized model.
Suddenly, the AI began seeing dishes the way a Korean chef would — by ingredients, by cooking method, by cultural context.

Some surprising discoveries:
▸ Songpyeon (rice cake) is the most "typical" Korean food for AI — sitting right at the center of the embedding space
▸ Gimbap is the most atypical — its visual diversity across different fillings confuses the model
▸ 33 out of 150 classes have excessive density — trimming duplicates through a "Data Diet" could boost training efficiency by over 20%

Just as a farmer must understand the soil's composition before deciding what to cultivate,
understanding how AI perceives food images is the essential first step to growing better, more culturally aware models.
