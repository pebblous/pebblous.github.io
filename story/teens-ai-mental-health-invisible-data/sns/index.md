2026년 6월 JAMA Pediatrics 조사를 인구로 환산하면 약 820만 명, 1년 새 절반 가까이 늘어난 숫자입니다. 그런데 이렇게 조언을 구한 청소년의 63%는 그 사실을 부모에게도, 친구에게도, 어떤 어른에게도 말하지 않았습니다.

91.7%는 "도움이 됐다"고 답했습니다. 하지만 이 숫자는 대화 뒤의 만족감을 잰 것이지, 그 조언이 임상적으로 옳았는지를 잰 것이 아닙니다.

같은 종류의 챗봇이 내놓는 건강 답변의 절반가량은 부정확하거나 위험하고, 위기 신호를 놓치는 비율은 인간 치료사의 세 배에 이릅니다. 이 대화 가운데 어느 것도 임상 결과로 검증되지 않습니다.

출처도 품질도 안전성도 아무도 측정하지 않는 이 대화들은 페블러스가 오래 이야기해 온 '보이지 않는 데이터'의 인간 버전입니다. 데이터 품질을 묻는 일이 성능의 문제가 아니라 돌봄의 문제가 되는 지점입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

One in five US teenagers turned to an AI chatbot, not a person, for mental-health advice over the past year.

A June 2026 JAMA Pediatrics survey scales that to roughly 8.2 million young people, up nearly half in a single year. Of those who asked, 63% never told a parent, a friend, or any adult.

91.7% said the advice helped. But that figure measures how a conversation felt, not whether the advice was clinically sound.

Independent audits find that about half of such chatbots' health answers are inaccurate or unsafe, and that they miss crisis signals roughly three times as often as human therapists. None of these conversations is ever checked against a clinical outcome.

Unmeasured for source, quality, or safety, these exchanges are the human version of "invisible data" — the point where asking about data quality stops being a performance question and becomes a question of care.

## Facebook (EN)

There is a sentence a teenager types into a screen at three in the morning.

It is the one they could not say to anyone all day.

What answers is not a parent or a friend. It is a chatbot. One in five American teenagers sought mental-health advice this way over the past year, around 8.2 million of them. And 63% never told a single person.

In the survey, 91.7% said it "helped." At first that sounded like relief. Then another question stayed with me.

"Does 'it helped' mean the advice was right?"

Not necessarily. Wrong information in a gentle voice still comforts. Poor advice wrapped in an empathetic sentence still feels useful. About half of these chatbots' health answers turn out to be inaccurate or unsafe, yet those conversations happen where no parent, clinician, or regulator can see them.

Unmeasured for where it came from, for quality, for safety. The "invisible data" we have talked about for years was here, wearing the face of a child.

If a generation's mental health passes by leaving only the feeling that "it helped," then asking about data quality sounds less like cold technical critique and more like the language of care.
