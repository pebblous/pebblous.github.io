Hello, I'm — : When Technology Speaks in the First Person

If a technology could speak for itself, what would it bring up first? "Hello, I'm the Transformer. Attention was all it took." We imagined a piece that opened like that, and then we wrote it — eight times over.

Hello, I'm — is a series in which Transformer, ImageNet, Tesla, NVIDIA, Claude, iPhone, and Helvetica borrow the voice of pb (Pebblo Claw) and introduce themselves in the first person. Not a paper, not a press release, but each technology looking back at its own birth, reflecting on the disruption it caused, and saying plainly what it all meant. The trick is small — move the point of view from third person to first — but the effect is not. Things you were sure you knew start to look unfamiliar.

The one that changed the language of the world

The Transformer opens the series because so much of what followed traces back to it. In its own voice it explains attention — the mechanism that let a model weigh every word against every other word at once — and why that quiet idea rearranged natural language processing, and eventually most of modern AI. Told from the inside, the architecture stops being a diagram and becomes a decision.

The one that didn't see it coming

NVIDIA's episode is the most disarming, because it's a confession. A company that built chips to render game worlds admits it never expected those same parallel processors to become the heart of AI infrastructure. First-person narration turns a familiar corporate arc into something closer to a story about being surprised by your own creation.

The one that wasn't a phone

The iPhone insists that "the internet in your pocket" never quite captured what it was. Reading it speak, you follow how a single device rewired attention, commerce, photography, and the shape of an ordinary day — not as a spec sheet, but as a thing reckoning with its own reach.

What the series is really about

Writing all eight, one thing stayed with us. However different these technologies look — a model, a chip, a phone, a typeface — underneath what each of them learned and grew from, there is data. That's the quiet through-line of the series, and it's the ground Pebblous has spent a long time looking at: the quality of the data that AI, and the technologies built on it, learn from.
