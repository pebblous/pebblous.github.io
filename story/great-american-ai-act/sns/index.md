## LinkedIn (EN)

The headline in Congress's first federal AI bill was a three-year freeze on state regulation. But the most structural clause sits elsewhere: the government's opening move isn't prohibition. It's measurement.

Released June 4 by Rep. Jay Obernolte (R) and Rep. Lori Trahan (D), the Great American AI Act draft preempts state laws governing AI development until December 2029. In the same draft, it hands federal statistical agencies a new job. The Bureau of Labor Statistics and the Census Bureau must revise their surveys to capture how AI is reshaping employment, and any mass layoff where AI was a substantial factor must be disclosed, down to an estimate of the jobs it eliminated.

Clean data won't follow automatically. New York ran the same disclosure a year early; 160-plus companies filed, but separating an "AI" layoff from ordinary software modernization proved hard.

Still, the sequence has flipped. Regulation now begins not by deciding what to ban, but by making what's happening countable.

## Facebook (EN)

Imagine a new line appearing on a layoff notice.

"Was AI a substantial factor in these cuts? If so, how many jobs did it erase?"

That clause sits inside the first federal AI bill the U.S. House unveiled on June 4, the bipartisan Great American AI Act. The headlines went to a different provision, a three-year pause on state AI rules. But I kept returning to the quieter clause beside it.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics would revise its surveys to count what AI is doing to employment, and the Labor Department would track the occupations most exposed to it. Before banning anything, before permitting anything, the government would first count.

Regulation usually begins with "what should be forbidden." This draft begins with "what is actually happening." The order has flipped. And to answer that question, each scattered layoff has to become a row of data someone can add up.

Counting isn't the same as knowing, of course. New York ran the same disclosure a year early, and the "because of AI" box still proved hard to fill honestly. Yet the instinct — make it countable before you act on it — is familiar to anyone who works with data. Good decisions have always rested on data you can trust.

Maybe that's where regulation is quietly moving: not toward deciding what to forbid first, but toward making what's happening countable first.
