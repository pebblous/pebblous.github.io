Your Data Is Worth $0.03 to You and $319 Billion to Them

The data-broker industry is worth roughly $319 billion. The individual whose data flows through it typically pockets about $0.03. That single spread says almost everything about the market we are now walking into, and it is the reason we built a hub around it: the moment data stops being fuel and becomes an asset, the interesting questions stop being technical and start being about ownership, price, and proof.

For years, data was treated as raw material — something you scraped, cleaned, and fed to a model, valuable only in the aggregate and rarely priced on its own. That framing is quietly ending. Data is now something that gets bought, sold, certified, and fought over as an asset in its own right. And an asset needs three things a fuel never did: a mechanism to set its price, a standard to measure its quality, and a system to prove who owns it. The Data Economy hub follows each of those threads through the pieces we've written, and read together they keep pointing at the same missing layer.

Whose data is it, anyway?

The first thread is ownership. "My Data Is Mine" looks at the strange economics that let a $319 billion broker market pay individuals almost nothing, and at the technologies trying to rebalance it — Web3, self-sovereign identity, and decentralized data marketplaces that let people hold and license their own data instead of surrendering it invisibly. The argument is not that decentralization is a cure-all. It is that data sovereignty only becomes real when the person who generated the data can prove the claim and set terms on it.

Putting a price tag on data

Ownership is meaningless without price discovery, which is the second thread. "The Technology of Pricing Data" examines a Pebblous patent, virtual-environment-based data value proof, and how it aims to become infrastructure for the emerging agent economy. Most valuable data trades in the dark today — no listed price, no provenance, no measurable quality. If autonomous agents are going to buy and sell data on our behalf, they need something to buy against: a defensible measure of what a dataset is actually worth.

When the law starts treating data as an asset

Markets need rules, and the third thread is institutional. "Data Is an Asset Too" reads Korea's Digital Asset Basic Law, which defines 12 business areas, already covers 11.13 million users, and formalizes a KRW 87.2 trillion market. A related piece follows the National Growth Fund's first direct software investment — KRW 560 billion into Upstage — and the sovereign-AI stakes behind a Korean-language data gap of 0.823%. Capital and legal scaffolding are arriving faster than most people realize; the constraint is no longer whether data can be an asset, but whether the asset can be trusted.

The math underneath quality

Which brings the fourth thread, and the one the whole hub keeps circling back to. "The Mathematics of Data Quality" opens up the QM codes inside ISO/IEC 5259-2 and shows how similarity, representativeness, and diversity get measured as numbers rather than adjectives. It sits next to the DataClinic diagnosis stories — 134 datasets and 12 million images examined — because they are two views of the same claim: quality is not a vibe, it is something you can quantify, and a data asset without a measured quality grade is a price waiting to be wrong.

Why it matters

The instinct across the data economy has been to build markets first: marketplaces, tokens, legal categories, price mechanisms. This hub argues for a different order. However elegant the pricing and however sound the law, a market for data cannot stand if no one can vouch for whether the data itself is accurate, representative, and genuinely owned. The bottleneck in turning data into an asset is not regulation and not blockchain. It is quality.

That is the lens Pebblous reads this space through. Not selling data faster, but making it provable — diagnosed for quality, measured for value, and carrying a record of where it came from. It is the work behind DataClinic, our data-quality diagnosis, refinement, and certification layer, connected by the patent that measures and proves what data is worth. The data economy will run on trust it cannot yet manufacture. That trust starts one layer down, in the quality of the data.
