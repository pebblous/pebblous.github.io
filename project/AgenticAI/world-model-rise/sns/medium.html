VLMs See, VLAs Act — But Neither Understands the World

Ask GPT-4V to describe a photo of a ball on a ramp and it will give you a fluent answer. Ask it where the ball will stop after rolling down, and the answer becomes unreliable. The model can perceive. It cannot simulate physics.

This is the structural gap at the center of today's AI architectures. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) excel at understanding images and answering questions about them. Vision-Language-Action Models (VLAs) go further, predicting robot movements from visual input. But neither can answer the question that matters most for physical AI: "If I do this now, what happens to the world in 5 seconds?"

Three Walls VLMs Hit

VLMs are powerful — GPT-4V, Gemini, and LLaVA have reached near-human performance on image classification and visual question answering. But they share three fundamental limitations:

  • The Symbol Grounding Problem — VLMs appear to understand that "an apple falls," but this is a statistical pattern, not internalized physics. The grounding that cognitive science demands is absent.
  • Reasoning requires language — All VLM reasoning is mediated through text. Humans can reason spatially without words. VLMs cannot.
  • Temporal disconnection — Most VLMs process frames independently. "Where was this object 5 seconds ago?" is structurally hard to answer.
"MLLMs cannot truly reason without language. Their reasoning, perception, and decision-making processes are tightly coupled to textual representations."

VLAs Add Action — But Not Understanding

VLAs like RT-2, Pi0, and NVIDIA GR00T added an action prediction layer. A self-driving car recognizing "there is a person ahead" and deciding "stop" is achievable with VLA. But predicting whether that person will suddenly step into the road in 3 seconds is a different capability entirely — causal reasoning and future state prediction.

Long-horizon tasks requiring thousands of sequential actions — mining a diamond in Minecraft, complex robotic assembly — are hard to solve with VLAs alone. The ability to imagine the future, evaluate consequences inside that imagination, and select an optimal path is what world models provide.

World Models: AI That Simulates Before It Acts

A world model is an AI's internal predictive simulation of the environment. Rather than simply perceiving the current state, it can imagine: "If I take this action, how will the world change?" Two core functions — world understanding (internalizing physics) and future prediction (simulating consequences of possible actions).

Yann LeCun has been the most vocal advocate: "In 3–5 years, nobody will be using LLMs the way we use them today — world models will be the mainstream AI architecture." He backed this conviction by founding AMI Labs with €500M at a €3B valuation.

Four Architectures Compared

2025 was an inflection point. Four major systems demonstrated real capability with different approaches:

  • V-JEPA 2 (Meta) — 1.2B params, trained on 1M+ hours of video but generates no pixels — predicts in latent space. Transfers to robot control with just 62 hours of robot data. Plans 30x faster than Cosmos.
  • NVIDIA Cosmos — Trained on 20M hours of real-world video. Strong at synthetic data generation for robotics and autonomous driving. 2M downloads in first month.
  • Genie 3 (Google DeepMind) — First general-purpose interactive world model generating real-time 3D worlds at 24fps from text prompts alone.
  • DreamerV3/4 (DeepMind) — RL agents train inside mental simulations created by the world model. Dreamer 4 solved Minecraft diamond using only offline data.

The Data Quality Question

World models learn physics from data — millions of hours of video, simulation outputs, sensor logs. The quality of that data determines whether the world model learns real physics or statistical artifacts. Noisy training video produces hallucinated physics. Biased simulation data produces models that fail in edge cases.

As these architectures move from research to deployment — in autonomous driving, robotics, manufacturing — the bottleneck shifts from model architecture to data infrastructure. Diagnosing distribution anomalies, temporal inconsistencies, and domain gaps in training data is the prerequisite for world models that actually work.
