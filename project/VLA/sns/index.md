## LinkedIn (EN)

Chatbots answer in text. Multimodal models answer while looking at an image. What comes next is a robot's body.

A VLA (Vision-Language-Action) model hears "put that ball in the cup," locates the ball and cup on the table, decides how to move its arm, and actually does it. Moving past the LLM that only reads and the VLM that sees and talks, it is AI that has finally gained a body.

That is why NVIDIA (GR00T), Google DeepMind (Gemini Robotics), and Physical Intelligence (π) are racing to build "robot brains" on very different architectures.

The real bottleneck is not the architecture but the data. Behavior data from a robot actually seeing and moving is slow and expensive to gather in the real world. So synthesizing it with 3DGS and simulation, and guaranteeing that synthetic data's quality, move to the center of the contest.

That is the angle Pebblous takes on VLA. This hub gathers five threads in one place — an introduction to Physical AI, an architecture comparison, synthetic data, and the critique of whether VLA is even the right answer.

## Facebook (EN)

"Put that ball in the cup."

Lately I keep seeing clips of a robot hearing that one line, reaching out, and actually picking the ball up.

A chatbot answers in text. A multimodal model answers while looking at a photo. This is something else — seeing, understanding, and moving a body to change the world. A model that links sight and language to action is called a VLA (Vision-Language-Action) model.

NVIDIA, Google DeepMind, and Physical Intelligence are each building this "robot brain" on a different structure. But reading through these pieces one by one, I came to think the hard part isn't the design of the brain.

To learn that single motion of reaching out, a robot needs data from actually seeing and moving. Collecting that data in the real world is slow, expensive, and sometimes dangerous. So we make the data in simulation, and then we have to ask whether the data we made is good enough to be real.

What a robot has seen and learned from decides what a robot can do. That is why Pebblous keeps looking at VLA through the lens of data.
