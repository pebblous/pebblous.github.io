The Data Robots Learn From — and the One Thing It Forgets

Open a public robot dataset and you find three things sitting side by side: the camera frames a robot saw, the states of its joints, and the action commands it was given. That triple is the backbone of nearly every dataset a robot foundation model trains on today.

What you almost never find is the record of how that data came to be. The pressure at a fingertip when the gripper closed. Whether the object slipped. Which seed and which lighting a synthetic demonstration was generated under. Whether the sequence is even physically consistent from one frame to the next. Datasets keep the result and throw away the history.

That missing history has a name: provenance. And it turns out to be the thread running through almost every serious question in physical AI data right now.

We gathered sixteen pieces on the datasets robots and physical AI learn from into a single hub — from real-robot teleoperation to simulator synthesis, from tactile data to national behavior-data policy. Different subjects, but read together, they keep lighting up the same gap from different angles. Here is a map of where to start.

Start here: six datasets, one format, zero provenance

The overview piece compares six well-known robot datasets — DROID, Open X-Embodiment, GR00T, RoboCasa, MimicGen, and LIBERO — along scale, license, and format. The surprising finding is a convergence: collection splits six different ways, from human teleoperation to physics-engine synthesis, but distribution has quietly converged on a single format, LeRobot. One shared container for six very different origins.

The other shared trait is less comforting. All six record what happened, but none record how it was produced or whether it holds up physically. Six datasets, six blind spots in the same place.

Real robots are slow; simulators forget the physics

Real-robot data — the kind in DROID and Open X-Embodiment — is collected by people teleoperating physical machines. It is realistic, and it is slow and expensive. So the field leans on synthesis. NVIDIA's GR00T Blueprint, for instance, can generate 780,000 robot trajectories in about eleven hours, and Isaac Sim advertises training roughly a thousand times faster than real time. The scale is real.

But synthetic scale carries a quiet liability. A physics engine can produce a video that looks correct and is subtly, physically wrong — a grasp that would never hold, a contact force that no real hand could exert. One piece in the hub dissects the emerging technique family that audits, after the fact, whether generated video from systems like Cosmos and Sora is physically correct. Another, on RoboScape, goes the other way: it enforces physical validity at the moment of generation, and reports matching the performance of 200 real videos using 200 synthetic ones.

The data that capital skipped: touch and failure

Two pieces in the hub sit on what the money and the cameras tend to miss. Humanoid robots and foundation models drew billions in funding; tactile data — the pressure and slip a robot feels through its fingers — drew only millions. AGIBOT WORLD 2026's tactile work records not clean successes but the texture of contact: misses, collisions, slips. It's the physics that vision alone can't teach.

And where most pipelines discard a failed demonstration, AgiBotWorld 2026 keeps it, annotating each failure with fields like error_cause and restorable. Keeping failure as labeled data, rather than deleting it, is still rare — and it is, in a sense, provenance by another name: a record of not just what happened, but why it went wrong.

Why this is a data-quality question, not just a data question

The instinct in physical AI has been to make more data — more teleoperation hours, more synthetic trajectories, more simulators running in parallel. The hub argues for a different axis. However much the data grows, if there's no evidence that it is physically correct, the trust bottleneck stays exactly where it was.

That is the lens Pebblous reads this data through: not making more data, but making proof-attached data. Data that carries a contact ledger, placement error, segmentation and depth, a generation history of seed and lighting, and a provenance chain sealed by content hashes — so the data itself can prove its physical consistency and origin. It's the work behind DataClinic, our data-quality diagnosis, refinement, and certification layer. Sixteen pieces, and the same underlying question in each: can data prove its own history?
