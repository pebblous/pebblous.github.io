Datagen Raised $70M and Shut Down. NVIDIA Bought Gretel for $320M.

Two companies were in the same business: manufacturing data that no one ever collected. Datagen raised $70 million and closed its doors. Gretel, working the same market, sold to NVIDIA for $320 million. Same raw material, opposite endings.

That contrast is the fastest way into a question the industry has been circling for a while. Synthetic data — information generated by algorithms and simulation rather than gathered from the world — was supposed to be the answer to two chronic shortages: not enough training data, and privacy rules that lock away the data that does exist. For years the pitch was simple. If collecting is slow and expensive, print it instead.

The rise and fall of eight global synthetic data companies suggests the pitch was only half right.

Why synthetic data, and why now

The urgency comes from Physical AI. Robots, self-driving cars, and smart factories need to learn from the physical world, and collecting that data in reality is slow, costly, and sometimes dangerous. So the field turned to digital twins — high-fidelity virtual replicas of the physical world — to generate the training data instead.

The market math is striking. Synthetic data is growing at a compound annual rate somewhere between 31% and 46%. The digital twin market sat around $21–29 billion in 2025; the Physical AI market was a smaller $5.1–5.4 billion the same year, but is projected to cross into a $20–40 billion range by 2030. And Gartner's often-cited forecast: by 2030, 60% of the data used to train AI will be synthetic.

A more recent wrinkle is self-distillation, where large language models generate their own high-quality training data. The supply side is getting stranger and more powerful at once.

Reading the market, chapter by chapter

The hub gathers scattered pieces into one place, and a few of them are worth calling out.

The truth about pricing. Synthetic data does not have a single price; it has a price that swings wildly by modality. Structured tables are cheap. Images and 3D data run 10 to 100 times higher. Vendors like MOSTLY AI, Gretel, and Tonic have converged on a three-part model — a platform floor, a variable meter, and value-added services — but the number a buyer sees depends almost entirely on what kind of data they need.

The rise and fall of companies. Datagen and Gretel are the two poles, and the pattern between them is consistent. The companies that struggled bet on a single modality. The ones that survived did something harder: they integrated generation with a way to prove the data was any good.

Digital twin meets Physical AI. The two markets are on a collision course, and the intersection is where high-fidelity synthetic data stops being a convenience and becomes infrastructure. A factory simulation that gets the physics wrong does not just produce bad data; it teaches a robot the wrong lessons.

Why it matters: the moat is validation, not generation

Here is the lesson underneath every one of those chapters. Making data in bulk is no longer the hard part. The hard part is vouching for it. Does this synthetic scene actually obey the physics of the real world, or does it carry what you might call a physical hallucination — a plausible-looking image that would never occur under real forces? A model trained on hallucinated physics learns confident nonsense.
