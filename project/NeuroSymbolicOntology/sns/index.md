## LinkedIn (EN)

You have probably noticed the strange gap: a chatbot writes fluent, confident prose and then invents a fact that never existed. That gap sits exactly where today's AI is strong and where it is not.

Deep learning reads patterns beautifully. In Kahneman's terms it is System 1, the intuition. What it still lacks is System 2, the part that explains, checks, and reasons consistently. Neuro-symbolic AI puts symbolic reasoning back on top of that intuition to keep hallucination in check and to make domain knowledge verifiable.

The floor that reasoning stands on is the ontology. Only when it is written down which objects exist and what relations and constraints hold between them can a model's output be checked against rules. As Henry Kautz argues in his account of the three AI summers, symbolism and connectionism were never rival camps. They were two axes that need each other.

The theory has already turned into revenue. Palantir layered an operational ontology onto its AIP platform and lifted U.S. commercial revenue by 137 percent. But the picture is only complete once you see that its closed-world approach differs from the open-world ontologies of the Semantic Web, and that letting an LLM build graphs on its own, as GraphRAG does, carries a quality cost in schema drift.

From Kautz's three summers to Palantir's 137 percent to a hard look at GraphRAG quality, the related pieces are gathered in one place.

## Facebook (EN)

There is a moment when a chatbot, mid-answer, calmly invents a citation that was never there.

The person who notices is usually the one who knows the field.

A machine sees texture. The texture of sentences, the texture of data. It catches the repetitions and the differences between them with astonishing skill. But seeing a texture and knowing what exists, and what quietly contradicts what, are two different things.

A neural network is close to the eye. It sees the fine grain of the world.
An ontology is close to the bones. It settles what exists, what relates to what, and the constraints anything must move within.

The eye alone cannot stand for long, and the bones alone cannot see. The word neuro-symbolic is, in the end, an old attempt to set the two back into one body. Henry Kautz called this not a turf war but the lesson left by three AI summers.

Word came recently that Palantir had lifted its revenue sharply with an operational ontology. Yet I find myself staying longer with the question that comes before the number.

"If a machine is to truly reason, what must be defined before it can begin?"
