We Graded Our Own Data, and It Failed

Pebblous built a dataset called Star-MNIST, fed it into DataClinic — our own data-quality tool — and watched it score 54, rated "Poor." Calling your own work bad with your own hands isn't comfortable. But a diagnostic tool that only inspects other people's data, and goes quiet in front of its maker's, isn't worth trusting. That moment of honest self-diagnosis is a good doorway into the DataClinic hub, where we gathered the full arc of this product: what it is, how it maps to standards, what it patents, and what it finds.

What DataClinic actually is

DataClinic is Pebblous's AI data-quality management platform. Its core engine, DataLens, began as a DNN-based system and evolved into a neuro-symbolic architecture. Paired with Data Imaging — a technique that turns a dataset's distribution, anomalies, gaps, and biases into a visual you can read at a glance — it makes quality legible instead of merely numeric. The technology has been validated through engagements with global enterprises including Hyundai Motor, LG Electronics, LG U+, and Hanwha Vision.

Grounded in a standard, sealed in a patent

Data quality is easy to assert and hard to prove. DataClinic automatically computes the Quality Measures required by ISO/IEC 5259, the international standard for AI data quality — completeness, similarity, representativeness — turning claims into numbers a standard recognizes. In 2025 it was designated a Public Procurement Service Innovation Product, and its core method is registered as US patent US 12,481,720 B2, covering the Data Value Proof approach behind the diagnosis.

The gap beneath a balanced surface

The most instructive pieces in the hub are the diagnoses of well-known public datasets. MIT's Places365 — 1.8 million images across 365 classes — looks, on the surface, like the most evenly balanced dataset you could ask for. DataClinic found 61 classes overlapping in feature space: beneath a uniform distribution, visual identities were quietly blurring into one another. That is the whole discipline in one example. Data quality is the habit of standing in front of data that looks well-organized and asking, once more, whether it really is.

From diagnosis to an autonomous pipeline

The hub also traces where this is heading. Pebblous is expanding DataClinic into Data Greenhouse, an Agentic AI-based data operating system that carries diagnosis through to synthetic-data generation and quality certification. And in one meta piece, the dc-story-produce pipeline shows the operational side of it: seven AI agents making 141 tool calls across nine stages, producing a Korean and English blog post in about two hours. Diagnosis, in other words, doesn't stop at a score — it becomes the input to everything downstream.

What distinguishes DataClinic's method is where it looks. Most quality checks operate on surface statistics — missing values, label counts, class balance. Data Imaging works in the embedding space, comparing datasets by similarity, representativeness, and separability rather than by tidy metadata. That is why Places365 could look balanced by class count and still fail: the classes were distinct on paper and overlapping in feature space, exactly the kind of gap a headcount can't see. The patent portfolio spanning the US, Korea, Japan, and PCT — covering Data Imaging, manifold learning, and synthetic-data generation — is the moat around that method, and the reason a giant with more capital can't replicate the diagnosis overnight.

The thread through all of it is a single conviction: however capable a model is, the trust sits one layer down, in the data. That is the work behind DataClinic — diagnosing data quality, proving it, and certifying it. Even when the data is our own.
