FTC법 제5조는 40년 넘게 광고와 상거래의 거짓을 다뤄 온 조항이다. 새 법을 만들지 않고 그 잣대를 AI 출력에 그대로 겨눴다. 지금까지 AI 정확성은 벤치마크 점수의 문제였지 법적 책임이 아니었는데, 그 경계선이 처음으로 소비자보호법 안으로 넘어온 것이다.

기술 한계에서 나오는 환각은 겨냥하지 않는다. 표적은 정확할 수 있는데도 미공개 목표를 위해 일부러 비트는 조정이다. 빠져나갈 길도 함께 열어 뒀다. 소비자에게 명확하고 지속적으로 공시하면 책임을 피할 수 있다.

다만 이건 확정 규칙이 아니라 2대 0으로 공개된 비구속적 초안이고, 의견수렴은 7월 31일 끝난다. 챗봇이 차별적 답을 내지 않게 학습시키는 통상적 안전장치까지 '의도적 조정'으로 걸릴 수 있다는 우려도 이미 나온다.

그런데 '공시하라'는 요구는 곧바로 더 어려운 질문을 남긴다. 무엇을 왜 그렇게 조정했는지 증명하려면 학습·튜닝 데이터의 이력이 남아 있어야 한다. 정확성을 규제가 요구하는 순간, 문제는 데이터 거버넌스로 돌아온다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

For the first time, the U.S. FTC has signaled that quietly steering an AI's answers toward an undisclosed goal can count as consumer deception.

On July 1 the agency released a draft policy statement that aims Section 5 of the FTC Act, its 40-year-old ban on deceptive commerce, at AI outputs. Until now, AI "accuracy" was a benchmark score, not a legal liability. That line has crossed into consumer protection law.

The statement spares hallucinations that stem from technical limits. Its target is deliberate steering: an output that could have been accurate but was twisted for another goal. There is an escape hatch, too. A company can avoid liability by disclosing the steering to consumers clearly and persistently.

But this is a non-binding draft, released on a 2–0 vote, with comments closing July 31. Critics already warn the wording could sweep in ordinary safety guardrails, such as training a chatbot not to produce discriminatory answers.

And the demand to "disclose" raises a harder question. Proving what was adjusted and why requires a preserved record of training and fine-tuning data. The moment regulators ask for accuracy, the problem returns to data governance.

지금까지 AI의 정확성은 벤치마크 점수였습니다. 잘 맞히면 좋은 모델, 못 맞히면 아쉬운 모델. 그뿐이었습니다. 그 정확성이 처음으로 소비자를 보호하는 법의 언어 안으로 들어왔습니다.

'그럼 정확성을 무엇으로 증명하지?'

## Facebook (EN)

You have probably trusted a chatbot's answer at least once.

Not gone back to a search engine to check it. Just taken the sentence on the screen and moved on.

A draft policy statement the U.S. FTC released on July 1 begins right at that moment.

If an AI company can produce an accurate answer but quietly steers it toward some other goal, and never says so, that alone may count as deceiving the consumer. It is the same standard that has governed false advertising for more than forty years, now aimed at what an AI says.

For years, accuracy in AI was a benchmark number. A good model got things right; a weaker one didn't. That was all. For the first time, accuracy has entered the language of consumer protection law.

What stayed with me was a quieter question.

'Then how do you prove accuracy?'

The statement tells companies to disclose, to tell consumers what was adjusted and why. But it never says what to show, or how to make it verifiable.

Proof, in the end, is a matter of records. Which data a model learned from, which tuning was applied and why, who made that decision and when. An organization without that lineage will have nothing to say when the demand to "prove your accuracy" arrives. And those records cannot be reconstructed after the fact.
