What Is Data Lineage? And Why Your AI Pipeline Can't Afford to Ignore It

When a medical algorithm delivered biased diagnoses to 200 million patients, investigators faced an uncomfortable reality: no one had documented where the training data had come from. That absence of documentation is the problem at the center of data lineage.

Data lineage is the practice of recording where your data originated, how it was transformed as it moved through your pipeline, and where it was ultimately consumed. It is the audit trail for the most consequential input any AI system receives — and until recently, most teams treated it as optional.

Three Incidents That Changed How We Think About This

In 2019, a study in Science revealed that a widely deployed healthcare algorithm systematically underserved Black patients. The root cause was not the model architecture. It was training data that encoded historical spending patterns as a proxy for healthcare need — a decision that had never been documented, questioned, or auditable after the fact.

The examples do not stop there. A 2025 study in NPJ Digital Medicine found that an AI-based psychiatric counseling model proposed treatment plans significantly more biased than those of medical trainees. Researchers traced the problem to the training dataset — but only after the fact, and only because they went looking deliberately.

Then there is the quieter type of failure: a null field silently converted to zero at an intermediate pipeline step. No one noticed. The downstream consequences accumulated over weeks before the error was identified and corrected. With lineage tracking at the column level, the incorrect transformation would have been visible at the point it was introduced.

What Data Lineage Is — And Is Not

Lineage is often conflated with provenance. Provenance refers to the legal and ethical origin of data: who collected it, under what consent, in what context. Lineage is narrower and more operational. It is the technical record of what happened to data between its origin and its use.

A complete lineage graph answers three questions at any point in your pipeline:

  • Upstream: Where did this data originate?
  • Transformation: What operations were applied, in what order, and when?
  • Downstream: Who consumed the output, and what did they produce from it?

There are two levels that matter for compliance and debugging. Table-level lineage shows which tables feed which models. Column-level lineage goes deeper: it tracks which specific fields were transformed and how. For regulatory compliance, table-level alone is insufficient. Column-level lineage is the minimum credible baseline.

Three Methods for Collecting Lineage

Parsing-based lineage analyzes SQL queries, Spark jobs, or DAG definitions statically to extract dependency graphs. It requires no code changes but cannot capture runtime transformations or conditional branching — what was supposed to happen, not necessarily what did.

Log-based lineage instruments the pipeline to emit lineage events at runtime, capturing what actually happened. OpenLineage standardizes the event schema across tools and frameworks; Marquez serves as the reference backend for storing and querying those events.

Self-contained lineage builds documentation into the transformation layer itself. Tools like dbt generate model-level dependency graphs as a natural side effect of running transformations — no separate instrumentation required. MLflow extends this to model training, linking dataset versions to model runs and their evaluation metrics.

The Regulatory Threshold Is Here

EU AI Act Article 10 becomes enforceable in August 2026. For high-risk AI systems — which includes applications in healthcare, hiring, credit scoring, and law enforcement — operators are required to document training data lineage. Fines for non-compliance reach up to €35 million, or 7 percent of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.

This is not a distant compliance horizon. Teams working on AI systems in regulated domains need to begin lineage instrumentation before that enforcement date, not at the point of an audit request. Retroactive lineage reconstruction is significantly more expensive than instrumentation from the start.

The Debugging Value Comes Before the Regulatory Value

Before any regulator asks to see your documentation, lineage instrumentation pays for itself in debugging speed. When a model produces an unexpected output, the traditional path involves reconstructing the data history from memory, from ad-hoc queries, and from colleagues who may or may not recall what happened three months ago. With column-level lineage in place, that reconstruction becomes a targeted query.

A significant fraction of the issues we identify trace back to lineage breaks: a transformation that no longer matches its documentation, a join that introduced silent duplication, a feature that was deprecated upstream but is still being consumed downstream.

Where to Start

If you are building lineage infrastructure from scratch, a practical four-step approach:

  • Instrument your most critical pipeline with OpenLineage events — it is tool-agnostic and increasingly well-supported across the data ecosystem.
  • Deploy Marquez or an equivalent backend to store and query those events. Marquez is the OpenLineage reference implementation and requires minimal configuration.
  • Add dbt to your transformation layer if you have not already. Its lineage graph is generated automatically as a side effect of normal operation.
  • Connect MLflow to your training runs to close the lineage loop from raw data to model artifact, enabling end-to-end traceability from source to inference.

For larger organizations, commercial platforms such as Atlan, Alation, and Collibra sit above this stack, providing governance interfaces, policy enforcement, and cross-domain lineage visualization. The open-source layer is sufficient for teams building lineage discipline for the first time.
