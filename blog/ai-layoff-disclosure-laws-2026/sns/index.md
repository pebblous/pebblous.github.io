## LinkedIn (EN)

From October 1, employers in Connecticut filing a mass-layoff notice must state in writing to the Department of Labor whether the layoff is related to AI — the first time in the U.S. that the link between a layoff and AI becomes a legal disclosure rather than a voluntary press release. In May, California's governor signed an executive order directing a dashboard that tracks AI's employment impact from unemployment-insurance data within 90 days, and a pending bill, SB 951, would require 90 days' notice for AI-driven cuts of 25 or more. The language of regulation is moving from what to prohibit toward what to record as data.

But none of the three rules defines what counts as an "AI-driven layoff." The party applying the label is the company being judged by it, and no independent channel verifies the claim. In 2026, "AI" ranked among the top monthly reasons for layoffs even as total layoffs fell 43% year over year, and roughly 90% of executives said AI had virtually no effect on their headcount. A reporting duty adds volume; without a definition, it does not add quality.

Treating labor displacement as measurement rather than argument is progress. It holds only once we settle who applies the label, and by what standard.

## Facebook (EN)

"We cut jobs because of AI."

For months this sentence has been easy to find in corporate press releases.

Starting October 1, in Connecticut, it stops being a voluntary line. An employer filing a mass-layoff notice now has to write, on a form sent to the Department of Labor, whether the layoff is related to AI.

California moved in a similar direction back in May. Rather than regulating companies outright, its executive order asked the state to build a dashboard first, one that reads AI's effect on employment out of unemployment-insurance data.

The language of regulation is shifting from what to prohibit toward what to record. It is a first attempt to hold labor displacement as a number rather than a hunch.

And yet one thing keeps catching me. None of the three rules says what actually counts as an "AI-driven layoff." The party applying the label is the company being judged by it, and there is no independent channel to check the claim. This year "AI" was among the most-cited reasons for layoffs, even as total layoffs fell.

"Once it becomes a filing, does the number become trustworthy?"

That is the question a company that works with data keeps returning to. If no one records who applied the label, and on what basis, then the retraining budgets and re-employment programs built on top of it rest on a shaky signal.

Deciding to measure and actually being able to measure do not yet sit in the same place.
