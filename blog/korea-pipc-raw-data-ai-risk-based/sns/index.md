한국의 개인정보 규제가 오래 지켜온 원칙 하나를 조건부로 풀었습니다. 이름과 얼굴을 가리는 가명처리입니다.

개인정보보호위원회가 7월 3일 발표한 제3차 개인정보 보호 기본계획(2027~2029)은, 적법하게 수집한 영상·음성 데이터라면 가명처리 없이 원본 그대로 AI 학습에 쓸 수 있는 특례를 열었습니다. 얼굴 표정이나 재난 현장의 동선처럼 AI가 배워야 할 신호가 정작 가명처리 과정에서 지워진다는 산업계의 오랜 지적을 받아들인 것입니다.

다만 무조건 허용은 아닙니다. 원본을 쓰려면 개인정보위 심사와 위험요인 평가라는 두 관문을 통과해야 합니다. 규제의 무게중심이 '쓰지 마라'에서 '위험에 걸맞게 관리하라'로 옮겨간 셈입니다.

이 전환은 기업의 방어 논리도 바꿉니다. 지금까지 가장 안전한 답은 '그 데이터를 안 썼다'였지만, 활용이 합법의 영역에 들어오면 방패는 '누가 어떤 데이터를 어떤 절차로 관리하며 썼는가'를 보여주는 증명이 됩니다. 데이터의 출처와 위험 이력을 언제든 펼쳐 보일 수 있는 상태, 곧 데이터 거버넌스와 프로비넌스가 이 대목에서 심사를 통과하기 위한 필요조건이 됩니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Korea just relaxed one of the oldest rules in its privacy regime, and it did so on a condition.

On July 3, the Personal Information Protection Commission's Third Basic Plan (2027–2029) opened an exemption: lawfully collected video and voice data can now feed AI training in raw form, without pseudonymization. The reasoning is one the industry has pressed for years. Masking faces and voices also erases the very signals a model needs to learn, such as a facial expression or the way people move through a disaster scene.

It is not a blanket permit. Raw data must clear two gates first, the PIPC's review and a risk-factor assessment. The center of gravity has moved from "do not use it" to "manage it in proportion to its risk."

That shift rewrites a company's line of defense. The safest answer used to be that it never touched the data. Once use becomes lawful, the shield turns into a demonstration of who used which data, through what assessment, and how. Data governance and provenance stop being nice-to-haves and become a condition for passing the review.

## Facebook (EN)

Picture the moment a face is blurred out of a video.

It is done to protect someone. And yet, in that same moment, the expression fades, the flicker of a glance goes, and so does the trace of how people moved through a disaster scene. The very signal an AI would need to learn disappears under the name of safety.

Korea's Personal Information Protection Commission touched exactly this nerve in the Third Basic Plan it released on July 3. For video and voice gathered lawfully, it opened a conditional path to use the data raw, without pseudonymization. One long-standing safeguard was, in effect, set down.

But an open door is not a free pass. Two checkpoints still stand in front of it: the commission's review and a risk assessment. What changed is the sentence of the rule itself, from "do not use it" to "manage it in proportion to its risk."

Which leaves a quieter question. When "we never used it" is no longer the safest answer, what does a company defend itself with?

The answer is probably a record that says "here is how we managed it." A state in which you can show, at any moment, where the data came from, under what consent it was gathered, and which risk assessment it passed through. That is why Pebblous has spent so long tracing the origin and history of data. This is the moment such proof becomes the infrastructure of compliance rather than an afterthought to it.
