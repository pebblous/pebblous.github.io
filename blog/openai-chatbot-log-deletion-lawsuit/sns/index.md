2026년 7월 9일 뉴욕타임스와 데일리뉴스 등 언론사가 OpenAI에 대한 제재를 뉴욕 남부지방법원에 신청했습니다. 2년 넘게 OpenAI는 학습 데이터와 ChatGPT 출력 로그에서 원고의 저작물을 검색할 능력이 없다고 법원에 진술해 왔습니다. 그러나 지난 4월 증언에서, 회사가 이미 약 7,800만 건 규모의 비식별 대화 데이터셋으로 자사의 침해 규모를 스스로 가늠하고 있었다는 사실이 드러났습니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The company that told the court it couldn't search its data had already built the tool to do exactly that.

On July 9, 2026, The New York Times and other news outlets asked a federal court in the Southern District of New York to sanction OpenAI. For more than two years, the company had maintained that it lacked the ability to search its training data and ChatGPT output logs for the plaintiffs' work. Testimony in April told a different story: OpenAI had already assembled a de-identified dataset of roughly 78 million conversations and was using it internally to gauge the scale of its own infringement.

The court itself put the volume of preserved chatbot logs "in the tens of billions." Yet the 20-million-record sample OpenAI was ordered to produce came back so heavily redacted that the court called it unusable. Before anyone could argue whether infringement happened, the fight became whether the evidence to prove it still exists.

For anyone who runs data, the lesson sits outside copyright law. Whether a training corpus and its output logs were kept searchable and preservable is now a question of evidentiary weight. Data built to be deleted can read, in a courtroom, as data deliberately concealed.

## Facebook (EN)

Deleting data is usually a sign of diligence.

You set a pipeline to erase conversation logs after a few days, for privacy's sake. That is good design.

And yet last week, The New York Times and other outlets asked a court to sanction OpenAI. What they objected to was exactly that deletion.

The logs kept disappearing even after the court had ordered them preserved, and for a long time the company had said it simply could not search that data. Then, under testimony, another fact surfaced: OpenAI had already built a dataset of tens of millions of conversations and was using it to measure the scale of its own infringement.

The same act of deletion reads one way as protecting privacy, and another way as destroying evidence.

"Why was this data erased at that particular moment?" The question a courtroom asks now is no longer whether you deleted it, but whether you can explain how you did.

Data built to be deleted, and data built so that the deletion itself can still be accounted for, look identical on an ordinary day. The moment a preservation order arrives, they split into two very different weights. That, I think, is why observability belongs in the pipeline from the start, not bolted on after the crisis has already begun.
