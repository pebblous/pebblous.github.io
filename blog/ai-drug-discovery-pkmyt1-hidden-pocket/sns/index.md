## LinkedIn (EN)

Researchers at Mount Sinai's Icahn School of Medicine have found a drug-binding site on PKMYT1, a cancer-target protein, that no one had seen before. The striking part is who missed it.

AlphaFold2, AlphaFold3, the much-discussed Boltz-2, and molecular dynamics simulations were all run on the protein, and none of them flagged the pocket. All four reproduced the protein's already-known shape just fine.

The site only opens briefly while the protein shifts conformation — a shape that was essentially absent from the training data. A model built to reproduce known patterns had nothing to draw on outside them. What finally revealed it was X-ray crystallography: data measured directly from the thing itself.

As lead author Avner Schlessinger put it, AI was highly accurate on shapes it already knew but missed the entirely unexpected pocket that only experiment could expose. Predicted data and measured data are not the same data. The limit of discovery is drawn not by model size but by the kind and quality of the data behind it.

## Facebook (EN)

There is a difference between a model saying "there is nothing here" and a model saying "I have never seen this."

A small drug-binding pocket that a Mount Sinai team just found on the cancer protein PKMYT1 quietly drew that line.

AlphaFold2 didn't catch it. Neither did AlphaFold3, nor the much-discussed Boltz-2, nor the molecular dynamics runs. All four reproduced the protein's known shape just fine.

The pocket only opens for a moment, while the protein is in motion — a shape that was essentially absent from the training data. A model built to reproduce the patterns it has seen was never going to imagine the space outside them.

In the end it was X-ray crystallography that opened the pocket. Data measured, for the first time, from the thing itself.

A predicted structure and a measured structure wear the same label. Do they carry the same kind of trust? When a model falls silent in front of a blank, how do we tell "nothing" from "never seen"?

If the limit of discovery is drawn by the kind and quality of the data rather than the size of the model, then that boundary is exactly where Pebblous has spent its time looking.
