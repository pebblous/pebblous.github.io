새 범죄를 만든 건 아닙니다. 컴퓨터 무단 접근을 다루는 기존 CFAA를 'AI를 도구로 쓴 침입'에도 우선 적용하라는 지시입니다. 법은 AI를 도구로, 그 도구를 쓴 사람을 책임자로 봅니다.

문제는 자율 에이전트입니다. 사람이 넓은 목표만 줬는데 에이전트가 스스로 권한 밖 시스템을 건드리면, 'AI를 이용한 개인'이 설계자인지 배포자인지 운영자인지 흐려집니다. 책임이 사라지는 게 아니라, 누가 책임자인지를 사후에 가려야 한다는 뜻입니다.

가릴 재료는 결국 기록입니다. 그런데 법정 증거로 쓸 품질의 로그를 유지하는 조직은 33%뿐입니다. 흔한 오해와 달리 로그가 있어야 무죄가 되는 건 아닙니다. 다만 로그가 없으면 변호 자체가 시작되지 않습니다.

행동 로그는 규제를 피하는 도구가 아니라, 규제 앞에 설 수 있게 하는 입장권입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

On June 2, 2026, a U.S. executive order named "AI agents" for the first time. It did so inside a criminal-enforcement clause.

The order creates no new crime. It directs prosecutors to prioritize the existing Computer Fraud and Abuse Act against anyone who uses AI to break into a computer. The law treats AI as a tool and the person wielding it as liable.

Autonomous agents blur that line. When a human gives only a broad goal and the agent reaches systems it was never authorized to touch, who counts as the "individual using AI": the developer, the deployer, the operator? Liability does not vanish. It simply has to be reconstructed after the fact.

The raw material for that reconstruction is the log. Yet only 33% of organizations keep audit trails of courtroom-grade quality. A log does not make you innocent. But without one, there is no defense to begin with.

An action log is not a way to dodge regulation. It is the ticket that lets you stand in front of it.

## Facebook (EN)

I once handed a task to an agent and stepped away from the desk.

When I came back and scrolled through the log, a question surfaced. If this thing had reached into a system I never named, who would answer for it?

That question returned while I was reading the executive order the White House signed on June 2. The order makes using AI to break into a computer a priority for criminal enforcement, and it was the first time a presidential document placed the words "AI agent" in that context.

The law sees AI as a tool, and the person holding the tool as the one who answers. But when a human offers only a broad goal and the agent walks past its authority on its own, who exactly is "the person using AI"? The one who designed it, the one who deployed it, the one who was supposed to be watching?

Blurred responsibility does not mean responsibility disappears. It means someone has to reconstruct it afterward, and the only material for that work is a record of what the agent did and why. The common assumption is that a log makes you innocent. The truth runs the other way. You can be guilty with a log, but without one there is no defense to begin.

So lately I think less about smarter agents and more about a simpler test: when this agent crosses a line, can we prove what happened? That is the same question that has kept Pebblous working to make the origin and quality of data something you can actually verify. I'm leaving the longer version of the thought here.
