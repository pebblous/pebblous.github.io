이름은 Frontier Company. 별도 법인을 세운 게 아니라 대부분 기존 직원을 재편해 고객 현장에 상주시키는 구조다. 그런데 발표에서 성능만큼 자주 등장한 단어가 따로 있었다. 고객의 데이터와 IP를 모델 학습에 절대 쓰지 않겠다는 약속이다.

배포의 진짜 병목은 엔지니어 수가 아니다. 그 위에 올릴 데이터가 거버넌스도 격리도 갖추지 못하면 6천 명이 들어가도 배포는 다시 막힌다.

사티아 나델라가 말한 '사회적 허가'의 첫 조건은 결국 남의 데이터를 먹어치우지 않는 것이다. 배포 전쟁의 진짜 판돈은 모델이 아니라 고객의 데이터 주권이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Microsoft just placed 6,000 AI engineers inside its customers' organizations. The price tag is $2.5 billion.

The unit is called Frontier Company. Rather than a spin-off, it mostly redeploys existing staff to sit on-site with clients. Yet one phrase appeared in the announcement as often as performance did: a pledge never to use customer data or IP to train its models.

This is not one company's play. In May, OpenAI committed $4B and Anthropic $1.5B; on June 30 AWS put down $1B, and two days later Microsoft followed. Four players bet on the same square within six weeks. As frontier models converged on enterprise tasks, the contest slid down from the model to the ability to finish deployment inside someone else's org.

The real bottleneck is not headcount. If the data underneath lacks governance and isolation, deployment stalls even with 6,000 engineers on the ground.

Satya Nadella called it "societal permission." Its first condition is simple: don't consume your customer's intelligence. The real stake in the deployment war is not the model. It is data sovereignty.

## Facebook (EN)

"Are they training my competitors on my data?"

When a company sits across from an AI vendor, the question it swallows is often this one, not the one about performance.

Last week Microsoft said it would spend $2.5 billion to place 6,000 AI engineers inside its customers' organizations.

What stayed with me was a phrase that showed up as often as performance did in the announcement: a promise to protect the customer's own data.

Within six weeks, OpenAI, Anthropic, and AWS had each staked billions on the same ground. As the top models grew hard to tell apart, the contest quietly shifted to who can finish deployment inside someone else's house.

But finishing deployment assumes the data underneath is ready. Six thousand engineers can walk in, and still the work stalls if that data has neither governance nor isolation.

This is the question Pebblous has long held under the name AI-Ready Data.

Satya Nadella called it "societal permission." If the first condition of trust is not consuming someone else's intelligence, then one question is left standing.

"Is our data ready to be deployed, and ready to be kept apart?"
