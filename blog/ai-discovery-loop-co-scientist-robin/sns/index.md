## LinkedIn (EN)

Two research teams that never cited each other published their own "discovery loops" in the same week of Nature. Google DeepMind's Co-Scientist and FutureHouse's Robin took opposite paths, one a general-purpose multi-agent system and the other a biology-specific pipeline, yet both arrived at the same place: setting a hypothesis and designing the experiment to test it, on their own. Robin proposed 30 candidates for dry macular degeneration and narrowed them to 2; a cancer drug flagged by Co-Scientist cut a target response by 91% in a liver-organoid assay. But both papers repeated the same line: every experiment and every validation was run by people and instruments, and humans stay in the loop.

What was automated is idea generation, not truth. The catch is that each turn's output becomes the next turn's input, so contaminated intermediate data doesn't stay a single error; it is absorbed as the premise of the next hypothesis and compounds. That is why the real bottleneck of autonomous science is not a bigger model but data validation. It is the same problem Pebblous has been diagnosing with DataClinic.

## Facebook (EN)

Thirty candidate compounds are sitting on a researcher's screen as a list.

Not one a person pulled together after nights in the literature. A machine proposed the whole list overnight.

That scene ran twice in Nature this past May. Google DeepMind's Co-Scientist and FutureHouse's Robin. Two teams that never even cited each other, each unveiling its own "discovery loop" in the same week.

The loop turns like this. It reads the literature, forms a hypothesis, designs the experiment to test it, then reads the result and moves to the next hypothesis. That is how Robin narrowed thirty candidates down to two.

But who actually ran the experiments?

Shaking the vials and watching the cells still fell to people and instruments. Both papers repeated the same sentence: humans stay in the loop, always.

What stayed with me was a quieter worry. When the loop completes one turn, that turn's output becomes the next turn's input. Let one error through, and the next hypothesis is already built on a false premise. The speed of automation becomes, exactly, the speed at which contamination spreads.

So the real bottleneck of autonomous science may not be a bigger model.

It may be the plainer work of checking whether the data circling the loop stands on fact. That is the work Pebblous has been doing with DataClinic, measuring the quality of data.

AI can now generate ideas as fast as we like. Confirming that an idea is true is still, for now, ours to do.
