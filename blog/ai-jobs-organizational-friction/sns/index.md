## LinkedIn (EN)

Three in four adults fear losing their jobs to AI. Yet a survey of 6,000 executives found that more than 80% of firms reported no measurable employment impact from AI over the past three years.

The forecasts weren't wrong. The wave simply hasn't arrived. Brookings pins the delay on a single cause: what has shielded workers so far isn't labor regulation, bargaining power, or the cost of AI, but organizational friction, the sheer inertia of firms that can't yet put the technology to work.

The catch is that removing friction is exactly what generative AI is for. Today's shield is tomorrow's target. The thinnest part has already given way, with early-career hiring in AI-exposed roles falling by as much as 16%.

If the buffers meant to replace that friction aren't designed now, we are left with no shield and no alternative. The time that friction has bought us is the only budget we have.

그런데 브루킹스의 보고서를 읽으며 생각이 조금 달라졌습니다. 지금 노동자를 지켜 온 것이 노동조합도, 규제도, AI가 비싸서도 아니라, 그저 조직이 굼떠서라는 겁니다. 새 기술을 실제 현장에서 굴리기까지 걸리는 시간, 그 '마찰' 하나가 방패였던 셈입니다.

그래서 이 글이 남기는 질문은 'AI가 얼마나 강한가'가 아니라, 마찰이 벌어 준 이 시간에 우리가 무엇을 지을 것인가에 가깝습니다.

## Facebook (EN)

The headlines say AI is coming for our jobs. But look around, and it is oddly quiet.

Three in four adults say they fear losing work to AI. Yet when researchers asked 6,000 executives, more than eighty percent said the past three years had brought no measurable change to their headcount.

For a while I read that silence as a simple "not yet." The forecasts weren't wrong; the wave just hadn't landed.

Then a line in a Brookings report shifted something for me. What has protected workers, it argues, isn't unions or regulation or the price of AI. It's friction, the plain slowness of organizations that can't yet put the technology to work.

And here is the unsettling part. Removing that friction is precisely what AI is built to do.

"So what remains once the shield is gone?"

The thinnest spot has already worn through. Entry-level hiring in AI-exposed roles is quietly falling, and the young people looking for a first job are being pushed out in ways the statistics barely register.

Making data into something AI can actually be trusted to run is part of that same shift, and it's the ground Pebblous has worked on for years.

The question this piece leaves me with isn't how powerful AI is. It's what we choose to build in the time that friction has bought us.
