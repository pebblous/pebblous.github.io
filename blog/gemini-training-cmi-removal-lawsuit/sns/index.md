2026년 7월 하치엣·엘스비어 등 출판사와 소설가 스콧 터로가 구글을 뉴욕남부지법에 제소하며 앞세운 것은, 학습에 썼다는 사실을 감추려고 책의 저작권 관리 정보(CMI)를 의도적으로 지웠다는 DMCA §1202(b) 위반 주장이었다.

무단 사용은 "이 데이터를 써도 되는가"를 묻고, 출처 삭제는 "썼다는 사실을 추적할 수 있는가"를 묻는다. 법적으로 별개의 청구다.

다만 2025년 Kadrey v. Meta에서 법원은 "학습이 공정이용이면 그 출처를 지운 것도 문제 삼을 수 없다"는 논리로 거의 같은 청구를 기각했다. 출처 삭제의 운명이 공정이용 판단 하나에 통째로 매여 버린 셈이다.

그러나 데이터를 다루는 쪽에서 보면 순서가 뒤바뀐다. 출처가 지워진 학습 데이터는 소송의 승패와 무관하게 감사할 수 없는 데이터가 되기 때문이다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The hardest claim in the publishers' new lawsuit against Google isn't that Gemini was trained on their books without permission.

In July 2026, Hachette, Elsevier and others, joined by novelist Scott Turow, sued Google in the Southern District of New York, leading with a different charge: that Google deliberately stripped the copyright management information (CMI) from those books to erase any trace of what went into training, a violation of DMCA §1202(b).

The two claims answer different questions. Unauthorized use asks whether the data may be used; source removal asks whether that use can still be traced.

There is a catch. In 2025, Kadrey v. Meta dismissed a nearly identical claim, reasoning that if the training is fair use, erasing its source cannot be actionable either. The removal claim's fate was tied wholesale to the fair-use ruling.

But from the data side the order flips. Once the provenance marks are gone, the training data becomes impossible to audit, no matter who wins the case.

## Facebook (EN)

A lawsuit saying "an AI trained on my book" barely counts as news anymore.

But reading the complaint publishers filed against Google this summer, I stopped at one line.

The charge they pressed hardest was not "used without permission" but "erased the traces of using it."

Titles, author names, the copyright information attached to a book, all stripped away before training. Once that is gone, so is the clue to which books ever entered the training corpus.

Whether the claim wins in court is genuinely uncertain. A nearly identical argument was dismissed last year on the logic that if the training is fair use, erasing the source cannot be a problem either.

Still, from someone who has spent years working with data, the question lands a little differently.

"When the provenance is gone, what is left to audit the data with?"

Win or lose, training data whose source marks have been erased becomes data you can no longer retrace. The ground for telling lawful use apart from quiet use simply disappears.

That is why we treat recording the source and lineage of training data as a question of quality, not paperwork. The verdict has to wait; a pipeline that keeps its provenance does not.
