Gartner expects 60% of enterprise AI projects to be scrapped this year. The culprit isn't the model. It's data quality.

Clario, a startup founded by five-time CIO Yousuf Khan, just raised $6M in seed funding to take that diagnosis head-on. Instead of building a bigger model, it clears the redundant, obsolete, and trivial (ROT) data piling up inside enterprises. In partner audits, up to 60% of corporate data qualified.

The product connects to Google Drive, SharePoint, and Confluence, flags ROT candidates with metadata heuristics, and routes keep-or-delete decisions through Slack and Teams.

But cleaning is necessary, not sufficient. Strip out the garbage and the data left behind, without structure, context, or labels, still behaves like 'clean garbage' in front of a RAG pipeline or an LLM.

Deleting garbage and making data usable point the same way, but they aren't the same job. The distance to AI-ready lives between them.

So far, hard to argue with. But I keep pausing one step further.

"Once the garbage is gone, is what's left actually ready for AI?"

Deleting garbage and making data usable look like the same direction. They are not the same work. When Pebblous talks about AI-Ready Data, it means the room you walk into after the cleaning is done.

### A $6M Bet That Your AI Problem Is Really a Data Problem

For four years, enterprises have poured billions into AI and struggled to show much for it. Gartner now expects 60% of AI projects to be scrapped this year, and the reason it names is not the model. It is data quality. RAND found in 2024 that more than 80% of AI projects never reach their intended business value. MIT's Project NANDA reported that 95% of generative-AI pilots produced no measurable effect on the bottom line. According to S&P Global Market Intelligence, the share of companies abandoning most of their AI initiatives jumped from 17% in 2024 to 42% in 2025.

The common thread is simple: the bottleneck isn't the algorithm. It's what goes into the algorithm.

A startup called Clario came out of stealth in June 2026 with a $6M seed round led by Preface Ventures, with Ridge Ventures, Rain Capital, Transform VC, and others joining — eleven investors in all. Its CEO and co-founder, Yousuf Khan, has been a CIO five times over. Its CTO, Madhu Vohra, led engineering at Oracle OCI, NetApp, and Nutanix. The company calls itself the first dedicated platform built to eliminate enterprise data ROT.

### What ROT actually is

ROT stands for redundant, obsolete, and trivial data — the stuff companies keep but no longer use. The MP3 someone downloaded years ago, the manual for a discontinued product, a legacy format that no longer opens, the movie an ex-employee parked on the company drive. Clario's read is that roughly 78% of stored enterprise data is unstructured, and conservatively more than a third of that is effectively garbage. In actual partner audits, the garbage share climbed as high as 60%. Veritas, in its Global Databerg Report, has classified about 85% of enterprise data as ROT or dark data.

### How Clario works

The product moves in four steps. It connects to existing content systems — Google Drive, SharePoint, OneDrive, Box, Confluence. It scans metadata such as file checksums, naming patterns, last-access times, and format support to surface ROT candidates. It routes those candidates into Slack or Teams so a human approves keep, archive, or delete, charging only when a decision happens — a pay-per-decision model. As decisions accumulate, it learns a recurring cleanup routine.

One detail matters here. The detection rests on metadata heuristics. AI embedding analysis that reads what a file actually means is still on the roadmap. So what Clario does well today is remove the obvious garbage quickly and safely. That alone has clear value. Preface Ventures' Saad Siddiqui calls Clario the only company genuinely doing the work of letting enterprises start from an AI-ready foundation.

### The question after the cleaning

Suppose the garbage is gone. Drives are lighter, storage costs drop, search gets faster. Whether the remaining data lets an AI answer well is a separate question. What's usually left after a cleanup is:

- Thousands of Word documents with no labels and no taxonomy
- PDF reports carrying no metadata beyond a title
- Confluence pages scattered with no links between them
- Policy documents where no one can tell which version is current

None of this is ROT. It is valuable data, which is exactly why Clario's metadata heuristics leave it untouched. The trouble starts when that perfectly fine data is pushed straight into a RAG pipeline or an LLM. Without structure, context, and labels, the model has no way to know which document is the authoritative latest version, or in what context a sentence was written. The result drifts back toward garbage in — or more precisely, clean garbage.

Khan knows this edge himself. He has described internal agents and RAG systems running over old policies, discontinued product docs, and retired support material, where "the LLM burns its compute budget filtering out the noise." Clario reduces that noise. But reducing noise and making the signal usable are different jobs.

### Cleanup versus readiness

Making data usable for AI splits into two stages. The first is cleanup — taking out the garbage — and that is Clario's domain. The second is preparation — turning what remains into something an AI can read. They point the same way, but they are not the same work:

- **Cleanup (subtraction):** remove duplicates, delete obsolete docs, clear legacy formats, handle trivial and untouched files.
- **Preparation (construction):** add schema and taxonomy, connect provenance and context, attach semantic labels, make content embeddable, and manage versions, ownership, and lineage.

What Clario calls a "baseline level of AI-ready" is, more precisely, the starting point of that journey. The product removes what blocks AI; it does not yet make data that AI can use. The distance between those two is the distance between stopping garbage in and producing genuine readiness.

That leaves every organization with one question. Where is our data right now — still being cleaned, or already cleaned and still failing to answer? The two call for entirely different tools and budgets. A cleaning tool can't solve a preparation problem, and a preparation tool can't do the cleaning.

When Pebblous talks about AI-Ready Data, it points at that second stage. The arrival of a $6M company on the "data over model" thesis isn't a competitive threat so much as a signal that the market is moving toward the same problem. Push that thesis all the way through, and preparation always follows the cleanup.
