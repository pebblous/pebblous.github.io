## LinkedIn (EN)

Up to 45% of survey responses collected from real people may not have been written by people at all.

That's the finding of an analysis published in Nature in June 2026. One psychologist flagged a response that read, "I don't experience confusion in the same way humans do," a telltale sign that someone had pasted a chatbot's answer. On crowdsourcing platforms where faster answers mean more pay, copying an AI reply has quietly become the rational move. Harder still: some researchers now build "silicon samples," AI stand-ins for human respondents, tuned to produce the result they want.

Data quality just gained a new axis. For years it meant accuracy, completeness, consistency. Now authenticity sits alongside them: was this written by a human at all? That is why genuinely human data is becoming scarce, and valuable.

## Facebook (EN)

"I don't experience confusion in the same way humans do."

A psychologist found that line sitting inside a survey response, an answer to a question about the respondent's own feelings.

She had asked a person. What came back was not quite a person. By her estimate, as much as 45% of the responses carried the same fingerprint.

It isn't hard to see why. On crowdsourced surveys, where answering faster means earning more, pasting a chatbot's reply has quietly become the reasonable thing to do. The people collecting the data believe they bought human opinion. More and more, what arrives is machine prose.

What stayed with me was the other side of it. Some researchers now summon entire panels of "synthetic respondents," AI stand-ins dialed to any age or leaning they please. One of them called the practice hard to distinguish from fraud.

So an old question turns heavy again. "Is this data really human?" Next to asking whether a value is correct, we now have to ask who, or what, produced it.

In an age when anyone can generate plausible sentences without end, the trace a person actually leaves becomes rare, and worth more. Checking whether the data we gathered from people truly came from people may be the quietest new discipline of all.
