대신 규제 대상이 된 것은 'AI 모델'이 아니라 '개인정보를 처리해 점수·순위·추천을 만들어 사람에 대한 결정에 영향을 주는 기술(ADMT)'입니다. 규제의 단위가 모델의 정교함에서, 데이터가 결정으로 흐르는 경로로 내려온 것입니다.

이유는 현실적입니다. 무엇을 'AI 시스템'이라 부를지부터 합의되지 않으니, 정의가 넓으면 계산기까지 규제망에 걸리고 좁히면 빠져나갈 구멍이 생깁니다. 그래서 콜로라도는 규제의 닻을 모델이 아니라 "개인정보를 처리해 중대한 결정에 영향을 주는가"라는 측정 가능한 자리에 내렸습니다.

남는 한계도 있습니다. 차별 방지 영향평가 같은 무거운 사전 의무가 사라지고 투명성과 정정 권리 중심으로 가벼워졌다는 점은 보호 후퇴라는 비판도 받습니다.

데이터를 다루는 입장에서 이 사건은 한 줄로 읽힙니다. AI 거버넌스의 실제 통제점은 모델 위가 아니라, 어떤 데이터가 어떻게 처리돼 결정이 되는가의 데이터 레이어라는 것입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Today, June 30, 2026, was supposed to be the day America's first comprehensive AI law took effect. Instead, six weeks before that date, Colorado repealed the law outright and struck the word "AI" from the replacement statute.

What the new law regulates is not an "AI model" but automated decision-making technology (ADMT): systems that process personal data to produce scores, rankings, or recommendations influencing consequential decisions about people. The unit of regulation moved from the sophistication of the model to the path along which data becomes a decision.

The reason is practical. There is no settled definition of an "AI system": cast it broadly and calculators get caught, cast it narrowly and loopholes open. So Colorado anchored regulation to something measurable: whether personal data is processed into a consequential decision.

The trade-off is real. The old law's heavy duties, such as discrimination impact assessments, are gone, replaced by lighter transparency and correction rights that some read as a rollback of protections.

For anyone who works with data, the event reads as a single line. The real control point of AI governance is not the model but the data layer — which data is processed, and how it becomes a decision.

## Facebook (EN)

When I looked at today's date on the calendar, one thing came back to me.

June 30, 2026. This was supposed to be the day America's first law for regulating AI took effect.

It never did. Six weeks before today, Colorado let the law go. Not by amending it, but by repealing it whole and putting an entirely different law in its place. The first comprehensive AI law in the United States never held force for a single day.

The replacement statute does not contain the word "AI." That word was removed on purpose.

In its place sits a longer, drier phrase: "technology that processes personal data to produce scores and rankings that influence consequential decisions about people."

At first I wondered why anyone would say it so indirectly. The longer I sat with it, the more honest it seemed.

"What do we even call AI?"

No one answers that cleanly. Define it broadly and a calculator is caught; define it narrowly and the loopholes open. So Colorado stopped reaching for the blurry word and went down to something it could measure: which data is processed, and which decision it flows into.

Regulation came down from the abstraction of the model to the path of the data.

As someone who has spent a long time with data, this is the image that stayed with me. The place where responsibility can finally be located was never how clever the model is. It was which data, processed how, became the decision.

Today is a day to ask what that empty space, where the word "AI" used to be, is pointing at.
