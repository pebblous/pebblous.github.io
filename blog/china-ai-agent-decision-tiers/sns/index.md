## LinkedIn (KO)

어떤 판단은 사람에게 남기고, 어떤 판단은 승인을 받게 하고, 어떤 판단만 에이전트가 스스로 하게 한다. 7월 15일, 중국이 이 세 줄을 법으로 못박았다.

국가인터넷정보판공실 등 3개 부처가 낸 《지능체 실시의견》은 AI 에이전트만을 독립된 규율 대상으로 삼은 세계 첫 국가 정책 문서다. 제6조는 에이전트를 배치하기 전에 그 결정 권한을 사람만·승인 후·자율 세 등급으로 나눠 두라고 요구한다. 등급을 가르는 기준은 두 가지다. 그 행위가 얼마나 민감하고, 얼마나 되돌릴 수 있는가.

어려운 건 등급을 나누는 일이 아니라 증명하는 일이다. 나중에 감사관이 "이 결정이 어느 등급이었고 사용자가 정말 승인했는가"를 물으면, 그 답은 코드에도 정책 문서에도 없다. 행위 하나하나의 결과와 근거, 승인 기록이 데이터 계층에 남아 있어야만 재구성된다.

그래서 등급을 나눈다는 것의 진짜 의미는, 나눈 등급을 증명할 데이터 인프라를 먼저 갖추라는 요구에 가깝다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Some decisions stay with a human. Some need the user's sign-off. Some the agent may take on its own. On July 15, China wrote those three lines into law.

Issued by the CAC and two other ministries, the Implementation Opinions on AI Agents is the first national policy anywhere to regulate AI agents as a distinct category. Article 6 requires companies to sort an agent's decision authority into three tiers before deployment, judged by how sensitive and how reversible each action is.

The hard part isn't drawing the tiers. It's proving them. When an auditor later asks which tier a decision fell under, and whether the user actually approved it, the answer lives neither in the code nor in the policy document. It can only be reconstructed from a record of each action's outcome, its grounds, and its sign-off.

Sorting autonomy into tiers, then, is really a demand to build the data infrastructure that can prove those tiers after the fact.

## Facebook (EN)

When you hand a task to an agent, a small question stays with you: how far should it go on its own?

Sorting your calendar is fine.

But moving money between accounts, or signing a contract, on its own?

On July 15, China drew that very line into law. Some decisions must stay with a person. Some the agent may propose, but not execute until you approve. Some it may handle on its own, within a scope you set. Three tiers of decision authority. The test is unexpectedly plain: how sensitive is the action, and how reversible.

What stayed with me wasn't the drawing of the tiers.

It was a later question. When someone asks whether a decision really fell under the tier it claimed, and whether the user truly approved it, what do you answer with? Not the code. Not a well-written policy document. Only a record of what the agent did, what it relied on, and who signed off can redraw that moment.

Pebblous kept circling the same spot in an earlier piece, "an agent doesn't inherit the entitlements of its source." Setting a permission and later showing it was honored turn out to be farther apart than they look.

Sorting autonomy into tiers starts to sound like a quieter request: first, keep the record that can prove the tiers.
