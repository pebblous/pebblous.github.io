## LinkedIn (EN)

Anthropic was valued at $965 billion, passing OpenAI ($852 billion) for the first time.

What flipped the ranking wasn't a smarter model. Anthropic's consumer base is roughly 5% the size of ChatGPT's, yet its annualized revenue runs ahead of OpenAI's.

The difference sits in where the money comes from. About 80% of Anthropic's revenue is enterprise, and enterprise contracts embed into workflows and rarely churn, so investors pay a higher multiple for them. What underwrites that stickiness is not price or performance but data trust.

Not training on customer data, auditable logs, compliance that clears regulated industries. Eight of the Fortune 10 chose Claude for that reason.

As model performance converges, the variable that sets AI valuations apart shifts to who customers trust with their data.

## Facebook (EN)

When a buyer at a regulated enterprise sits down to evaluate an AI vendor, what is the first thing they ask?

Not the benchmark score, it turns out.

"Can we trust them with our data?" In finance, healthcare, and government, that is the question where the buyer actually pauses.

Last month Anthropic was valued at $965 billion and passed OpenAI for the first time. The interesting part is what drove it. For every twenty people using ChatGPT, only about one uses Anthropic day to day, and yet Anthropic came out ahead on revenue. The reason: roughly 80% of that revenue is enterprise.

Enterprise contracts, once they settle into a workflow, rarely come loose. And when you ask what holds that stickiness in place, the answer keeps returning to data trust. A promise not to train on customer data, logs you can audit, compliance that clears the regulators. Eight of the Fortune 10 chose Claude for the same reason.

For a long time the line has been that the moat in AI is not the model but data trust. This reversal reads like the market finally putting a price on that line. As models converge, maybe the next question moves away from the bigger model and toward the data you can actually trust.
