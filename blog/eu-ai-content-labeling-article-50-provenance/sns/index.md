## LinkedIn (KO)

EU AI Act Article 50이 이날 집행을 시작한다. 딥페이크, 공익 사안과 관련된 AI 생성 텍스트, 챗봇은 "이것은 기계가 만들었거나 조작했다"를 밝혀야 한다. 6월 10일 유럽위원회가 확정한 실행 강령은 방법까지 못 박았다. 문구 한 줄로는 부족하고, C2PA 암호 서명 메타데이터와 비가시 워터마킹을 겹쳐 콘텐츠 자체에 출처를 새겨야 한다.

적용 대상은 EU 기업만이 아니다. EU 사용자에게 서비스하는 한국 기업도 본사가 어디든 의무를 진다.

여기서 진짜 전환이 일어난다. 그동안 자발적 관행이던 출처 메타데이터가 감사 제출용 법적 증거로 격상된다. 데이터의 족보가 비용이 아니라 자산이 되는 순간이다.

질문은 단순하다. 우리 조직의 데이터는 "어디서, 어떻게, 누가 만들었는지"를 증명할 수 있는가.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Starting August 2, failing to label AI-generated content can cost a company up to 1.5% of global revenue.

That is the day EU AI Act Article 50 takes effect. Deepfakes, AI-generated text on matters of public interest, and chatbots will all have to disclose that a machine made or altered them. The Code of Practice the European Commission finalized on June 10 goes further and fixes the method: a single line of text is not enough, so provenance must be inscribed into the content itself through C2PA cryptographic signatures layered with invisible watermarking.

This reaches well beyond Europe. Any Korean company serving EU users is bound by it, no matter where headquarters sits.

The real shift is quieter. Provenance metadata, until now a voluntary governance practice, becomes legal evidence you submit to an audit. A data lineage stops being a cost line and starts being an asset.

The question Article 50 asks is plain: can your organization prove where its data came from, how it was made, and who made it?

## Facebook (EN)

Can a machine tell apart a sentence written by a person and one written by another machine?

Starting August 2, Europe has decided the answer has to be yes.

That is when Article 50, the transparency clause of the EU AI Act, takes effect. Deepfakes, AI-generated text on matters of public interest, and chatbots will each carry a mark: a machine made this.

What stayed with me was not the label itself, but how the label is inscribed.

A single line of text is not enough, they say. One screenshot, one format conversion, and it falls away. So the Code of Practice finalized on June 10 asks for something sturdier — a cryptographic signature and an invisible watermark, layered inside the content itself.

Engraving "who made this" onto every piece of content, in a form a machine can read.

The problem we have long called "data provenance" had quietly climbed into the language of regulation.

For years, keeping a record of origin was simply good practice. After August 2, it becomes evidence you place in front of an auditor. The point where a data lineage turns from a cost into an asset.

"Can our organization prove where its data came from, how it was made, and who made it?"

There are about 45 days left.
