## LinkedIn (EN)

Two AI research agents landed in Nature on the same day: Google DeepMind's Co-Scientist and FutureHouse's Robin.

Given dry age-related macular degeneration as input, Robin scanned roughly 400 papers and singled out ripasudil, a glaucoma drug, as a new treatment candidate. No prior study had recorded that link. A real result.

But the two systems share a limit that rarely makes the headlines: both form hypotheses only within open-access papers. About 64% of biomedical literature sits behind paywalls, outside their field of view. The editorial that ran alongside them put it plainly — greater efficiency is not the same as greater insight.

The ceiling of automated discovery is set not by how clever the model is, but by the boundary of the data it can read.

## Facebook (EN)

Imagine a colleague who reads fast, combines vast amounts of material, and finds links no person had managed to draw.

It actually happened. Given a blinding eye disease as input, FutureHouse's Robin scanned nearly 400 open papers and named a glaucoma drug as a new treatment candidate. No human researcher had written that connection down.

The same day, Nature carried another AI research agent, Co-Scientist. The two systems are quite different in design, yet they draw their hypotheses from the same well. Both reason only inside "open" papers.

And about 64% of biomedical literature stays locked behind paywalls. The fields closest to new drugs, like pharmacology, are the most tightly shut. However clever the AI becomes, it cannot form a hypothesis about a paper it cannot see.

Maybe that is why the editorial running alongside the two papers put it so plainly: efficiency is not the same as insight.

What sets the ceiling of automated discovery turned out to be not the cleverness of the model, but the boundary of the data we have made readable. So who opens the data that stays closed, and who verifies that it can be trusted.
