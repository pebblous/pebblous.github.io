## LinkedIn (EN)

AlphaFold solved a still photograph of a protein. But a protein's function lives in how that shape keeps moving.

The reason motion remains unsolved is data, not models. AlphaFold succeeded because PDB gave it more than 220,000 experimentally verified structures as ground truth. Protein dynamics has no equivalent canonical trajectory dataset. The public dynamics data is three to four orders of magnitude smaller than the static structures.

So teams are making the data that doesn't exist yet. BioEmu runs its own supercomputer simulations to build training material; DiffEnsemble mines the traces of motion latent inside static structures with a diffusion model.

With no ground truth, there is also no standard to check whether the generated data is real. The next frontier of AI-Ready Data is not data that is hard to clean, but data that isn't there at all.

## Facebook (EN)

I still remember the first time I heard that getting a single protein structure had gone from months to minutes.

What AlphaFold solved was, without question, a hard and beautiful problem.

But that picture is closer to a still photograph. A protein never sits still inside a cell. It folds and unfolds, opens and closes, meets another molecule and briefly becomes a different shape. Most of the moments when a drug works or an enzyme does its job happen on top of that motion.

So why is the motion still unsolved?

The answer that stayed with me was not about a better model. AlphaFold stood on PDB, a rare and clean dataset where more than 220,000 verified structures had piled up as ground truth. Protein dynamics has no such canon. The answers simply don't exist in the world yet, not in enough number.

So teams are making the missing data themselves. They run their own simulations; they mine the traces of movement hidden inside static structures. And a quiet shadow follows. With no ground truth, there is still no way to tell whether the data they made is real.

Not data that is hard to clean, but data that isn't there at all. That, I think, is the next question a company that has spent its days on data quality walks into. When there is no answer to point to, what do we set as the ground for learning?
