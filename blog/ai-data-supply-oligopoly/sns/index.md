## LinkedIn (EN)

More than 50 companies sell the training data and RL environments that AI labs run on. Over 75% of the revenue goes to just four of them: Scale AI, Surge AI, Mercor, and Handshake.

The market looks crowded, but a buyer shopping for a vendor at scale effectively has four options. As frontier work shifts from static labeling to reinforcement-learning environments, the lineup hasn't reshuffled. The same four that led in labeling moved one rung up and took the new layer too.

The concentration has already been tested. When Meta bought 49% of Scale for $14.3 billion in June 2025, Google cut its spending and OpenAI ended its partnership. But the volume that left Scale flowed to the remaining three, mostly Mercor. The four-way structure held. It was a transfer, not an escape.

When supply sits with four vendors, so does control over data quality, provenance, and bias. Diversifying across vendors isn't enough when all four converge; what buyers need is control over their own data lineage.

## Facebook (EN)

A market map went around my feed a few days ago.

It laid out the fifty-odd companies that sell AI training data on a single screen. What held me wasn't the count of logos. It was the small figure beside it: three-quarters of the revenue flowing to just four of them.

Scale, Surge, Mercor, Handshake. If this is a gold rush, the labs are the ones digging, and these four are the smithies selling the picks. Somehow the smithies narrowed to four before the diggers did.

Last summer the structure made a sound. When Meta bought into Scale, rival labs pulled their work, and that work didn't leave the market. It moved to the remaining three. They changed vendors; they didn't leave the dependency.

One question stayed with me. "Can I explain, on my own, where my data came from and how it was checked?" If only four companies sell it, they hold the key to that answer too.

We've grown used to saying data is an asset. Maybe the phrase only holds up once we ask who gets to set the asset's spec. That's the part Pebblous keeps working on — putting a buyer's data lineage and quality back in the buyer's own hands.
