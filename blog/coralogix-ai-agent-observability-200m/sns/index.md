## LinkedIn (KO)

관측 가능성 회사 Coralogix가 Series F로 2억 달러를 받았다. 밸류에이션은 16억 달러. 그런데 이 돈이 향한 곳은 로그를 모으는 전통적 모니터링이 아니라, "AI 에이전트가 무슨 짓을 하고 있는지" 들여다보는 새로운 층이었다.

이유는 에이전트가 다르게 실패하기 때문이다. 전통 소프트웨어는 망가지면 에러를 던져 로그에 흔적을 남기지만, 에이전트는 자신만만하게 틀린 답을 내고 같은 작업을 무한히 반복하면서도 에러를 남기지 않는다. 응답 시간도 정상, 에러율도 0인데 결정은 틀려 있다. 기존 APM이 잡지 못하는 실패다.

지금 조직의 57%가 에이전트를 운영에 올렸지만, 같은 조사에서 관측 가능성은 AI 스택에서 가장 낮은 점수를 받았다. 쓰는 속도가 지켜보는 능력을 한참 앞질렀다. 캐나다 연금투자위원회(CPPIB) 같은 기관 자본이 이 빈자리에 들어왔다는 건, 에이전트 감시를 한철 트렌드가 아니라 구조적 수요로 봤다는 신호에 가깝다.

자동화는 코드로 끝나지만, 신뢰는 그 코드가 무엇을 하는지 계속 보일 때만 성립한다. 관측 가능성 없는 자율성은 위임이 아니라 방치다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Coralogix, an observability company, just raised a $200M Series F at a $1.6B valuation. The money isn't chasing the old business of pulling logs together. It's funding a newer layer: watching what AI agents are actually doing.

Agents fail differently. Traditional software throws an error when it breaks and leaves a trace in the log. An agent gives a confidently wrong answer, repeats a task endlessly, or calls a needless tool, all while response times look normal and error rates sit at zero. Conventional APM never sees it.

57% of organizations now run agents in production, yet in the same survey observability scored lowest of any layer in their AI stack. Adoption has outrun the ability to watch. When institutional capital like the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB) backs that gap, it reads agent monitoring as structural demand, not a passing trend.

Automation ends with code. Trust holds only when you can keep seeing what that code does. Autonomy without observability isn't delegation; it's neglect.

## Facebook (EN)

Picture an engineer closing the dashboard and, instead, asking an AI assistant: "How are the systems doing right now?"

Coralogix's CEO calls this the slow dissolving of the interface layer. More than half of the company's enterprise customers, he says, already work with their operational data through a command line or an agent rather than a screen built for human eyes.

Once agents start watching what people used to watch, one question lingers.

"Then who watches what the agent itself sees and decides?"

The $200M Coralogix just raised is, in a sense, a price put on that watching layer. What stays with me is that large institutional money, like the Canada Pension Plan, joined the bet. Capital that doesn't chase fads moved here, which suggests it reads watching agents as a lasting need rather than a season's trend.

Building an agent keeps getting easier. Trusting one does not. The more something acts on its own, the less reason we have to trust it while its movements stay invisible.

Automation ends with code. Trust only appears when you can keep looking at what that code does. So what do we call autonomy we cannot see? Delegation, or neglect?
