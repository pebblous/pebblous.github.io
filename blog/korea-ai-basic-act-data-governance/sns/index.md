## LinkedIn (EN)

While the EU pushed its high-risk AI rules back to late 2027, South Korea did the opposite.

On July 21, 2026, Korea's AI Framework Act took full effect alongside the decree that spells out operators' duties. Its focus is "high-impact AI" — the systems that decide credit, healthcare, and hiring. In June the EU had moved the other way, delaying its high-risk (Annex III) obligations by 16 months under the Digital Omnibus.

The substance of the rule is what matters. Operators must now keep a "safety-reliability document" for five years, and it must include the explainability of the AI and the data it was trained on.

A grace period of at least a year applies to most duties, but the duty to label generated content, such as deepfakes, starts immediately.

So the real question the law asks is not about paperwork. It is whether a company can still prove, five years later, what data a decision was trained on and how good that data was. Data governance has moved from a nice-to-have cost to a precondition for putting high-impact AI on the market.

## Facebook (EN)

Have you ever gotten the rejection text that says your application didn't make the cut?

If that call was made by a model rather than a person, what are we supposed to lean on to accept it?

On July 21, 2026, Korea's AI Framework Act took full effect.

It groups the places where data judges people — credit scoring, loan screening, medical reads, hiring — under one label, "high-impact AI," and asks operators to write their safety and reliability down.

Around the same time the EU moved its high-risk rules back to late 2027, which leaves Korea as the country that put binding duties on people-judging AI first.

The document the law asks for is called a "safety-reliability document." But look at what goes inside, and it reads less like paperwork than like a record. What data was this decision trained on, and can you still explain its provenance and quality five years from now? It is not a question that closes with a single signature.

So I read this law less as a regulation than as a question.

"The judgment your AI made — what data is it standing on?"

A company that scrambles to produce documents after the rules arrive, and a company that recorded where its data came from from the start. A year into the grace period, the distance between them will have grown wide. That distance is also why Pebblous has spent its time using DataClinic to measure the quality of training data.

Documents can be filled in later. The provenance of data that has already flowed past is much harder to recover.
