## LinkedIn (EN)

Peregrine, a public-safety software company, raised $250 million in a Series D at a $6.8 billion valuation, roughly 2.7x its mark 15 months ago. The notable part: not a single new investor joined. Every participant was already a shareholder.

What they're buying isn't a bigger dataset. Peregrine doesn't collect new data. It connects what agencies already hold, from police records to 911 logs to permit databases, into a single permission-aware view. Role-based access, purpose limitation, and audit trails sit in the data layer itself, not bolted onto each application. Any app that queries the data passes through governance first.

The shadow is real. A log that exists is not the same as a log that prevents misuse. In the same period, 54% of Americans called AI-driven mass surveillance too risky, and CEO Nick Noone himself concedes these audit controls have been abused before.

If quality makes data usable, governance makes it permissible to use. Peregrine's $6.8 billion is a sign the market has started pricing that second condition too.

## Facebook (EN)

A child had gone missing.

I read that a suspect in a Fairfax County abduction was identified in thirteen minutes, and I thought about what those thirteen minutes must have meant to someone.

A few pages later, in the same reporting, residents were standing before a city council to oppose a contract with the company behind that speed.

The same tool. One side calls it rescue, the other calls it surveillance.

Peregrine sits in that uncomfortable middle. It doesn't gather new data. It takes what agencies already hold, police records, 911 logs, permit files, and joins them into a single view that knows who may see what, and for what purpose. Access rules and audit trails aren't bolted on afterward; the company says they live in the data layer itself.

The design is clean. What stayed with me was a line from the CEO, who admits audit trails have been abused in the past. So a question sits under all of it: is a log that exists the same as a log that prevents misuse? If the people who can read the log are the ones who might abuse the data, is that record a safeguard, or an alibi?

Technology can make data auditable. But the eyes that actually hold that audit to account come from somewhere outside the technology. Peregrine's $6.8 billion may have answered only half of the question.
