Executive Summary

Over the past four years, the price of a token has dropped 98%. Across the same span, the enterprise AI bill rose 320%. Prices fell and spending tripled — and this contradiction is not an accounting error. It happens because almost everyone overlooked a single variable wedged between the organization that budgeted off the unit price and the agents that actually burn the tokens. This piece looks at what that variable is, and why it is a question of data quality.

The variable is the retry. A simple chatbot asks once and answers once. An agent, when its output falls short of the bar, throws the entire accumulated conversation context back at the model and tries again. The cost of a ten-turn session does not grow tenfold; it balloons to roughly fifty times. That is why the per-interaction price jumped 30x, from $0.04 for a chatbot to $1.20 for an agent. The cheaper tokens got, the more often people ran agents — and those agents reburned the same tokens every time they failed.

Go one step deeper and what triggers the retry is, in the end, the input data. Noisy, inconsistent data produces hallucinations and validation failures, and those failures spin the retry loop. If that is true, then data quality is not first an accuracy metric — it is a cost printed directly on the bill. This is exactly where AI-Ready Data stops being a matter of ethics and becomes a matter of accounting.

Between the Price Tag and the Bill — The Shape of the Paradox

In 2022, GPT-4-class processing cost roughly $20 per million tokens. As of 2026, the blended price for the same processing sits around $0.40. That is a 98% drop. Go all the way back to the GPT-3 era and the decline reaches 99.7%. Look only at the price curve and the conclusion writes itself: "AI is getting closer to free." Plenty of organizations built their 2026 budgets on that assumption.

The bill, however, moved the other way. The average enterprise AI budget grew about 480%, from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026, and actual billed spend rose 320% in a single year. The unit price fell to a quarter while spending climbed nearly fivefold. Miss what happens in the stretch where the price tag and the bill split in opposite directions, and any cost-optimization tactic will only scratch the surface.

1.1Cheaper Means More — The Efficiency Paradox

In the 19th century, the economist William Stanley Jevons observed that as coal engines became more efficient, coal consumption did not fall — it exploded. When the cost per unit drops, new uses for the resource open up, and the growth in usage overwhelms the efficiency gain. The same thing happened to tokens. When the price was high, organizations spent tokens only on ask-once chatbots; when the price dropped, they began pouring them into agents that write code, dig through documents, and run multiple steps on their own.

The problem is that the budgeting model never kept up with this shift. Budgets were drawn as "unit price × expected number of calls," but what actually determined the cost was how many retries and context resends a single call set off internally. The unit price was the number the finance team watched; the retry was a hidden line item no one ever pulled out of the bill to look at.

2

The Culprit — The Agent's Retry Loop

Chatbots and agents burn tokens in fundamentally different ways. A chatbot produces one answer to one question and stops; the cost scales almost linearly with input and output length. An agent is different. Given a goal, it breaks the work into steps on its own, calls tools, validates intermediate results, and — when they fall short of the bar — tries again. And every time it tries again, it resends the entire conversation context accumulated so far to the model.

This "resend the whole context" behavior is the heart of the cost. If turn 1 carries 10,000 tokens of context, turn 2 resends a longer context that now includes turn 1, and turn 3 resends one longer still. Cost grows not linearly but quadratically with the number of turns. By Augment Code's analysis, the cost of a ten-turn session reaches not 10x but roughly 50x. The finding that ten repeated retries consume 50x the tokens of a single linear pass shows up consistently across multiple sources.

That this 50x is not one analysis exaggerating becomes clear when you swap sources. Gartner reports that agentic AI uses 5x to 30x the tokens of a standard chatbot, and Anthropic's engineering team measured internally that a single agent consumed 4x on average while multi-agent systems consumed 15x. The range of the estimates is wide, but the direction is single: the more times the context gets reread, the more cost grows by multiplication rather than addition.

Why 10 Turns Becomes 50x

Because every turn resends the prior context. Call one turn's worth of context 1, and the total volume sent is close to 1+2+3+…+10 = 55. When output fails to pass on the first attempt and retries get inserted, that accumulation grows larger still. The scariest item on the bill is not the new work — it is the cost of rereading the same context over and over.

2.1A 264-Hour Ping-Pong — The $47,000 Infinite Loop

In November 2025, a team chained four LangChain agents into an automated analysis pipeline. An Analyzer agent produced results and a Verifier agent checked them. Then a single API error code was misread as a "try again" signal, and the two agents began a ping-pong of tossing the work back to each other. Nothing stopped it. The loop ran for 264 hours — 11 days — and the bill came in at $47,000. The one decisive cause was that no budget ceiling had been set.

This incident is extreme but not an exception. On SWE-bench Verified, the token consumption between agent runs solving the same coding task differs by up to 30x, and one measurement found that a full agent run uses about 1,000x the tokens of an ordinary code chat. Given the same job, some runs finish in one pass while others get stuck and reread the same context dozens of times, driving up the cost. What separates the two is the subject of the next section.

The Enterprise Reality, in Numbers

The retry loop is not an abstract risk. It has already punched holes in the quarterly financials of several companies. Pull together the most frequently cited cases and one thing stands out: the cost blowup is not confined to any one industry or company size.

Case What happened
Uber Burned through its 2026 AI coding budget in four months. Claude Code adoption jumped from 32% to 84%, pushing API cost to $500–$2,000 per engineer per month.
Microsoft Fully revoked Claude Code licenses just six months after rollout.
Unnamed company Ran up $500M in Claude costs in a single month for lack of a usage cap.
Priceline Routine Cursor contract renewal cost rose 4–5x.
Fintech startup When fraud-detection agent users grew 10x, monthly cost went from $5,000 to $15,000. Per-user cost fell, but the total tripled.

More frightening than any individual case is how universal the forecasting failure is. Only 15% of companies predict their AI costs within ±10%, and one in four miss by more than 50%. In other words, no one knows what the bill will be until it arrives. That uncertainty moved organizations: the share of companies with a FinOps owner managing AI spend spread from 31% in 2025 to 98% in 2026 — nearly the entire industry — in a single year.

And the curve has yet to bend. Enterprise token consumption jumped 13x in just over a year since January 2025, and Goldman Sachs projects that if agentic patterns continue as they are, token demand will rise as much as 24x. The cases above are frightening not because they are exceptions but because they are points that arrived a little early on the same curve. Even if the unit price keeps falling, the bill will not change direction as long as consumption grows faster.

Gartner sees a dark end to this trend. As cost, unclear business value, and absent risk management converge, the firm projects that roughly 40% of agentic projects will be canceled by the end of 2027. Meanwhile, 72% of total AI production cost arises outside the model invoice — in orchestration, retrieval, retries, and observability. To cut the bill, you have to look at this 72%, not the model's unit price.

4

How Data Quality Gets Printed on the Bill

Here is the one question Pebblous wants to raise: why do retries happen? The moment an agent throws the same context back is the moment its output failed to clear the bar. And the most common reason output fails to clear is not the model's incompetence — it is a flaw in the input data the model stands on. The causal chain runs like this: bad data produces hallucinations, hallucinations lead to validation failures, validation failures spin the retry loop, and retries burn tokens.

In a RAG pipeline this chain is even sharper. By an observation IBM laid out in 2026, indiscriminately increasing the number of documents to search creates a range where accuracy actually drops. The more noisy documents pile up, the more the model grabs the wrong evidence, fails validation as a result, and lengthens the loop of re-searching and re-generating. One developer wrote that they belatedly discovered a single retry routine buried in their agent was adding 40% to their LLM bill — because no one had been treating it as a cost.

"Garbage In, Money Out"

The old data-industry adage "Garbage in, garbage out" was a quality warning: put bad data in and you get bad results out. In the agent era, the adage turns into an accounting proposition. Put bad data in and the model retries again and again to correct for it on its own — and that retrying drains straight out as money. The output may end up bad or barely usable, but either way the bill has already swelled. Garbage in, money out.

This view changes the logic for investing in data quality. For years, "good data" was justified in the language of ethics and quality — more accurate models, fairer decisions, regulatory compliance. It was an area that was hard to measure and awkward to attach an ROI to. But run it through the retry mechanism and data quality translates immediately into the token bill. Cut a single retry and the entire cost of that cumulative context resend disappears. Improving data quality raises accuracy and, at the same time, becomes a line of defense against token waste further upstream than any caching or routing.

One estimate holds that $0.82 of every $1 of AI spend never reaches production — it scatters into bug fixes, rework, and re-review. A large share of that is money spent by the model trying to paper over poor input on its own. Treat data quality only as an accuracy metric and that $0.82 stays invisible. Read it as a line on the token bill and it finally comes into view.

Editor's Note — Where Pebblous Stands

Pebblous diagnoses and improves data quality. In the logic of this piece, what that work means becomes clear. AI-Ready Data gives the model cleaner input, which reduces retries, and fewer retries shrink the token bill. We see this not as an ethical imperative but as a financial line of defense. The moment data quality becomes a cost item, it is no longer a task you can keep putting off.

5

What to Do First

The tactics for taming cost are already well known. The problem is the order. Most organizations bolt on caching and routing only after they see the bill — but that merely makes the retries that already happened cheaper; it does not reduce the retries themselves. Lined up by impact, the proven tactics look like this.

Tactic Savings What it reduces
Model routing Up to 87% Offloads simple tasks to cheaper models
Prompt caching 50–90% Avoids rebilling repeated input tokens
Context management Up to 53.7% Compresses resent context via history summarization
Batch processing 50% Bundles non-real-time work for a discount

These tactics are all effective, but they share one thing: they shave the cost after a retry has happened. The real upstream holds two more levers. One is the retry budget ceiling. The $47,000 infinite loop was an accident a single line of a ceiling would have stopped. The other is causing fewer retries in the first place — that is, input data quality. If caching makes retries cheaper, data quality reduces the number of retries itself.

5.1FinOps for AI — Start by Making It Visible

The order goes like this. First, visibility. You have to be able to pull apart the bill and see which agent burned tokens, on which task, through how many retries. Next, measurement. Make retry rate, resent-context volume, and average tokens per task into tracked metrics. Then optimization — ceilings, caching, routing, and, furthest upstream, data quality. Skip this order and jump straight to tactics, and the invisible retries keep burning money.

There is also a diagnosis that a siloed structure — where the technical, finance, and strategy teams operate apart — deepens the crisis. If finance watches the unit price, engineers watch the retries, and yet another team watches data quality, no one follows the causal chain that inflated the bill all the way to the end. Seeing, at one table, that the token-cost problem ultimately converges on a data-quality problem — that is the first step.

The answer to the paradox — token prices fell 80% yet the bill rose 320% — is, in the end, simple. Cheaper prices drew people toward agents, agents reburned the same context every time they failed, and a large share of those failures came from bad data. The fastest path to a smaller bill is not using the model more cheaply but reducing the work the model has to retry. And that work starts with the data.
