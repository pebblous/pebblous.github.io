## LinkedIn (EN)

193 nations gathered in Geneva to debate how to govern AI for the first time. What went on the table first wasn't a set of rules — it was a science report.

At the inaugural Global Dialogue on AI Governance on July 6–7, the UN inverted the usual order. Before bargaining over methods of control, member states laid down the preliminary report of a 40-member scientific panel as the common floor of the negotiation. Co-chaired by Turing Award laureate Yoshua Bengio and Nobel Peace Prize journalist Maria Ressa, the panel produced not a norm but a shared evidence base. As Secretary-General Guterres put it: "The science is here. We can no longer say we didn't know."

The trouble comes next. The report was opened to every government in six languages, yet who gets access to frontier models still runs on undisclosed criteria. The evidence is open; enforcement is closed.

A rule earns legitimacy only when it rests on evidence, and that evidence exists only when someone shapes reality into something measurable. So whose data, and whose measurement, do the rules that govern AI ultimately stand on?

## Facebook (EN)

What does it mean to agree on the facts before agreeing on the rules?

Last week in Geneva, 193 nations sat down together for the first time to talk about how to govern AI.

And the first thing on the table wasn't a ban, or a risk-tier chart.

It was a single report, written by forty scientists.

The panel was co-chaired by Yoshua Bengio, a Turing Award laureate, and Maria Ressa, a Nobel Peace Prize–winning journalist. What they built wasn't a norm but a common floor, a shared evidence base on which different countries could lay their own principles. "The science is here," said Secretary-General Guterres. "We can no longer say we didn't know."

I keep coming back to that order. When rules come before evidence, negotiation becomes a question of who is stronger. When evidence comes before rules, it becomes a question of what is true. That 193 nations chose the latter felt, to me, like a quiet kind of signal.

And yet I wasn't entirely reassured. The report was opened to every government in six languages, but who actually gets access to frontier models still sits behind a closed door. "The evidence is open — so why is what we do with it not?"

A rule earns its legitimacy only when it rests on evidence, and that evidence exists only when someone has shaped reality into something you can measure. That is close to the work Pebblous has spent years on: making data ready to be used by AI. Underneath governance, too, lies a question of data quality.

Whose data, whose measurement, do the rules that govern AI finally stand on?
