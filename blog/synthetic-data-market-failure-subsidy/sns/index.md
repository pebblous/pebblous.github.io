## LinkedIn (KO)

AI를 오염시키는 합성 데이터 문제를, 한 경제학 논문이 '오염'이 아니라 '시장 실패'라고 다시 불렀다.

이름을 바꾸면 처방이 바뀐다. 오염이라 부르면 해법은 걸러 내고 막는 검열의 언어가 되고, 시장 실패라 부르면 잘못 매겨진 값을 바로잡는 세금과 보조금의 언어가 된다. 논문의 핵심은 진본 데이터 생산자에게 얼마를 지불해야 하는지를 확정한 보조금 공식이다. 지금 데이터가 원본에서 얼마나 멀어졌는지가 지불할 값을 정한다. 오염이 심할수록 진본 데이터의 값이 오른다는 직관이, 관측이 아니라 방정식으로 적혔다.

이 보조금을 시장에 반복 적용한 알고리즘은 오염률을 78%에서 41%로 끌어내렸다. 다만 논문은 완전한 진본성 검증이 불가능하다는 것도 같이 증명한다. 블록체인 원산지 인증조차 이 한계를 벗어나지 못한다.

진본성이 규제 문서의 단어에서 경제학의 변수로 옮겨가는 순간이다. 값을 매기려면 먼저 그 데이터의 계보를 알아야 한다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

An economics paper just renamed the synthetic-data problem. It isn't "contamination." It's a market failure.

The rename changes the cure. Call it contamination and the answer is to filter and block — the language of censorship. Call it a market failure and the answer is to fix a mispriced good — the language of taxes and subsidies. The paper's core is a formula that fixes exactly how much to pay the producers of authentic data. How far today's data has drifted from the original sets the price you owe. The intuition that authentic data grows more valuable as contamination deepens is now written as an equation, not an observation.

Applied iteratively to a market, the subsidy pulled the contamination rate down from 78% to 41%. The paper is not naive, though: it also proves that complete provenance verification is impossible. Even blockchain-based origin certificates cannot escape that limit.

This is the moment provenance moves from a word in a regulatory document to a variable in an economic model. To set the price, you first have to know the data's lineage.

## Facebook (EN)

If good data and bad data sold for the same price, which would you buy?

If you have ever bought data to train a model, the question probably doesn't feel abstract.

When AI grows up on the output of an earlier generation of AI, the data drifts a little further from the original with each cycle. We usually call this contamination.

A paper posted to arXiv this year called it something else: a market failure. I keep coming back to how much that rename carries.

Call it contamination, and you reach for filters and blocks. That is the language of censorship. Call it a market failure, and it means the price was wrong. Then the work becomes fixing the price.

What the paper computed was how much to add on top for authentic data. The deeper the contamination, the higher that value climbs. In its experiments, the contamination rate fell from 78% to 41%.

One thing stayed with me. To set the price, you first have to know how far the data has moved from the original. Without lineage, there is no number to pay against.

The ability to measure authenticity turns out to be the same thing as the ability to charge for it. That is a place Pebblous has stood for a long time.

Provenance is crossing over from a principle worth protecting into an asset with a price.

And when it does, what exactly are we paying for?
