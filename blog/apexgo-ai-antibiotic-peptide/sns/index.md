정작 주목할 대목은 실패하지 않았다는 사실입니다. ApexGO는 생성한 후보를 또 다른 AI 모델의 점수로 채점하는 폐쇄 루프입니다. 보통 이런 구조는 점수판에서만 만점을 받고 실험실에서는 무너지는데, 이번엔 대부분이 실제로 작동했습니다.

차이를 만든 것은 더 큰 모델이 아니라 데이터 설계였습니다. 결합한 경우만이 아니라 결합하지 않은 경우까지 측정한 활성 데이터로 채점 기준을 세웠고, 이미 검증된 템플릿을 반복 편집해 탐색을 좁혔습니다. 검증 가능한 데이터 위에 세운 점수라야 실험실에서도 유지됩니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

An AI-designed antibiotic candidate matched a last-resort drug inside living mice.

The tool is ApexGO, built at the University of Pennsylvania. Starting from antimicrobial peptides mined from extinct animals like the mammoth and the ground sloth, it edited amino-acid sequences into new candidates. In vitro, 85% halted bacterial growth; in mice infected with a multidrug-resistant pathogen, two of them rivaled or beat polymyxin B, one of medicine's drugs of last resort.

What stands out is what did not happen. ApexGO scores its own candidates with a second AI model, a closed loop that usually rewards molecules that ace the scoreboard and collapse at the bench. This time, most of the designs actually worked.

The difference was not a bigger model but the data underneath it. The scoring model was trained on verifiable activity data that measured non-binding cases as well as binders, and optimization edited already-validated templates instead of roaming free. Scores hold up in the lab only when they sit on data that can be checked.

## Facebook (EN)

The first person to doubt the AI was the researcher who built it.

Jacob Gardner, at the University of Pennsylvania, worried out loud. His tool had been tuned against a second AI model, so he expected it to surface molecules that looked good to the model and fell apart at the bench.

The result went the other way.

Most of the peptides ApexGO designed actually worked. In the test tube, 85% halted bacterial growth, and in mice infected with a multidrug-resistant pathogen, some candidates matched polymyxin B, a drug doctors reach for only when nothing else is left. The story we usually hear runs the opposite direction: a candidate that glows on a screen and quietly disappears the moment someone tries to make it.

"A model scoring a model, and yet it held up. Why this time?"

I keep landing on the same answer, and it is not a bigger model. It is the data. The scoring model learned from activity measurements that recorded what failed to bind as well as what did. Only on a floor that holds failures, not just successes, does a model learn caution instead of optimism. That is the same question Pebblous asks of any dataset.

None of this means a new antibiotic is around the corner. A mouse is the first step on a long road. But the center of gravity seems to have shifted, from AI that spills out plausible candidates to AI that narrows them down to the ones a lab can reproduce.
