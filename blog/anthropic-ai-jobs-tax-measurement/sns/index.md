## LinkedIn (KO)

프런티어 AI 랩이 처음으로 자기 매출에 세금을 매기자고 나섰다. 앤트로픽이 AI 출력 매출의 3%를 일자리 대체 기금으로 내자고 제안한 것이다.

가장 많이 회자된 건 이 3%라는 숫자였다. 그런데 다리오 아모데이가 내놓은 3단계 대응에서 정작 1단계는 보상도 과세도 아니었다. 'AI로 인한 일자리 변화의 측정'이다. 무엇을 보상하고 어디에 과세할지 정하려면, 먼저 무슨 일이 일어났는지를 숫자로 합의해야 한다는 순서다.

문제는 그 측정이 노동 통계에서 가장 어려운 작업에 속한다는 데 있다. 해고에는 경기·구조조정·금리가 뒤섞여 있어 AI의 몫만 떼어 내기 어렵다. 2024년 AI에 직접 귀속된 실직은 약 1만 2,700건에 그쳤지만, 같은 시기 예측은 엔트리급 화이트칼라 일자리의 최대 절반이 사라질 수 있다고 경고했다. 예측과 실측 사이의 이 간극이 곧 측정의 난이도다.

측정되지 않는 실직은 어떤 보상에서도 빠진다. AI 시대 노동 정책의 출발점이 윤리나 재정이 아니라 '데이터로 잡히는가'로 옮겨간 순간이다. 데이터의 품질과 출처를 점검해 온 입장에서는, 익숙한 질문이 정책의 1번 문항이 되는 장면이기도 하다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

A frontier AI lab just proposed taxing its own revenue. Anthropic wants 3% of the revenue its models generate to fund the jobs they displace.

Most of the coverage fixated on that 3%. But in Dario Amodei's three-tier response, the first tier isn't compensation or taxation. It's measurement. Before you can decide what to compensate or where to tax, you first have to agree, in numbers, on what actually happened.

The trouble is that measuring AI-driven job loss is among the hardest tasks in labor statistics. Layoffs mix recession, restructuring and interest rates, and isolating AI's share is methodologically brutal. Roughly 12,700 layoffs were directly attributed to AI in 2024, while forecasts warned that up to half of entry-level white-collar jobs could disappear. That gap is the measurement problem.

A job loss that goes unmeasured falls out of every remedy. The starting point of labor policy has quietly shifted from ethics or budgets to a data question: can this even be counted? For anyone who audits data for gaps and provenance, it's a familiar question arriving as policy's opening line.

## Facebook (EN)

Can you write "because of AI" in the ledger when someone loses their job?

That question has stayed with me for a few days now.

Anthropic proposed taxing 3% of the revenue its models generate to help pay for the jobs they displace. It's the first time a frontier AI lab has offered to tax its own revenue. Most of the attention went straight to that 3%.

What held my eye was somewhere else.

Dario Amodei's response comes in three tiers, and the very first one isn't compensation or taxation. It's measurement. Without accurately measuring what's happening on the ground, he wrote, we're unlikely to get good policy.

There's a reason that line lingered. Counting "laid off because of AI" turns out to be far harder than it sounds. Around 12,700 layoffs were directly attributed to AI in 2024, while forecasts warned that up to half of entry-level jobs could vanish. The forecast says half; the count finds a handful. The distance between them is made of people the data hasn't found yet.

A job loss that goes unmeasured falls out of every remedy.

However elegant the mechanism for collecting the money, distribution fails when there's no data to identify who should receive it. So the first question of labor policy quietly moves from ethics or budgets to something else: can this even be seen in the data? For anyone who has spent their days checking datasets for gaps and provenance, it's a familiar question arriving as policy's opening line.

Maybe what can't be measured can't, in the end, be governed.
