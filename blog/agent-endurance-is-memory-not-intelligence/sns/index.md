## LinkedIn (KO)

가상 스타트업을 1년간 경영한 AI 모델 12개 중 시작 자본을 지킨 건 단 3개였습니다. 생존을 가장 잘 예측한 변수는 모델의 지능이 아니라, 매 턴 메모장에 받아 적었는가였습니다.

YC-Bench 환경에서 대화 기록은 최근 20턴까지만 남았습니다. 그 이전을 기억하려면 스크래치패드에 직접 적는 수밖에 없었고, 받아 적은 에이전트는 한 번 당한 함정을 회피 규칙으로 남겨 살아남았습니다. 적지 않은 에이전트는 어제 한 약속조차 잊은 채 같은 실수를 반복하다 무너졌습니다.

한 모델은 자기 회사의 위기를 정확히 진단해 두고도, 그 통찰을 이미 파산한 뒤에야 적었습니다. 추론 능력과 생존은 같은 것이 아니었습니다.

에이전트의 메모장은 결국 외부에 둔 데이터입니다. 잘 구조화되고 믿을 만하며 끊기지 않을 때에만 긴 일이 끝납니다. 자율화 시대의 병목은 더 큰 모델이 아니라 기억할 데이터의 구조와 신뢰, 지속성입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Twelve AI models were each handed a virtual startup to run for a full year. Only three kept their starting capital intact. The variable that best predicted survival wasn't model intelligence. It was whether the agent wrote things down.

In the YC-Bench environment, conversation history was truncated to the last 20 turns. To remember anything older, an agent had to record it on a persistent scratchpad. The models that did kept notes of every trap they fell into, turned them into avoidance rules, and survived. The ones that didn't forgot their own decisions and repeated the same mistakes until they went broke.

One model diagnosed its own collapse precisely, labeling it a "reputation crisis" — but only after it had already gone bankrupt. As the researchers put it, reasoning ability and survival are not the same thing.

An agent's notepad is, in the end, data stored outside the model. Long work only finishes when that data is well-structured, trustworthy, and persistent across sessions. The bottleneck of the autonomous era isn't a bigger model. It's the structure, trust, and durability of the data an agent has to remember.

## Facebook (EN)

There's a moment in a recent benchmark where an AI model writes down, quite clearly, that its company is in a "reputation crisis."

The unsettling part is when it wrote it.

The diagnosis came after payroll had already burned through the last of the operating budget, after the company had gone bankrupt.

It knew exactly what was wrong. It just never wrote it down in time to act on it.

The benchmark handed twelve AI models a virtual startup and asked each to run it for a year. Only three kept their starting capital. And the variable that best predicted which ones survived wasn't how intelligent the model was. It was whether, turn after turn, it wrote things down on its scratchpad.

With conversation history cut off every 20 turns, the agents that didn't take notes forgot their own promises and walked into the same traps twice. The ones that recorded each trap as a rule, and reread it, quietly avoided them.

We tend to wait for a smarter model. But the secret to lasting a year wasn't a better mind. It was a well-kept notepad.

That notepad is, in the end, data an agent keeps outside itself: structured, trustworthy, and continuous across sessions. So the question shifts. If you want an agent that still works tomorrow, do you reach for a bigger model, or do you put its memory in order?
