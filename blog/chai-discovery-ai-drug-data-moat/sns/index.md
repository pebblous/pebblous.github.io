## LinkedIn (EN)

An AI startup that designs antibodies just tripled its valuation in seven months.

Chai Discovery raised a $400M Series C at a $3.8B valuation, up from $1.3B last December, with Pfizer, Eli Lilly and Novartis now on its client roster.

The detail worth reading is the structure of the Pfizer deal. Pfizer didn't just license the shared Chai-3 model; it also received a private version retrained on its own proprietary data. Rivals can rent the same model, but no one else has Pfizer's decades of experimental records.

Over the same period, roughly $20B has flowed into generative AI drug discovery, yet not a single AI-designed drug has won regulatory approval. Design got fast. The validation data proving a molecule actually becomes a drug did not.

What gets priced in this market isn't the foundation model. It's access to validated data. That's the ground Pebblous has been mapping as AI-Ready Data.

## Facebook (EN)

When a company triples its value in seven months, we usually reach first for its technology.

Chai Discovery designs antibodies with AI. This July its valuation reached $3.8 billion, three times what it was seven months earlier.

But the number that held me wasn't the valuation.

It was the shape of what Pfizer bought.

Pfizer didn't just license the shared model that anyone can use. It also received a private version, retrained on decades of Pfizer's own proprietary data. A model only Pfizer can run.

Competitors can rent the same model. No one else has Pfizer's data.

Over the same stretch, more than $20 billion has flowed into generative AI drug discovery. The number of those drugs approved by regulators, so far, is zero.

Design has become astonishingly fast. The data proving a molecule actually becomes a medicine still accumulates at the old pace.

"Where does the moat really sit?"

In the company with the better model, or the one holding the validated data? This is the ground Pebblous has watched under the name AI-Ready Data.

Pfizer's contract, quietly, points to the second.
