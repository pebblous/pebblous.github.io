## LinkedIn (KO)

초전도체는 지난 115년간 7,000종 넘게 발견됐지만, 그중 만들기 전에 예측된 건 20종이 채 안 된다. 나머지는 대부분 우연과 시행착오의 산물이었다.

알토대·라이스대가 참여한 SuperC 컨소시엄이 이 순서를 뒤집었다. 머신러닝으로 유망한 원소 조합을 먼저 좁히고, 계산으로 거른 뒤, 그것만 골라 합성해 실험으로 확인하는 방식이다. 이렇게 예측되고 검증된 것이 카고메 구조의 YRu₃B₂와 LuRu₃B₂다.

다만 실측 임계온도는 각각 0.81K, 0.95K로 실용과는 멀고, 이론 예측치에도 못 미쳤다. 그런데도 이 연구의 성과는 온도가 아니라 순서에 있다. 처음으로 합성 전에 예측되고 예측대로 확인된 초전도체, 발견이 우연에서 탐색으로 바뀐 첫 사례이기 때문이다.

탐색의 해상도를 정하는 건 연산량이 아니라 후보를 표현하는 데이터의 품질이다. 실험이 데이터의 부산물이던 방식에서, 데이터가 다음 실험을 지목하는 방식으로 넘어가고 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

In 115 years, scientists have confirmed more than 7,000 superconductors. Fewer than 20 were predicted before anyone made them. The rest came from trial, error, and luck.

The SuperC consortium, led by Aalto University with Rice, Princeton, and others, reversed that order. Machine learning narrows vast elemental combinations to promising candidates, first-principles calculations filter them, and only the survivors are synthesized and measured. Two materials came through that pipeline: the kagome-lattice superconductors YRu₃B₂ and LuRu₃B₂.

Their measured critical temperatures, 0.81K and 0.95K, are far from useful and below the theoretical estimates. The result matters anyway, because it is about sequence, not temperature. For the first time, superconductors were predicted before synthesis and confirmed as predicted — discovery shifting from serendipity to search.

What sets the resolution of that search is not compute but the quality of the data that represents each candidate. Research is moving from treating experiments as a source of data toward letting data point to the next experiment.

## Facebook (EN)

New materials have mostly been found the same way.

You pick something that looks promising, mix it, make it, and cool it down until you can watch whether electricity starts flowing without any resistance. Over 115 years, more than 7,000 superconductors piled up like that. Yet fewer than twenty of them were called out in advance, predicted before anyone had made them.

The rest, in other words, were luck.

What a team at Aalto and Rice did this year was quietly invert that order. They let machine learning sweep through vast combinations first, kept only the promising ones to compute in detail, and made the material last. Two came through: the kagome superconductors YRu₃B₂ and LuRu₃B₂.

The materials themselves are modest, if I am honest. They only superconduct below one kelvin, still a long way from the old dream of room temperature, and the predicted temperatures missed the measured ones by a fair margin.

And yet the study stayed with me. For the first time, a calculation pointed to the spot before the material existed, and the experiment walked over to confirm it. Discovery moved from something you stumble into toward something you go looking for.

So what sets the resolution of that search? A faster computer, or how faithfully we describe each candidate as data? That is the question Pebblous keeps returning to. What you can find is decided, first, by how well you put it into data.
