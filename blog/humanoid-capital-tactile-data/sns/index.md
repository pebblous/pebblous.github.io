## LinkedIn (EN)

Robot bodies and brains raised billions this year. The sense at their fingertips still runs on millions.

Germany's Neura Robotics closed up to $1.4B in a single Series C round. Over the same stretch, Sensetics, a startup built entirely around tactile sensing, raised $1.75M in pre-seed. Both sit under the same Physical AI umbrella, yet the gap between them spans three orders of magnitude.

The reason is structural, not a matter of taste. Vision and language data already sit on the internet, ready to scrape. Touch does not. Pressure, slip, and texture only exist at the instant of physical contact, so there is nothing to crawl. On top of that, a commercial tactile sensor used to cost up to $10,000, which priced most labs out of collecting it at all.

That price has fallen to around $350. DAIMON Robotics released a one-million-hour tactile dataset, and NVIDIA folded a tactile hand into its open reference humanoid as a default component.

But cheaper sensors do not fill datasets on their own. Touch still accrues one hour at a time, hand by hand in the field, and the labeling and alignment that turn raw signals into trainable assets do not get solved by a falling price tag.

The moment touch becomes standard, the contest moves from the sensor to the data.

## Facebook (EN)

How does a robot know a strawberry is slipping out of its grip?

The question stayed with me for a few days.

Over the past year, billions flowed into robot bodies and brains. Neura Robotics alone raised $1.4B in a single Series C. Yet the tactile data that captures the pressure and slip at those fingertips still runs on millions.

They live under the same Physical AI umbrella. So why did the money split so far apart?

Half the answer lies in what touch is. Vision and language already sit on the internet, ready to be gathered. Touch is born only at the instant of contact, when something is actually held or pressed. It waits nowhere on the web.

The other half was price. A commercial tactile sensor once cost up to $10,000, and lately that figure has fallen to around $350. So DAIMON began printing touch as a million-hour dataset, and NVIDIA placed a tactile hand into its humanoid as a default part.

So if sensors are cheap now, does the data simply accumulate?

Not quite. Cheaper gear means more hands to record contact, not more contact itself. Touch still gathers one hour at a time, out in the field, under someone's actual grip.

That is close to why Pebblous reads data quality less as "how much was gathered" and more as "what was kept, and at what fidelity." The moment sensors got cheap may be less an arrival than a starting line for the contest over who owns this data.
