## LinkedIn (KO)

AI를 연구에 쓴 과학자는 논문을 세 배 더 냈고 인용도 크게 늘었다. 그런데 같은 기간, 과학이 통째로 다루는 주제의 폭은 오히려 줄었다.

4,130만 편을 분석한 James Evans 연구팀의 결과가 나온 지 한 달, 네이처 지면 안팎에서 후속 논평 세 편이 이어졌다. 세 편은 서로 다른 언어로 같은 곳에 닿는다. 좁아짐을 멈추는 열쇠는 더 나은 모델이 아니라, 연구자와 심사자와 펀더가 무엇을 보상하느냐에 있다는 것.

속도와 생산성을 보상하는 한, 개인은 합리적으로 AI가 잘 풀어 주는 질문에 몰린다. 그 선택이 쌓여 과학 전체가 한곳으로 수렴한다. Northwestern의 Luís Amaral은 이를 "같은 구덩이를 점점 더 깊게 파는 중"이라고 표현했다.

위험은 컴퓨터가 획일적으로 사고하는 데 있지 않다. 사람이 컴퓨터가 잘 푸는 질문만 골라 묻기 시작하는 데 있다. 무엇을 최적화하면 무엇이 조용히 사라지는가. 이 질문은 데이터 파이프라인에도 그대로 적용된다. 페블러스가 데이터 다양성을 계측하고 복원하는 인프라를 만들어 온 이유이기도 하다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Researchers who adopted AI published roughly three times as many papers, and were cited far more. Over the same period, the range of topics science covers as a whole actually shrank.

A month after James Evans's team published its analysis of 41 million papers, three follow-up commentaries appeared in and around Nature. They reach the same place by different routes: the key to stopping the narrowing is not a better model, but what researchers, reviewers, and funders choose to reward.

As long as speed and productivity are rewarded, individuals rationally crowd onto the questions AI solves well. Those choices accumulate until the whole field converges. Northwestern's Luís Amaral put it bluntly: we are digging the same hole ever deeper.

The danger is not that computers think uniformly. It is that people begin to ask only the questions computers solve well. What quietly disappears when you optimize for something? The same question applies to any data pipeline, which is why Pebblous builds infrastructure to measure and restore data diversity.

## Facebook (EN)

What if the researcher who publishes more ends up asking narrower questions?

Is that a personal failing, or a sign of what we've decided to praise?

Scientists who brought AI into their work published far more papers than before. Their citations climbed too. And yet, over the same stretch, the range of topics science covers as a whole grew narrower.

One line moving up, another moving down. The individual's metrics and the field's diversity, pulling apart in the same window of time.

After publishing that result, Nature turned the question back on itself: what would it take to stop the narrowing? What's striking is that the three follow-up commentaries didn't reach for a better model. They said something else. As long as we reward speed, people will keep crowding onto the questions that solve easily. One researcher called it digging the same hole ever deeper.

The danger isn't that computers think alike. It's that people start asking only the questions computers answer well.

What quietly disappears when you optimize for something?

The question stays with anyone who works with data. Just as good data that loses its diversity narrows the model built on it, science that leans on AI drifts toward the same place. That's not far from why Pebblous has stayed with the work of measuring and restoring diversity in data. Making the vanishing thing measurable is where any conversation about stopping the narrowing has to begin.
