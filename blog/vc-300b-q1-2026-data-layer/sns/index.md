## LinkedIn (EN)

Global venture funding hit $300 billion in Q1 2026, the largest quarter on record.

Roughly 80% of it, $242 billion, went to AI, and most of that was absorbed by just four mega-rounds: OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, and Waymo. The headline writes itself. Capital is betting on frontier models.

But in the same quarter, Meta paid $14.3 billion for a 49% stake in data-labeling firm Scale AI, valuing it at $29 billion. Surge AI, bootstrapped and never venture-backed, overtook Scale on revenue and pushed for a $25 billion valuation. As model prices climb, so does the price, and the leverage, of the data that feeds them.

A model is bought once. Data is re-purchased every quarter through licensing fees, litigation settlements, and compliance costs. If 80% of the capital went to AI, 80% of AI's labor is still tied up in data. The thing quietly getting expensive sits behind the headline.

## Facebook (EN)

$300 billion.

That's what global venture funding reached in a single quarter, Q1 2026. The largest on record, they say.

Most of the coverage stops at one fact: 80% of it went to AI. OpenAI, Anthropic, and a few other names fill the headlines.

A different question stayed with me. What are these models eating to get so expensive?

In the same quarter, a quieter deal closed. Meta paid for half of Scale AI, a data-labeling company, at a $29 billion valuation. Not long ago, firms like it were filed under "outsourced annotation, call when needed."

A model is bought once. Data, I keep noticing, has to be bought again and again. Through licensing. Through lawsuits. Through the cost of proving where it came from.

Maybe the old line that data is an asset is finally being settled not on a balance sheet, but at a market price.
