## LinkedIn (EN)

Three AI agents picked a drug candidate for an untreatable cause of blindness, with humans barely touching the science.

FutureHouse's multi-agent system Robin took a single prompt, "dry age-related macular degeneration," read more than 500 papers in 30 minutes, set a therapeutic strategy, and narrowed 30 compounds to ripasudil, an approved glaucoma drug, as a repurposing candidate. The agents wrote the hypotheses, the experimental plans, and the data analysis; the human scientists only ran the bench work. The study appeared in Nature in 2026.

But the more revealing number is buried in the same paper. The effect Robin's analysis reported as 7.5x dropped to 1.75x once humans re-analyzed the same data. Swap the literature-search agent for a different model, and hallucinated citations jumped from 0% to 45%.

The direction held; the magnitude did not. The faster AI generates hypotheses, the heavier the job of checking whether its output is grounded in reality. That is why Pebblous quantifies the quality of AI training data through DataClinic.

## Facebook (EN)

An AI wrote down that a drug worked 7.5 times better than the control.

A human re-ran the same data and got 1.75.

I keep returning to the distance between those two numbers.

FutureHouse's AI system, Robin, was handed a disease with almost no treatments: dry age-related macular degeneration, a leading cause of blindness.

In thirty minutes it read more than 500 papers, decided which cellular function to target, narrowed 30 compounds, and named ripasudil, an approved glaucoma drug, as a candidate.

The thinking parts of the research, the hypotheses and the interpretation, were almost entirely the machine's. The humans only ran the experiments at the bench.

It is fast, and it is genuinely remarkable.

But what stopped me wasn't the speed. It was the gap between 7.5 and 1.75.

Both numbers pointed the same way. AI and humans agreed that ripasudil does something.

What they disagreed on was how much. And in drug discovery, how much is the number that decides where the next dollar goes.

When the team swapped in a different search model and fabricated citations climbed from 0% to 45%, the same question came back:

"When an AI answers this quickly, what exactly are we trusting?"

We tend to say automation will take work off our hands.

Reading this paper, it looks more like automation hands us a new kind of work: verification. The faster the answers arrive, the heavier it gets to check whether they are standing on solid ground.

That is the same place Pebblous keeps looking, through DataClinic, at the quality of the data these systems learn from and analyze.

The last word still belongs to people. And it is getting heavier, not lighter.
