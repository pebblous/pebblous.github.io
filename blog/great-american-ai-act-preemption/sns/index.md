269페이지짜리 미국 연방 AI 법안이 가장 먼저 멈춰 세운 주(州) 법은, 모델의 행동을 규제하는 법이 아니라 학습 데이터를 공개하라는 법이었습니다.

6월 4일 공개된 Great American AI Act 초당파 초안은 주가 AI '개발'을 규제하지 못하도록 3년간 막습니다. 그 첫 표적으로 명시된 것이 캘리포니아 AB 2013입니다. 데이터의 출처, 저작권 상태, 개인정보 포함 여부, 합성 데이터 사용까지 요약해 공개하라던, 미국에서 보기 드문 훈련 데이터 투명성 의무죠.

연방은 이 공개 의무를 멈추는 대신 자체 규율을 얹습니다. 연매출 5억 달러를 넘는 소수 프론티어 개발사에 인증 기관의 반기 감사를 지우는 방식입니다. 누구나 읽을 수 있는 '공개'에서, 검증된 제3자만 들여다보는 '감사'로 무게중심이 옮겨갑니다.

문제는 감사가 데이터 안으로 더 깊이 들어가더라도, 대중이 학습 데이터를 볼 시야는 오히려 좁아진다는 데 있습니다. 규제가 다투는 대상이 모델의 출력에서 모델의 재료로, 그리고 그 재료를 누가 들여다볼 수 있느냐로 이동했습니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The first state law a 269-page federal AI bill moved to freeze was not a law about how models behave. It was a law about disclosing what they were trained on.

Released June 4, the bipartisan Great American AI Act draft would bar states from regulating the "development" of AI models for three years. The named first target is California's AB 2013, a rare US training-data transparency mandate requiring developers to summarize their data sources, copyright status, personal-information content, and synthetic-data use.

In place of that disclosure, the federal draft adds its own discipline. A small set of frontier developers above $500M in revenue would have to hire certified third parties for biannual audits. The center of gravity shifts from open disclosure, which anyone can read, to audit, which only a vetted party sees.

The catch: an audit reaches deeper into the data, yet the public's view of training data narrows. The fight has moved from a model's outputs to its materials, and to who is allowed to look at those materials.

## Facebook (EN)

"Development, or deployment?"

Lawyers are arguing over that one line in a 269-page AI bill. The longer I sit with it, the more it seems to be asking something else entirely.

The federal Great American AI Act draft would stop states from regulating how AI models are made for three years. The federal government claims the making; the states keep the using.

The first state law placed on that line was California's training-data disclosure law. A law that asked companies to summarize, in the open, what their models were trained on.

Gathering and cleaning data is clearly development. Yet publishing a summary of it looks more like something that happens after a product ships. Draw the line one way, and the same training data is a trade secret. Draw it the other way, and it is a public filing.

So the jurisdictional fight, put in the language of people who work with data, becomes this: "Who has the right to look at a model's training data?"

The federal answer leans from "anyone" toward "only a certified third party." The weight moves from disclosure to audit. An audit reaches deeper, but the public's view grows narrower.

Whether the rule asks for disclosure or for an audit, the question underneath seems to settle into one. What data was this model made from?
