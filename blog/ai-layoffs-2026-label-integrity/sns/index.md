## LinkedIn (EN)

In May 2026, AI became the single most-cited reason for layoffs for the first time on record.

Read the same report one column over, though, and the picture flips. Total announced layoffs were down 43% from a year earlier. The pie shrank, while the slice labeled "because of AI" kept growing.

The real issue is who applies the label. Layoff reasons are self-attributed by the very companies being judged on them. In machine-learning terms, that is the most dangerous setup there is: the labeler has a stake in the outcome.

It shows. In an NBER survey, roughly 90% of executives said AI had had essentially no effect on their hiring over the past three years. The same kind of executives put AI at the top of their layoff announcements.

So the sharper question is not whether AI is taking jobs. It is whether we can measure that honestly right now. When the integrity of the "reason" label collapses, the retraining budgets and regulations built on top of it rest on a false signal.

## Facebook (EN)

"AI is now the most-cited reason companies give for cutting jobs."

When I first read that line, it was the next sentence that stayed with me longer.

In the same report, total layoffs in 2026 were down 43% from a year earlier.

The pie had shrunk. Only the slice labeled "because of AI" had grown.

So I kept turning over a quieter question. Is it the layoffs that are rising, or just the name we give them?

One thing wouldn't let go of me. These reasons are self-reported by the very companies being judged on them. The one who applies the label and the one being measured by it are the same. In one survey, about 90% of executives said AI had had essentially no effect on their hiring over the past three years, while the same kind of executives put AI at the front of their layoff announcements. One mouth says one thing, the other says the opposite.

What this piece wanted to ask was never whether AI takes jobs. Some roles are genuinely being automated. The harder truth is that we can't measure the size of that change honestly right now, because the integrity of the "reason" label has come loose.

When a label goes unverified, everything built on top of it wobbles. It's the argument Pebblous has been making about training data for a long time, replaying now on top of employment statistics.
