## LinkedIn (EN)

An AI beat doctors, the headline ran in Science this April. The catch: the model never saw a patient, only text.

OpenAI's o1-preview scored 89% on complex clinical vignettes, against 34% for physicians who had search engines and medical databases at hand. Every input was text — vignettes and health records that someone had already translated a patient into.

Real clinicians read scans, hear heart sounds, watch for pallor. Most of those signals are summarized or lost before they ever become text. In the study's live emergency-room comparison, the gap between model and specialists all but vanished.

So 89% is a score on clinical reality already converted to text, not on clinical reality itself. Whether a win on exam data holds on field data is less a question of bigger models than of how the data is represented.

## Facebook (EN)

"An AI beat doctors." I saw the sentence again in my feed a few days ago.

It came from a study in Science this April. OpenAI's o1-preview answered 89% of complex clinical vignettes correctly. Physicians, search engines in hand, managed 34%.

By the numbers, the contest looks settled.

Then you read one more line and stop. Every input the model received was text. The vignettes, the health records — each one a patient already translated into prose by someone else.

A real clinician doesn't work that way. They read the scan, listen to the heart, notice the pallor and the cold sweat. Most of those signals are summarized or lost before they ever become words. In the same study's live emergency room, the gap between model and specialist nearly disappeared.

So I've come to think of this as a win on "exam data." In the exam, the patient is already clean text. In the clinic, the patient is still images, sounds, and touch.

"Does a win on exam data still hold as a win on field data?"

The honest answer points less to bigger models than to the data itself — to carrying the signal of the room across without losing it. That is the question Pebblous keeps returning to with AI-Ready Data.
