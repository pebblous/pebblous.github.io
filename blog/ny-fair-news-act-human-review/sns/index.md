NY FAIR News Act는 AI가 만든 기사에 꼬리표를 다는 데서 그치지 않는다. AI가 생성한 기사·오디오·이미지·영상은 편집권을 가진 사람이 게재 전에 검토해야 하고, 기자의 취재원과 기밀 자료는 AI 시스템이 접근할 수 없도록 차단해야 한다. EU AI법이 결과물에 라벨을 붙이는 데 집중했다면, 뉴욕은 그 결과물이 만들어지는 워크플로 안으로 들어와 사람의 개입 지점과 데이터 접근 경계를 규정한다.

아직 확정은 아니다. Kathy Hochul 주지사의 서명이 남아 있고, 뉴욕 뉴스출판협회는 편집국에 공시를 강제하는 것이 수정헌법 1조 위반이라며 맞서고 있다.

규제의 다음 질문은 '무엇을 표시하는가'가 아니라 '누가 어떤 데이터에 손댈 수 있는가'로 옮겨 가고 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

New York's legislature has passed a bill requiring that AI-generated news be reviewed by a human before it runs.

The FAIR News Act goes beyond labeling. AI-generated articles, audio, images, and video must be checked by a person with editorial authority before publication, and a reporter's sources and confidential materials must be walled off from AI systems entirely. Where the EU AI Act focused on labeling the output, New York steps into the workflow that produces it, fixing where a human must intervene and where AI may not reach.

It is not settled yet. The bill awaits Governor Kathy Hochul's signature, and the New York News Publishers Association argues that compelling newsroom disclosures violates the First Amendment.

The regulatory question is shifting from what gets labeled to who is allowed to touch the data.

## Facebook (EN)

Did a single person read the article you just finished?

It's a strange thought to have while scrolling. The smoother the sentences, the more I find myself wondering who, if anyone, stood behind them.

New York has turned that question into law. It isn't content to stamp "made by AI" on the finished piece; it now requires that a person with editorial authority read the copy before it ever runs.

Then it goes one step further. A reporter's sources, the names and documents they protect at real cost, are placed in a zone that AI systems are not allowed to enter.

A label is a tag on the thing the reader sees. Review and access limits reach into how that thing gets made. Quietly, the question has moved from what we show to who is allowed to touch the data.

For those of us who have spent years tracing where data comes from and drawing lines around who may reach it, none of this feels foreign. The governor's signature is still pending, and a fight over free speech is already promised. Nothing is settled. But the fact that regulation has begun to reach the data itself, and not just the output, is likely to stay with us.
