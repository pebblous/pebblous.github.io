## LinkedIn (EN)

OpenAI has unveiled its first in-house inference chip, Jalapeño. A company that built models is now laying the silicon those models will run on.

Designed with Broadcom and fabricated on TSMC's 3nm process, it is a reticle-sized inference ASIC. The headline isn't the spec; it's the schedule. A large custom ASIC typically takes 18 to 24 months from blank sheet to tape-out. OpenAI says it did it in nine, by putting its own language models to work on the design optimization.

The loop is the point. A model helps shape the chip, and the finished chip runs the next model more cheaply. Software builds hardware, and hardware in turn raises better software.

But efficiency carries a cost in opacity. When chip design, model training, and inference serving all converge inside one company, who audits the data, evaluation, and verification circulating within it? Vertical integration speeds internal optimization exactly as much as it makes external oversight harder.

## Facebook (EN)

A chip was unveiled this week, and yet what stayed with me wasn't the chip.

OpenAI introduced Jalapeño, its first custom inference chip, built with Broadcom.

A large chip usually takes a year and a half, sometimes two, to move from a blank page to a finished design. OpenAI says it compressed that to nine months — and the reason it offered was its own language models, put to work on the design itself.

A model helps shape the chip it will eventually run on. The finished chip then runs the next model more cheaply.

Software builds hardware, and that hardware raises better software in turn. Call it a 'self-referential loop.' The faster that circle turns inside a single company, the narrower the window for anyone outside to see in.

And so one question lingers:

"When a single company holds the whole stack, top to bottom, whose eyes audit the data, the evaluation, the verification moving within it?"

Efficiency is a real virtue. I keep wondering, though, whether what we quietly hand over in exchange is verifiability itself. That is why a company that has spent its days diagnosing data reads this announcement less as a story about speed, and more as a question about oversight.
