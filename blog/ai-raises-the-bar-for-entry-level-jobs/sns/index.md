## LinkedIn (KO)

AI에 가장 많이 노출된 기업일수록 사람을 더 많이 뽑았다. PwC가 27개국 채용 공고 10억 건을 분석한 결과, AI 노출 상위 기업의 인력은 2018년 대비 52% 늘어 하위 기업(36%)을 크게 앞섰다.

그런데 같은 시기 신입의 자리는 줄었다. 사라진 건 일자리 총량이 아니라, 신입이 처음 발을 들이던 진입로였다. AI가 신입이 일을 배우던 단순 업무를 가져가자, 신입 공고는 리더십과 판단 같은 경력자급 역량을 요구하기 시작했다. AI 노출이 높은 신입 직무가 시니어 역량을 요구할 확률은 그렇지 않은 직무의 7배였다. 기업이 22세에게 35세의 능력을 요구하기 시작한 셈이다.

문턱이 올라가자 그 문턱을 넘은 사람의 값도 올랐다. AI를 다룰 줄 아는 사람의 임금 프리미엄은 2년 만에 25%에서 62%로 뛰었다.

인간의 판단이 더 비싸지는 시장에서 다음 질문은 분명하다. 그 판단을 떠받치는 데이터를 누가 제대로 쥐고 있는가.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The companies most exposed to AI hired more people, not fewer. PwC analysed over a billion job ads across 27 countries and found headcount at the most AI-exposed firms grew 52% since 2018, well ahead of the least-exposed (36%).

Yet entry-level seats shrank over the same period. What disappeared wasn't the total number of jobs but the on-ramp newcomers used to step onto. As AI absorbed the routine tasks juniors once learned on, junior ads began demanding capabilities once reserved for experienced hires. Entry-level roles with high AI exposure were 7x more likely to ask for senior-level skills than low-exposure ones. Employers, in effect, started asking a 22-year-old for a 35-year-old's abilities.

As the bar rose, so did the price of clearing it. The wage premium for AI skills more than doubled in two years, from 25% to 62%.

In a market where human judgment keeps getting more expensive, the next question is plain: who actually holds the data that judgment runs on.

## Facebook (EN)

Picture someone applying for their very first job at twenty-two.

Their résumé holds a couple of internships, and the posting asks for leadership and face-to-face negotiation.

When PwC went through a billion job ads across 27 countries, it turned out this wasn't one person's bad luck but a pattern across the whole market. The more AI-exposed a junior role was, the more likely it asked for skills once reserved for experienced hires, by a factor of seven.

The surprising part is that AI hadn't erased the jobs at all.

The most AI-exposed firms actually hired more people. What vanished was the first rung, the simple work where newcomers used to learn slowly. With the bottom step removed, companies started looking for juniors who already wear a senior's face.

And that leaves a question worth sitting with. "If juniors never grow through the simple work, where do the seniors of ten years from now come from?"
