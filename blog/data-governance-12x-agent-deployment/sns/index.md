## LinkedIn (EN)

Organizations using governance tools pushed 12 times as many AI projects into production as everyone else.

That single line comes from Databricks' 2026 State of AI Agents report, drawn from anonymized usage data across more than 20,000 organizations. Until now governance was a cost line finance teams fought to trim; this number turns it into a deployment multiplier. An independent survey by Larridin, polling 364 enterprise leaders with an entirely different method, points the same way: organizations with a formal governance policy were 2.2 times more likely to prove ROI on AI.

The 12x is correlation, not proven causation, and it rests on one vendor's telemetry. A policy document is not governance, either. In the same survey 69% claimed to have an AI policy, yet nearly half could not state their own adoption rate. What creates the multiplier is enforcement on the data, not paperwork.

The long-standing verdict that the bottleneck is data and organization, not the model, now has a number attached. That is why Pebblous built DataClinic to diagnose data quality and readiness.

There's a question finance teams ask whenever a data team requests budget for governance.

"And what is that problem worth, exactly?"

For years we had no number to offer back. We knew the data wasn't ready, but "not ready" is a feeling, not a figure.

This year a figure arrived. Databricks, looking across more than 20,000 organizations, found that the ones using governance tools had moved 12 times as many AI projects into production. A separate survey, built on an entirely different method, leaned the same way. When two different lenses settle on the same spot, I find it hard to look away.

It would be a mistake to read this as "governance multiplies deployment twelvefold." It's correlation, not cause, and drafting one more policy document buys you nothing. What actually creates the multiplier isn't paperwork; it's the plainer condition of being able to see where your data comes from and where it goes.

Maybe what the report really changed isn't the nature of governance, but the fact that someone can finally measure it. Once the value of readiness becomes a number, postponing the work of preparing data stops being a saving and becomes a quiet subtraction from how much you can deploy.
