## LinkedIn (EN)

A thousand mathematicians signed a single declaration within a day of its release. Among the names: Fields medalist Peter Scholze and the International Mathematical Union.

The trigger was OpenAI's May claim that an AI had disproved an 80-year-old Erdős conjecture. But the result arrived as a press release, not a journal paper, and the model was proprietary, so no outsider could check the method or the training data. What the mathematicians objected to was not AI but the process: training on papers without consent, announcing results before peer review, and failing to attribute the work it relied on.

The declaration does not ban AI. It simply asks of machines what scholarship has always asked of people. It names consent, attribution, and peer review not as bureaucracy but as the substance of trust.

On August 2, Article 53 of the EU AI Act turns disclosure of training data into a legal obligation. What the academy stated as a norm, regulation is now writing down as enforcement.

## Facebook (EN)

How do we come to trust that a proof is right?

Last May, OpenAI announced that an AI had disproved an 80-year-old problem in mathematics.

But the announcement came as a press release, not a journal paper.

Which model, used how, was never disclosed, and the path to check it was closed from the start.

In mathematics, a proof is not finished when the answer happens to be correct. It is a proof only when anyone can walk the reasoning again and see why it holds.

It was at that blocked path that a thousand mathematicians put their names to the Leiden Declaration, Peter Scholze and the International Mathematical Union among them. What they opposed was not AI. It was training on papers without consent, skipping verification, and leaving unsaid whose work a result was built on. They asked of machines only what scholarship has always asked of people.

"Is the data we feed our AI traceable in its origins, consented to, and open to being checked again?"

The question these mathematicians called the substance of trust seems to sit, unchanged, at every place where data is handled.
