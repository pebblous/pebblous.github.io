그러나 한 가지는 분명해졌습니다. 성능을 가른 것은 데이터의 양이 아니라, 데이터에 구조가 잡혀 있었는지였습니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

A robot that used 80× less training energy turned out to be more accurate than the model fed far more data.

At ICRA 2026, a Tufts University team pitted a neuro-symbolic model head-to-head against a VLA on the Tower of Hanoi. On the three-block task they trained on, the split was 95% to 34%; on a four-block task neither had ever seen, it was 78% to 0%. The side that ate less generalized better.

The reason was rules. One model pulled the rules out of 50 demonstrations on its own; the other memorized 300 whole trajectories. A robot that knows the rules searches only the allowed moves, so it grinds through less trial and error and burns less energy.

The Tower of Hanoi is, of course, a structured task with clear rules. Whether the same edge holds in open settings where the rules are vague remains an open question.

But one thing is now clear: what decided performance was not the volume of data, but whether the data had structure.

## Facebook (EN)

Why did the side that learned more do worse?

In an experiment that asked a robot to solve the Tower of Hanoi, the model that had memorized 300 full solutions failed every single time once one more block was added.

The smaller robot, which had pulled the rules out of just 50 demonstrations, solved it 78% of the time.

A robot that knows the rules moves only within the allowed set. It never wastes a moment trying to place a large block on a small one. That alone cut its training energy by nearly 80 times.

What stayed with me was not the numbers but the order of things. The robot was not handed the rules; it read them off the data. Relations between blocks — on, clear, larger_than — were already sitting inside the demonstrations.

So the question shifts. "Are we spending our effort gathering more data, or on giving the data we already have a structure?"

That is the same thread Pebblous follows in working on data quality and structure. For rules to come out of data, the data first has to hold a structure in which rules can be read.
