## LinkedIn (KO)

미 하원이 공개한 초당적 AI 법안 초안은 AI를 별도의 '기술 규제'로 다루지 않는다. 노동법과 사이버보안법 위에 얹어서 규율한다.

6월 4일 나온 269페이지짜리 Great American AI Act(GAAIA)는 프런티어 거버넌스, 노동, 사이버보안, 연구개발 네 축을 나란히 놓는다. 전력망이나 통신망을 감독할 때 쓰던 언어 — 독립 감사, 사고 보고, 국가 안보 — 가 AI에 그대로 옮겨 왔다.

핵심은 규제 문법의 이동이다. '무엇을 공개하라'가 아니라 '감사에 답할 수 있느냐'를 묻는다. 인증받은 독립 기관이 6개월마다 회사 기록에 전면 접근하고, 대량해고 공시는 어떤 AI 시스템을 썼는지 명시하도록 요구한다.

이 법안은 아직 통과를 장담하기 어려운 초안이다. 그래도 AI를 인프라로 보는 시각은 초안이 통과되든 아니든 남는다. 새 공개 의무가 없어도, 추적 가능한 데이터·모델 계보를 갖춘 조직만이 감사와 공시와 사고 보고에 답할 수 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

A bipartisan draft in the U.S. House doesn't treat AI as a standalone "tech" problem. It regulates AI on top of labor and cybersecurity law.

Released June 4, the 269-page Great American AI Act lines up four titles: frontier governance, workforce, cybersecurity, and R&D. The vocabulary we reserve for power grids and telecom networks — independent audits, incident reporting, national security — now applies to AI.

The real shift is in the grammar of regulation. It stops asking "what will you disclose" and starts asking "can you answer an audit." A certified independent body would review compliance every six months with full access to a company's records, and mass-layoff notices would have to name which AI system was used.

The bill may not pass. But the view of AI as infrastructure outlasts any single draft. Even without a new disclosure mandate, only organizations with traceable data and model provenance can answer an audit, a notice, or an incident report.

## Facebook (EN)

If a layoff notice had to name the AI system that shaped the decision, could the company actually answer?

I read the bipartisan AI draft the U.S. House put out a few days ago, and what stayed with me wasn't any single clause. It was the shape of its table of contents.

The bill doesn't set AI aside as a "tech" matter. It places it next to labor law, next to cybersecurity law.

The words we use for power and telecom were sitting right there: independent audits, incident reporting, national security. Somewhere along the way, AI had moved into the seat we keep for the infrastructure we depend on.

And so the question the law asks has changed. Not what did you disclose, but are you in a state to answer, whenever someone asks. Disclosure ends the moment you post it. Answering an audit means keeping the links between data, model, and decision open at all times.

This draft may never pass. The question it leaves behind will. When someone asks which data built this model, what can your organization actually show? That is the question Pebblous has been sitting with for a long time.
