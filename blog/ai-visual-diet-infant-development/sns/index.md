2026년 Nature Machine Intelligence에 실린 연구는 사람 아기의 시력 발달 순서를 그대로 학습 커리큘럼으로 옮겼다. 흐릿한 흑백에서 선명한 컬러로, 저대비에서 고대비로. 새 데이터를 만들지 않고 기존 이미지에 필터만 걸었는데, 지금까지 보고된 것 중 가장 강한 형태 편향과 함께 손상·적대적 공격에 더 잘 버티는 눈이 만들어졌다.

원리는 단순하다. 세부가 지워진 흐릿한 이미지에서는 질감이라는 지름길이 막히고, 남는 정보는 큰 형태뿐이다. 표준 CNN의 형태 편향이 22%에 그치던 문제를 순서 설계 하나로 밀어 올린 것이다.

가장 깔끔한 증거는 반대 실험이다. 같은 이미지, 같은 양이어도 순서를 무작위로 섞자 개선분이 기준선까지 사라졌다.

깨끗하게 정제하는 것만으로는 부족하다. 데이터 품질을 '상태(clean)'가 아니라 '궤적(curriculum)'으로 다시 보게 하는 사례다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

It wasn't more data. What made an AI vision model more robust was changing the order in which it was fed images.

A 2026 study in Nature Machine Intelligence rebuilt the training curriculum to follow how an infant's vision matures: from blurry grayscale to sharp color, from low to high contrast. No new data was created; existing images were simply filtered by developmental stage. The result was the strongest shape bias reported to date, along with better resistance to image corruption and adversarial attacks.

The mechanism is plain. When detail is stripped away, the texture shortcut is closed off and only global shape is left to learn from. A standard CNN's shape bias sits at just 22%; reordering the diet pushed it well past that.

The cleanest proof came from the reverse test. With the same images and the same volume, shuffling the order erased the gains back to baseline.

Clean data isn't enough. This reframes data quality from a state (clean) to a trajectory (curriculum).

## Facebook (EN)

For a long time I thought of it only as a limitation: that a newborn spends its first months in a blurry, grayscale world.

Something to grow out of, quickly.

Then I read a study published this year in Nature Machine Intelligence, and began to wonder whether that blur was ever a defect at all. Whether it was an order.

The researchers created no new data. They simply filtered existing images to follow an infant's developmental sequence, from blurry to sharp and grayscale to color, and fed them to an AI vision model in that order.

And the eye that used to be fooled by texture started reaching for shape first.

When detail is stripped away, there is nothing small to cling to. Only the large mass remains: the shape. The slow opening of an infant's eye, it turns out, had its reasons.

What stayed with me longest was the reverse test. Same images, same volume. Shuffle the order, and the entire gain vanished. It wasn't what the data contained. It was the sequence in which it was shown.

This is the same point Pebblous keeps returning to when we talk about AI-Ready Data: cleaning data once is not the end. How you prepare it, and in what order you hand it over, changes what the model becomes. The full piece is on our blog: https://blog.pebblous.ai/blog/ai-visual-diet-infant-development/en/

So the question I'm left with is a quiet one. Does our pipeline manage only what goes into it, and leave untouched the order in which it's revealed?
