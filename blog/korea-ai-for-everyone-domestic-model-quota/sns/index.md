## LinkedIn (KO)

무료 AI를 5,200만 전 국민에게 나눠 주는 나라가 처음 나왔다.

과학기술정보통신부가 공모한 '모두의 AI' 사업이다. 12월에 이용량 제한 없는 무료 챗봇을 띄우고, 이후 복지와 행정을 대신 찾아 신청까지 해 주는 공공 에이전트로 넓힌다. 여기 조건이 하나 붙었다. 서비스 사업자는 국산 모델을 최소 80% 써야 한다.

이 조건은 산업 육성책의 얼굴을 하고 있지만, 실제로는 데이터 주권의 실행 규정에 가깝다. 전 국민에게 AI를 주는 것은 곧 전 국민 규모의 데이터 수집면을 만드는 일이고, 그 수집면을 어느 나라 모델이 처리할지를 국가가 지정하는 결정이기 때문이다.

문제는 모델의 국적은 정해도 그 모델을 먹일 한국어 데이터는 지정으로 만들어지지 않는다는 데 있다. 한국어는 대표 웹 코퍼스 Common Crawl의 0.823%에 불과하다. 게다가 자격을 먼저 읽어 주는 공공 에이전트는, 국민의 소득·의료·고용·주거 데이터를 먼저 들여다본다.

'무료' 뒤에 남는 질문은 페블러스가 줄곧 물어온 것과 같다. 그 데이터는 누가 만들고, 누가 지키는가.

## LinkedIn (EN)

South Korea is set to become the first country to give free AI to all 52 million of its citizens.

The Ministry of Science and ICT's "AI for Everyone" program launches an unmetered free chatbot in December, then expands into a public agent that finds and files welfare and administrative benefits on a citizen's behalf. It comes with one condition: service providers must run domestic models for at least 80% of the workload.

The rule wears the language of industrial policy, but it reads closer to a data-sovereignty mandate. Giving every citizen AI creates a nationwide data collection surface, and the 80% floor decides which country's models get to process it.

The catch is that a model's nationality can be legislated, but the Korean-language data to train it cannot. Korean makes up just 0.823% of Common Crawl. And an agent that reads your eligibility first must first read your income, health, employment, and housing data.

Behind the word "free" sits the question Pebblous keeps asking: who makes the data, and who guards it.

## Facebook (EN)

"You qualify for youth housing support."

Imagine that message arriving before you ever apply for it.

A few days ago, Korea's science ministry opened its "AI for Everyone" program. The plan is to give free AI to all 52 million citizens. A free chatbot arrives in December, and after that comes a public agent that finds welfare and administrative benefits and files them on your behalf.

It is a welcome idea. If someone claims the support you never knew existed, that is real help for people who have been left on the far side of the information gap.

But sit with the phrase "reads your eligibility first" for a moment, and another texture surfaces. To read my eligibility first, it has to look at my income, my medical bills, my employment, my housing first. As the convenience grows, so does the question of who makes that data and who guards it.

Denmark and the Netherlands have already seen preemptive welfare algorithms wrongly flag certain groups as high-risk. Read against that, the question is not a distant one.

I hope the word "free" never hides the flow of the data underneath it. After December, the real report card for this policy will come not from its convenience, but from how it handles data.

Who makes that data, and who guards it.
