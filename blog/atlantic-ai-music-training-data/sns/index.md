## LinkedIn (KO)

AI가 학습한 음악 2,120만 곡의 출처를 밝힌 건, 그 데이터를 쓴 AI 회사가 아니라 한 언론사였습니다.

디 애틀랜틱은 'AI Watchdog' 프로젝트로 AI 음악 생성 모델 훈련에 흘러다닌 공개 데이터셋을 분석해, 누구나 아티스트 이름이나 곡명으로 검색할 수 있는 데이터베이스로 만들었습니다. 가장 큰 데이터셋 하나에만 유튜브에서 긁어 온 음악 1,230만 곡이 들어 있고, 한 내슈빌 뮤지션은 자기 디스코그래피의 71%가 그 안에 있다는 걸 직접 확인했습니다.

문제는 이 장부를 데이터를 쓴 쪽이 아니라 제3자가 사후에 복원해야 했다는 사실입니다. 학습 데이터의 계보는 수집하는 그 순간에 기록하지 않으면 나중에 되돌릴 수 없습니다.

EU AI Act가 2026년 8월부터 학습 데이터 출처 공개를 의무화하면, 어제까지 회색지대였던 침묵은 내일 위반이 됩니다. 출처를 처음부터 설계에 박아 두는 일은 규제 대응 서류가 아니라 모델을 신뢰받게 만드는 인프라입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The source of the 21 million songs used to train AI music models was disclosed not by the AI companies that used them, but by a magazine.

The Atlantic's AI Watchdog analyzed the public datasets circulating through AI music training and rebuilt them into a database anyone can search by artist or track name. A single dataset, LAION-DISCO-12M, holds 12.3 million songs scraped from YouTube. One Nashville musician found 71% of his discography inside it.

The uncomfortable part is the order of events. The companies that used the data left no record of provenance, so a third party had to reconstruct it after the fact. Training-data lineage cannot be rebuilt retroactively; it is only accurate at the moment of collection.

When the EU AI Act begins enforcing training-data disclosure in August 2026, yesterday's silence becomes tomorrow's violation. Recording provenance by design is not regulatory paperwork. It is the infrastructure that makes a model trustworthy.

## Facebook (EN)

You can now type your own name into a search box and find out whether your music was used to train AI.

A Nashville musician did exactly that a few days ago. The result that came back was 71% of his own discography.

The Atlantic released a tool called AI Watchdog. It takes the roughly 21 million songs that circulated through AI music training and makes them searchable by artist or track name, no account required.

But there is one strange seat in this whole arrangement.

The ledger was not built by the AI companies that trained on the music. The side that actually used the data never said what it had learned from, and a newsroom standing outside filled that emptiness instead.

So who carries the burden of proving that data is clean? The history of training data is only written accurately at the moment of collection. Miss that moment, and all that remains is reconstruction from the outside, by comparison and inference. A rebuilt record is never as clean as the original.

This is close to why we at Pebblous keep looking at a dataset's lineage from the very start. A model that can explain where its data came from may earn trust before a merely capable one does.

That the source of 21 million songs had to be rebuilt by a magazine, rather than by the people who used them, says something quiet about where the AI data ecosystem still stands.
