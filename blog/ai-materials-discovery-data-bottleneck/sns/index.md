나머지 99%는 쓸 만한 데이터를 확보하는 데 들어갔습니다. 네이처 머티리얼스가 EPFL의 베렌트 스밋과 헤리엇와트대의 수사나 가르시아에게 청탁한 코멘트 「The data-only illusion in materials discovery」의 결론입니다.

탄소 포집 후보로 꼽히는 금속-유기 골격체(MOF)는 지금까지 약 9만 종이 보고됐습니다. 그런데 성능이 실제로 측정된 것은 한둘뿐입니다. AI가 후보를 대량으로 그릴수록, 그리는 속도와 만들어 측정하는 속도의 격차는 오히려 벌어집니다.

데이터가 귀한 도메인에서는 많은 데이터가 좋은 발견을 보장하지 못합니다. 관건은 양이 아니라, 그 데이터가 도메인 맥락을 담고 있느냐입니다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

Building the AI took 1% of the effort. The other 99% went to getting usable data.

That is the conclusion of a Nature Materials comment, "The data-only illusion in materials discovery," written by Berend Smit of EPFL and Susana García of Heriot-Watt.

In vision and language, the data already sat on the web. In materials science, making the data is the research. To know whether a new material works, you have to synthesize it and measure it in a lab.

Take metal-organic frameworks, a leading candidate for carbon capture. Roughly 90,000 have been reported, yet only one or two have measured performance. The more candidates AI draws, the wider the gap grows between drawing them and actually making and measuring them.

In data-scarce domains, more data does not guarantee better discovery. The question is not quantity but whether the data carries its domain context.

## Facebook (EN)

"We drew ninety thousand, and only one or two had ever been made and measured."

I had to read that line twice.

It comes from the world of new-materials research. Metal-organic frameworks, or MOFs, are porous crystals built from metal ions and organic molecules, and they are a leading candidate for trapping carbon. On a computer you can imagine endless variations, and so far about 90,000 have been drawn.

Yet only one or two have actually been synthesized and measured.

A comment in Nature Materials put the gap plainly. In their own project, building the AI took just 1% of the effort, while 99% went to securing data worth using. Vision and language already had oceans of data on the web, but the data for a new material only exists once someone makes it, one experiment at a time, in a lab.

And so a question lingers. When we say we do not have enough data, do we mean the quantity is too small? Or that the context the data should carry is still missing?

Gathering more and holding the right context may have been two different tasks all along.
