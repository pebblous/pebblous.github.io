## LinkedIn (EN)

For the first time, a US federal bill decides who it regulates not by what they build but by how much they earn.

The Great American AI Act, a bipartisan discussion draft released in the House in June, applies only to companies that clear $500 million in annual revenue and train a frontier model. That captures a small set: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, xAI, and it puts disclosure, third-party audits, and incident reporting on them.

Facing the same question, the EU thresholds on training compute, Korea on the domain of use, and China on reach. Revenue, an accounting figure, has never before been the primary line for AI regulation. It is cheap to verify because tax filings already confirm it, but it misses the young lab training a powerful model before it earns a dollar.

Deciding who to regulate comes down to which column you put a threshold on. You have to define what to measure before you can measure it — in regulation, and in valuing training data.

미국 연방의 자리였습니다.

눈길이 오래 머문 건 특정 조항이 아니라, 이 법이 규제할 대상과 규제하지 않을 대상 사이에 선을 긋는 방식이었습니다. 미국은 그 선을 '무엇을 만드느냐'가 아니라 '얼마를 버느냐'에 그었습니다. 연 매출 5억 달러를 넘으면서 프런티어 모델을 훈련하는 회사만 대상이 됩니다.

## Facebook (EN)

Read through the AI rules of China, the EU, and Korea one after another, and a single cell stays oddly blank.

The federal United States.

In June, bipartisan members of the US House put out a 269-page draft to fill it. The Great American AI Act. Not law yet, just a draft opened up for feedback.

What held my attention wasn't any single clause. It was where the bill draws the line between who it regulates and who it leaves alone. The US drew that line not on what a company builds, but on what it earns: $500 million a year in revenue, plus training a frontier model.

If you've worked with data, the move is familiar. To filter a set, you pick one observable column and set a threshold. The EU picked training compute. Korea picked the domain of use. China picked how far a system has spread. Same question, different columns, different beliefs about what counts as a signal.

So the question that lingers is this one: "What single number are we using to approximate something as continuous as AI?" Defining who gets regulated, and measuring how far AI has spread as the same draft asks the Census Bureau to do, may be two sides of one problem: you have to decide what to measure before you can measure it. It is the same spot Pebblous keeps arriving at when it tries to measure the value and lineage of training data.
