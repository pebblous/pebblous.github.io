일리노이 주지사 프리츠커가 지난 7월 6일 SB 315에 서명했다. 연매출 5억 달러를 넘고 10²⁶ 연산 이상으로 학습한 프런티어 모델을 만드는 회사가 대상이다. 이들은 매년 이해관계 없는 제3자로부터 안전 감사를 받고, 요약본을 30일 안에 공개해야 한다. 캘리포니아 SB 53과 뉴욕 RAISE Act도 안전 프레임워크 공개를 요구했지만, 회사가 스스로 문서를 내는 데서 의무가 끝났다. 바깥에서 확인하는 절차를 법에 새긴 것은 일리노이가 처음이다.

정작 조문에 다 적히지 않은 것은 감사관이 실제로 무엇을 들여다보느냐다. 법은 "학습데이터"라는 단어를 쓰지 않는다. 그러나 감사가 서류 확인을 넘어 실질을 담보하려면, 감사관은 결국 어떤 데이터로 무엇을 평가했고 그 로그가 남아 있는지를 확인해야 한다. GovAI 연구진도 자기보고 문서만으로는 낮은 수준의 확신밖에 얻지 못한다고 지적한다.

한계도 분명하다. 감사기관이 큰 고객을 잃지 않으려는 이해충돌, 국가 표준 없는 주관적 판단에 대한 업계의 반발이 남아 있다. 그럼에도 방향은 한곳으로 모인다. 시행은 2028년 1월. 데이터가 어디서 왔고 어떤 평가를 통과했는지를 지금부터 추적 가능한 형태로 남기는 일이, 규제 준수의 실질적 통화가 되고 있다.

## LinkedIn (EN)

The first US law forcing large AI developers to have their safety operations checked by an outside auditor every year has been signed.

Illinois Governor Pritzker signed SB 315 on July 6. It applies to companies building frontier models trained on more than 10²⁶ operations with over $500M in annual revenue. Each year they must submit to a safety audit by an independent third party with no financial stake, and publish a summary within 30 days. California's SB 53 and New York's RAISE Act already required disclosure of safety frameworks — but the obligation ended once a company published its own document. Illinois is the first to write outside verification into the statute.

What the text does not spell out is what the auditor actually inspects. The law never uses the words "training data." Yet for an audit to mean more than a paperwork check, the auditor eventually has to see which data was used to evaluate what, and whether those logs still exist. GovAI researchers make the same point: self-reported documents yield only a low level of assurance.

The limits are real — auditors face a conflict of interest in not wanting to lose large clients, and industry groups object to subjective judgment without a national standard. Still, the direction is converging. Enforcement begins January 2028. Keeping a traceable record of where data came from and which evaluations it passed is becoming the practical currency of compliance.

"Get audited. Every year."

The moment the law handed large AI developers that one sentence, I found myself wondering what the auditor's day actually looks like.

This month Illinois signed SB 315, requiring large frontier AI developers to undergo an annual safety audit by an independent third party. No US state had done this before. California and New York stopped at asking companies to publish their own safety frameworks; Illinois added one more layer on top — outside verification.

And yet the statute never spells out what the auditor is supposed to inspect. The law does not use the words "training data."

Picture the auditor at a desk, turning the pages a company submitted. In front of a sentence that reads "catastrophic risk was properly mitigated," wouldn't the next question be — "With which data, and which records, did you confirm that?"

Verifiable evidence. That is what everything narrows down to the moment an audit tries to mean something. Does a trace remain of where the data came from and which evaluations it passed? It is not far from why Pebblous has spent its time quantitatively diagnosing the quality of AI training data through DataClinic.

Enforcement begins January 2028. It looks like there is still time, but evidence cannot be assembled in a hurry after the fact. The records being kept now become the answer then.
