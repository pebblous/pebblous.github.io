## LinkedIn (EN)

A city of 65,000 just banned data centers for good: not by a council vote, but at the ballot box.

In Monterey Park, east of Los Angeles, 86% of voters approved Measure NDC in June, permanently barring any new data center inside city limits. The trigger was a 250,000-square-foot facility planned for a vacant mall site. The developer argued it would draw less than 1% of regional power demand and manage noise and water with sound walls and closed-loop cooling. Nine in ten residents voted no anyway.

The side that won on the numbers lost at the polls, because the fight was never about numbers. It was about trust. To residents, "under 1%" was an unverifiable promise; the noise and diesel exhaust were realities they had already seen in other towns.

The benefits of AI compute scatter thinly across the cloud, but its electricity, water, and noise are billed thickly to whatever neighborhood hosts the hardware. Monterey Park is the first vote to refuse that bill. Pebblous has covered data center regulation from the top down, through federal moratoriums and compute overinvestment; this case adds the view from the ground, where local consent is becoming as decisive as power procurement in whether AI infrastructure gets built at all.

Sixty-five thousand people. That's the size of Monterey Park, a city just east of Los Angeles. In June, nine out of ten of its residents voted the same way: 86% approved Measure NDC, which bars any new data center from ever being built inside city limits.

It started with a 250,000-square-foot facility planned for an old shopping mall.

The developer said it would use less than 1% of the region's power, that it would raise sound walls and recycle its cooling water. On paper, it was a safe plan.

People voted against it anyway. That's the part I keep returning to. Why did "technically safe" never become "trusted"?

Perhaps because "under 1%" was a promise residents had no way to verify, while the noise and the diesel exhaust were things they had already seen in other towns. AI's intelligence feels like it lives somewhere far away, in the cloud. But the electricity, the water, and the noise it costs pile up outside someone's window.

"The invisible bill for scaling AI: who ends up receiving it?"

Where the physical cost of data is charged, and to whom, is a question Pebblous has sat with for a long time. The 86% in Monterey Park were the first to send that bill back. And in Wisconsin, in Nevada, in Maine, the same question is drifting toward the next neighborhood.
