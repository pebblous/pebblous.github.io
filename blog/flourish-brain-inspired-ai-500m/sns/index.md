## LinkedIn (EN)

A two-year-old startup with no revenue and no working product just raised $500 million. Jeff Bezos doubled his initial commitment to roughly $100 million, and the company was valued at $2.5 billion.

What Flourish sold was not a product but a single question: a human brain learns for a lifetime on 20 watts, so why does AI need thousands of GPUs that each draw more than 700? The company plans to copy cortical circuitry directly under an electron microscope and build a Cortex AI that runs on 3 to 7 percent of a GPU's power.

The catch is that neuroscience has spent decades unable to confirm whether the brain even has a single 'core algorithm.' Flourish is selling a hypothesis, not a product.

Still, the question it targets is the right one. With training data and electricity hitting their limits at the same time, if intelligence turns out to hinge on structure rather than scale, the axis of competition shifts from how much data you have to how good it is.

## Facebook (EN)

An incandescent bulb pulls about 60 watts.

A human brain runs on a third of that. Twenty watts, and on it we learn to walk, to speak a first language, to recognize thousands of faces across a lifetime.

A piece of news slid past my feed last week and made me sit with that number. A two-year-old startup called Flourish had raised $500 million with no revenue and no working product. Jeff Bezos, they say, doubled his original commitment to get in.

Their question was a plain one. If a brain does all of this on 20 watts, why does AI need thousands of GPUs, each drawing more than 700, just to approximate it?

Flourish's answer is not a faster chip. It is to look at the brain's actual wiring under an electron microscope and rewrite the computation hidden inside it as software. Whether the brain really holds a single 'core algorithm' is something neuroscience still cannot say. So what the company is selling is closer to a hypothesis than a product.

But the timing of that hypothesis is strange. The two fuels that have grown AI, data and electricity, are running low at the same moment.

When 'more' is blocked on both sides, what remains is 'better.' Drawing more signal from less data. That is the ground Pebblous has spent a long time looking at.

No one knows whether the $500 million lands or misses. What is clear is the question it points to. Is intelligence a matter of scale, or of structure?
